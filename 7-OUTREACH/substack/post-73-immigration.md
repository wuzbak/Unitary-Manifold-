# Immigration: What's Broken, Who Pays, and Whether It Can Be Fixed

*Post 73 of the Unitary Manifold series.*
*No physics claim is made in this post. It is a thorough, honest account of the
American immigration system: what has failed structurally, whose existence has been
diminished and by how much, and what evidence-based repair looks like. The framework's
language — information encoding, fixed-point stability, entropy accumulation, the HILS
governance principle — provides a useful structural lens but does not replace the hard
political and institutional work that is actually required.*

---

Immigration is not an abstract policy debate. It is the question of which human beings
are permitted to belong — and on what terms, under what conditions, with what assurances —
to the place where they already live, work, and raise their children.

The United States has approximately 11 million undocumented residents, a 3-million-case
backlog in immigration courts, employment visa queues that will take a skilled Indian
software engineer more than a century to clear at current rates, and a border management
apparatus that has been in perpetual crisis for thirty years. It is one of the most
prosperous nations in human history, built almost entirely by people who came from
somewhere else, and it cannot tell those people — the ones arriving now — what will
happen to them, on what timeline, under what conditions.

That is a system failure. This post attempts to describe it precisely: what broke,
who is paying the cost, and whether the damage can be repaired.

---

## What is actually broken

### 1. The legal immigration pathway is effectively closed for most people

The United States offers legal permanent residence through four main channels: family
sponsorship, employment, the diversity lottery, and humanitarian protection (asylum
and refugee programs). Each of these channels is broken in distinct ways.

**Family-based immigration** has per-country caps set in 1990 that have never been
adjusted for population growth. The result: a Filipino citizen sponsored by a US
citizen sibling is currently waiting approximately 24 years for a visa. A Mexican
citizen in the same category: over 20 years. These are not outliers. They are the
standard processing time for legal applications filed by real people following the
law exactly as written.

**Employment-based immigration** applies a per-country cap of 7% on all employment
green cards regardless of the sending country's population. In practice, this means
citizens of India and China — who constitute the majority of skilled worker applicants
because they are the majority of skilled workers in the fields where the US has
chronic shortages — face backlogs that are not decades but lifetimes. The USCIS's own
projections have shown that an Indian national entering the EB-2 or EB-3 queue today
would wait, at current clearing rates, over 100 years. This is not a policy that delays
people. It is a policy that functionally excludes them.

**The diversity lottery** allocates 50,000 green cards per year to countries with low
historical immigration rates. It is the only pathway that is remotely proportionate
to actual demand, and it is blocked from citizens of high-immigration countries (Mexico,
China, India, the Philippines) — meaning it serves as a path primarily for people from
countries with lower economic and political pressures to migrate.

**Humanitarian protection** — asylum — is addressed separately below, because it has
become a system in complete collapse.

The result of these combined failures: the United States does not have an immigration
system that people can use. It has a theoretical immigration system that most of the
people who want to come here legally cannot actually access on any realistic timeline.
The "come here legally" instruction that dominates political discourse is, for most
people, an instruction to wait in a queue that will outlast their working lives.

### 2. The asylum system has collapsed under structural neglect

Asylum is not discretionary admission. Under the 1951 Refugee Convention and the 1980
Refugee Act that implemented it in US law, a person who reaches the United States and
presents a credible fear of persecution on the basis of race, religion, nationality,
political opinion, or membership in a particular social group has a legal right to
have that claim heard. The United States committed to this.

The immigration court system that adjudicates asylum claims is not a real court.
Immigration judges work within the Department of Justice, under the executive branch —
they are not Article III judges, they do not have the independence of the federal
judiciary, and they can be fired or have their case-completion quotas adjusted by
whoever leads the department. The court system has an active backlog of over 3 million
pending cases. Average wait times exceed four years in most jurisdictions. In that
time, applicants are typically prohibited from working legally, cannot travel, and
live in a sustained state of uncertainty that is, by any reasonable standard, psychologically
devastating.

The backlog is not primarily because too many people are filing claims. It is because
the system has never been adequately resourced to process the claims that arrive.
Adding immigration judges reduces backlogs; removing them or freezing hiring creates them.
This is a solvable problem that has not been solved because the political incentive
has been to perform crisis management rather than build institutional capacity.

The people waiting in this system are not abstractions. They are parents who fled
gang violence in Honduras and do not know if their children will grow up in the
United States or be deported to a country the children have never lived in. They are
women who fled domestic violence in countries where the state will not protect them.
They are LGBTQ+ people from countries where their existence is criminalized. The law
says they are entitled to a hearing. The system delivers the hearing, on average,
four years later, with procedural requirements that are nearly impossible to navigate
without a lawyer in a system where there is no right to appointed counsel.

### 3. Eleven million people are encoded in American society and the law treats them as not there

The undocumented population in the United States has been approximately 10-12 million
for over two decades. These are people who are here — who have been here for years and
decades — who pay taxes (undocumented immigrants pay an estimated $11.7 billion in
state and local taxes annually), who hold jobs, who own homes in some cases, who have
children who are United States citizens, who coach Little League and run restaurants
and harvest crops and build buildings.

The information encoding point from the framework is not metaphorical here. These
people are literally encoded in the fabric of American society: their children are in
the public schools, their labor is in the goods and services the economy produces,
their relationships are in the communities they live in. They are here. The legal
fiction that they are not here — that enforcement could return 11 million people to
countries many of them have not lived in for decades, at a cost the American Action
Forum has estimated at $400-600 billion — is not a policy. It is a denial of a reality
that has already happened.

The undocumented status itself is the source of enormous harm. It means:

- **No labor protections in practice.** Wage theft, dangerous working conditions,
  sexual harassment, and other labor violations are dramatically underreported by
  undocumented workers because reporting requires contact with authorities who may
  report them for deportation. Employers know this. Some employers exploit it
  systematically. Undocumented status creates a captive workforce that cannot exercise
  the legal rights it nominally has.

- **No access to most federal benefits.** Undocumented immigrants are ineligible for
  Social Security (despite paying into it via false or borrowed Social Security numbers),
  Medicare, Medicaid (except emergency care), SNAP, and most other federal programs.
  The contribution to these systems without access to their benefits is substantial and
  undiscussed in most policy debates.

- **Permanent fear.** The psychological literature on the effects of deportation fear
  on immigrant families — including on US-citizen children in mixed-status households —
  is consistent and damaging. Elevated cortisol, disrupted attachment, school
  performance impacts, anxiety disorders. The children of undocumented immigrants are
  growing up under a condition of chronic low-grade threat, and that condition has
  measurable consequences for their development.

- **Exploitability in all domains.** Housing (landlords who threaten to report tenants
  to ICE), commerce, transportation, healthcare-seeking. The undocumented status is
  a structural vulnerability that bad actors exploit because they can.

### 4. Border enforcement has been substituted for immigration policy

The southern border of the United States is not the cause of the immigration crisis.
It is the location where the gap between migration pressure and legal pathway capacity
becomes visible.

People cross the border without authorization primarily because they cannot cross
with authorization. A person fleeing violence in El Salvador who presents at a port
of entry and requests asylum is legally permitted to do so; since 2019, various
administrations have used a series of tools — the Migrant Protection Protocols ("Remain
in Mexico"), Title 42, the asylum ban executive actions — to prevent this from
happening. The result is that the legal pathway is closed and the illegal pathway
is open, and so people use the illegal pathway. This is a predictable response to
the incentive structure. It is not a mystery.

The border enforcement apparatus has grown enormously: CBP and ICE together have
budgets exceeding $25 billion annually, more than the FBI and the ATF combined.
This spending has not stopped unauthorized immigration. It has made the experience
of attempting unauthorized immigration more dangerous — more money to smugglers,
more deaths in the desert — but it has not substantially reduced the volume, because
the underlying migration pressure (violence, economic deprivation, climate displacement)
has not been addressed.

This is not to say that border management doesn't matter. It does. Orderly, humane,
efficient processing at ports of entry serves both the immigrants' interest (in a
fair hearing) and the country's interest (in knowing who is here). The argument is
that border enforcement, scaled up, is not the same as immigration management,
and that they have been confused in public discourse in ways that are expensive,
deadly, and counterproductive.

### 5. The political system has been incapable of reform for forty years

The last comprehensive immigration reform in the United States was the Immigration
Reform and Control Act of 1986, which legalized approximately 3 million undocumented
residents, created employer sanctions for hiring unauthorized workers, and promised
enhanced border enforcement. The legalization happened. The employer sanctions were
never seriously enforced. The enhanced border enforcement was deployed but did not
address root causes.

Since 1986:
- The Immigration Act of 1990 restructured visa categories and created the diversity
  lottery but did not address undocumented populations or asylum.
- The Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996 dramatically
  expanded deportation authority and created bars to reentry that trap people in
  permanent legal limbo.
- The DREAM Act has been introduced in Congress repeatedly since 2001 and has never
  passed, despite polling showing consistent majority support for protecting people
  who were brought here as children.
- The comprehensive immigration reform bills of 2006, 2007, and 2013 each passed one
  chamber or had significant bipartisan support and each failed — the 2013 bill passed
  the Senate 68-32 and was never brought to a House floor vote.

The failure is not primarily about lack of solutions. The outlines of a comprehensive
reform have been clear for decades: a pathway to legal status for long-term residents,
modernized visa categories matched to labor market needs, a properly resourced asylum
system, and border management that is humane and efficient. The failure is about
political economy: immigration has become a mobilizing issue for base voters on both
sides, which means elected officials have more incentive to perform outrage about it
than to solve it.

## Who pays the cost

The human cost of this failure is not distributed equally.

### For immigrants and asylum seekers

The primary cost is borne by the people inside the broken system. This includes:

**Years and decades in legal limbo.** An H-1B worker from India on an employment
visa who applies for a green card in 2025 may be on their fourth or fifth visa renewal
by the time their application clears — if they are still alive. Their children, brought
here as dependents, age out of the dependent visa at 21 and become undocumented through
no action of their own. This is a category of people — "documented Dreamers" — whose
existence the system creates and then ignores.

**Detention.** The United States detention system currently holds approximately 30,000
immigration detainees on any given day, at a cost of roughly $140 per person per day.
The conditions in these facilities have been documented by the DHS Inspector General
to include inadequate medical care, overcrowding, and in some cases, preventable deaths.
Many detainees have no criminal history. Many are asylum seekers whose only offense is
presenting at a border in the way the law technically permits.

**Deportation of people who are American in every meaningful sense.** The United States
has deported people who came here as toddlers, who do not speak the language of their
birth country, who have no family there, whose entire social world is here. The legal
mechanism that permits this — the 1996 removal bars — treats criminal convictions
(including minor drug offenses that would not result in deportation for a citizen) as
automatic grounds for permanent removal. The human consequences are not theoretical.
They are documented: people removed to countries they do not remember, unable to
communicate with their children, excluded from the country that formed them.

### For mixed-status families

Approximately 5 million US-citizen children have at least one undocumented parent.
These children are American. They go to American schools, they will pay American taxes,
they will serve in the American military. The conditions in which they grow up — chronic
fear, family instability, economic marginalization of their parents — are conditions
the United States is imposing on its own citizens by failing to resolve their parents'
legal status.

The research on this population is clear: children in mixed-status families exhibit
elevated rates of anxiety, depression, post-traumatic stress symptoms, and educational
disruption compared to peers in similar economic circumstances without immigration
status concerns. The country is investing in the diminishment of these children's
potential for reasons that have nothing to do with the children.

### For receiving communities

The political discourse around immigration frequently invokes the concerns of people
in receiving communities — places with large immigrant populations, border towns,
cities that have declared themselves sanctuaries or that have faced sudden influxes of
migrants. These concerns are real and they deserve honest engagement, not dismissal.

The actual evidence on the economic impacts of immigration is substantially positive
at the aggregate level: immigrants are net contributors to Social Security, they start
businesses at higher rates than native-born Americans, they fill labor shortages in
both high-skill and low-skill sectors. The National Academy of Sciences 2016 comprehensive
review concluded that immigration has an overall positive fiscal and economic impact.

But aggregate economics conceals distributional effects. Workers without college degrees
who compete in labor markets with large undocumented populations can face wage
depression — the exploitation of undocumented workers suppresses wages for everyone
in those markets. Local jurisdictions that receive large numbers of migrants bear costs
(schooling, emergency medical care, law enforcement) that are often not reimbursed by
the federal government that controls immigration policy. These are real costs imposed
on real communities.

A functional immigration system would manage these distributional effects — through
enforcement of labor standards (which would reduce the wage-depression dynamic),
through federal reimbursement of local services, through orderly processing that
distributes new arrivals to communities with labor shortages rather than concentrating
them in already-strained jurisdictions. The current system does none of this.

### For the United States as a project

The deepest cost is the one that is hardest to quantify: what the treatment of
immigrants costs the United States in terms of who it is.

The United States was built — literally built, its infrastructure, its agriculture,
its cities — on immigrant labor, often immigrant labor obtained under conditions of
coercion or exploitation (the history includes Chinese laborers building the railroads
under conditions they could not refuse, bracero workers without labor protections,
waves of European immigrants crowded into tenements). The stated American narrative
is one of aspiration and belonging: the tired, the poor, the huddled masses. The
operational American reality has frequently been one of exclusion, exploitation, and
racialized gatekeeping.

The current system continues this pattern. It says: we want your labor, we want the
taxes you pay, we want the businesses you start, we want your children to serve in
our military — but we do not want to acknowledge your presence, extend you rights,
or make your status secure. We will hold the insecurity of your presence over you
as a mechanism of control.

That is a moral failure independent of any policy argument. A country that treats
people this way is not the country it claims to be.

## Can it be fixed, and how

Yes. The problems are difficult but they are not mysterious. The solutions have been
known for decades. What has been lacking is the political will to enact them and the
institutional capacity to implement them. Both of those failures can be addressed.

### 1. Legalization for long-term residents

The 11 million undocumented people who are already here need a path to legal status.
Not because they "deserve" it as a reward, but because their presence is real, their
encoding in American society is permanent, and the alternative — sustained mass
deportation or permanent legal limbo — imposes enormous costs on them and on the
country.

The specific conditions can be debated democratically: length of residence (most
proposals require 5 or 10 years), criminal background (most proposals exclude people
with felony convictions or certain misdemeanors), continuous presence, tax compliance.
These are legitimate calibration questions. What is not a legitimate position is that
the status quo — 11 million people in permanent insecurity — is acceptable.

The DACA population — approximately 600,000 people who were brought here as children,
who have passed background checks, who are enrolled in school or working — should be
the first and easiest case. That it has not been resolved legislatively in twenty-five
years is an institutional failure, not a policy disagreement.

### 2. Immigration court reform

The immigration court system needs to be removed from executive branch control and
constituted as an independent Article I court — a specialized judicial body with
full judicial independence, proper funding, and the ability to develop consistent
case law. This is the structure of the Tax Court and the Court of International Trade.
It is not a novel institutional form.

The backlog of 3 million cases requires emergency resourcing: hundreds of additional
judges, support staff, public defenders (or government-funded representation for
those who cannot afford counsel). The cost of providing counsel to asylum seekers
is offset by faster, more accurate adjudication — represented applicants are more
likely to appear for hearings, more likely to have their cases resolved in a timely
manner, and more likely to receive accurate decisions.

The current system denies due process not through explicit policy but through resource
starvation. Fix the resources and you fix the process.

### 3. Visa modernization

The visa allocation system was last comprehensively updated in 1990. The labor market,
the global migration pattern, and the US economy have changed substantially. The
reforms needed are technical but tractable:

- Eliminate or dramatically increase per-country caps on employment visas. The 7%
  cap made no sense in 1990 and makes less sense now. It does not serve any legitimate
  policy purpose.
- Create responsive labor market pathways that adjust visa allocations to actual
  workforce demand, with labor market protections to prevent displacement of US workers
  (which means wage floors, not exclusion).
- Modernize the family-based system to reduce backlogs by increasing the visa cap
  or by prioritizing more recent applications while clearing the queue.
- Create a pathway for agricultural workers and other sectors that depend on seasonal
  or temporary labor — the H-2A program exists but is expensive and administratively
  burdensome in ways that push employers toward informal hiring.

### 4. Asylum system adequacy

An asylum system that takes four years to process a case is not an asylum system. It
is a waiting list. The goal should be processing within 12 months, with a decision
within 6 months of application in most cases.

This requires:
- Funding immigration courts adequately (see above).
- Establishing asylum offices at ports of entry and border regions with surge capacity.
- Creating expedited processing for straightforward cases (people with official
  documentation of persecution, LGBTQ+ applicants from countries with documented
  criminalization) while maintaining full hearings for complex cases.
- Ending the practice of using asylum processing restrictions as deterrence. If the
  legal pathway is actually accessible, the alternative pathway is less attractive.

### 5. Root cause engagement

The United States cannot process its way out of the migration pressure from Central
America, the Caribbean, and parts of South America. The violence in Honduras, El
Salvador, and Guatemala; the economic collapse of Venezuela; the climate-driven
agricultural failure in parts of Mexico and Central America — these are the engines
of migration. Border enforcement does not address them.

Investment in stability and economic development in the sending regions is not charity.
It is self-interest: a more stable, more prosperous Central America produces less
migration pressure. The evidence from targeted development programs (the Alliance for
Progress, more recently the Biden-era root causes strategy) is that they can reduce
migration from specific communities. The scale of investment required is significant
but it is an order of magnitude less than the enforcement spending.

Regional agreements on burden-sharing — with Mexico, with Canada, with the wider
hemisphere — are necessary for a sustainable asylum system. No single country can
process all claims from a hemisphere under migration pressure. The United States has
the capacity to lead in constructing these frameworks. It has largely declined to do so.

### 6. Labor market enforcement

If employers cannot hire undocumented workers without significant legal risk, the
pull factor for unauthorized immigration is reduced. The current system — employer
sanctions that exist in statute but are rarely enforced — creates the worst of all
worlds: an underground labor market that exploits workers and suppresses wages for
everyone.

Universal E-Verify, combined with real enforcement against employers who violate it,
would reduce unauthorized employment. This is opposed by industries that depend on
unauthorized labor — agriculture, construction, hospitality, meat processing —
because those industries have built their business models around the exploitation of
workers who cannot report violations. The enforcement gap is not accidental. It
reflects the preferences of powerful economic interests.

Fixing the labor market requires enforcing labor standards for everyone, documented
or not — making it illegal to exploit undocumented status as a way to evade wage and
safety laws. This removes the competitive advantage of hiring undocumented workers
and reduces the incentive for employers to prefer unauthorized labor.

## The structural lens: what the framework adds

The Unitary Pentad framework asks a simple question about governance: are the people
affected by the decisions involved in making them? Immigration policy in the United
States is made almost entirely without the participation of the people most affected
by it. Undocumented residents cannot vote. Asylum seekers cannot vote. Even legal
permanent residents — people who have been here for decades, who pay taxes, who have
US-citizen children — cannot vote on the federal laws that govern their status.

This is the HILS failure at the heart of the system: the people with the most
information about the actual conditions of the immigration experience have no
institutional voice in designing the system they must navigate. Policy is made by
citizens about non-citizens, often by people who have never met an undocumented
worker or an asylum seeker, based on information filtered through political media
that has strong incentives to emphasize fear and crisis.

A system designed with the participation of the people it governs would look different.
It would have feedback mechanisms — ways for the system to learn from the experience
of the people inside it and adjust. It would treat those people as sources of
information about what the system is actually doing, not as subjects to be managed.

The information encoding point also applies directly: the people who are here are
here. Their lives, their relationships, their children, their labor are encoded in
this country. Policy that refuses to acknowledge this encoding is not operating on
reality. It is maintaining a fiction that is expensive to sustain and harmful to
everyone inside it — including the citizens whose government is doing this in their name.

## What this means for the country

The United States has always been in tension with itself on immigration. The aspiration
— the "nation of immigrants" narrative, the Statue of Liberty, the Ellis Island story —
and the reality — the Chinese Exclusion Act, the Japanese American internment, the
bracero exploitation, the current system — have never fully aligned.

The question is not whether to have immigration policy. Every country has immigration
policy. The question is whether to have immigration policy that acknowledges the
humanity of the people it governs and that actually functions.

The current system does neither. It maintains a fiction of control while exercising
no real control over who is here. It claims to offer legal pathways that are not
actually accessible. It exposes vulnerable people to exploitation and fear as a
structural feature of the system, not a bug. It makes children pay for the legal
status their parents could not obtain.

The costs of this are paid primarily by people who have no political voice to
demand otherwise. That is why it persists.

Whether it changes depends on whether the people who do have that political voice —
citizens — decide that this is something they will not accept. That is a decision
about what kind of country this is and what it owes to the people inside it.

The geometry does not answer that question. Only the people do.
