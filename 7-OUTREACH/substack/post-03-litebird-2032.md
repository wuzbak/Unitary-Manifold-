## What is cosmic birefringence?

Light is a wave. Its electric field oscillates perpendicular to the direction the
light travels, and the direction of that oscillation is called the light's polarization.
Ordinary sunlight has polarization randomly distributed in all directions. Light
reflected off water or glass tends to be polarized in a preferred direction — which is
why polarized sunglasses cut glare.

The cosmic microwave background (CMB) — the oldest light in the universe — is also
polarized. Its polarization pattern was set by conditions in the early universe and has
been travelling toward us for 13.8 billion years.

Cosmic birefringence is what happens if space itself has a slight optical activity:
if some background field in the universe causes the polarization direction to rotate
slowly as light travels through it. This is exactly what certain theoretical
frameworks — including this one — predict. After 13.8 billion light-years, even a
tiny rotation-per-unit-length adds up to a measurable angle.

In 2020, Yuto Minami and Eiichiro Komatsu re-analysed the Planck satellite's CMB
polarization data and found a hint of exactly this effect: a rotation of approximately
0.35° ± 0.14°. A 2022 independent analysis by Diego-Palazuelos and colleagues found
a consistent result. The hint is real enough to take seriously. The error bar is wide
enough that it cannot yet be called a detection.

The standard cosmological model has no explanation for this rotation. If it is real,
something new is causing it.

## What LiteBIRD is and when it measures

LiteBIRD (Lite (Light) satellite for the studies of B-mode polarization and
Inflation from cosmic background Radiation Detection) is a joint mission of the Japan
Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) and international partners including NASA and ESA.
It is designed specifically to measure CMB polarization with precision roughly seven
times better than current constraints.

Its target: measure the birefringence angle β to a precision of σ(β) ≈ 0.02°.
(The CMB-S4 ground-based experiment, currently under construction, will eventually
reach σ(β) ≈ 0.01°.)

The current constraint is ±0.14°. LiteBIRD will shrink this to ±0.02°. That is
enough to distinguish the predicted values from zero, from each other, and from any
value outside the admissible range.

Launch is targeted for approximately 2028. Preliminary results are expected within
a few years of launch — call it 2032 as the reference date for when the decisive
measurement becomes public.

## What confirmation would mean (carefully stated)

If LiteBIRD measures β in the predicted window — say, β = 0.310° ± 0.018° — that
would be consistent with the framework. It would not prove the framework is correct.

There are two reasons for this careful language.

First, other theories might also predict birefringence in roughly this range. Axion-like
particle models, for example, can produce birefringence from various parameter
combinations. A confirmed detection would narrow the field but not uniquely select
this framework.

Second, confirmation of the birefringence value does not resolve the remaining open gap
discussed in Post 0: the full CMB acoustic peak position computation (Boltzmann
integration of ℓ-values). The APS argument for n_w = 5 has now been substantially
proved (Pillars 70-B, 80, 89); that gap has been substantially closed analytically.
A complete confirmation would still require the full Boltzmann integration of peak
positions.

What a positive LiteBIRD measurement would do is make this framework a serious
candidate deserving full formal review, and provide the strongest available external
confirmation that the integer k_CS = 74 is not a coincidence.
