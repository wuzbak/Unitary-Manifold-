You cannot un-break an egg. You remember yesterday but not tomorrow. Stars die but
never undie. Your coffee cools and never spontaneously reheats.

These are not separate phenomena. They are all consequences of the same thing: the
arrow of time. And for the last 150 years, the best scientific answer to "why does
time run forward?" has been: *probability*. The universe started in an extraordinarily
unlikely low-entropy state. Disorder increases because there are astronomically more
ways for a system to be disordered than ordered. Given enough time, nearly anything
that can happen, will — and nearly everything that can happen involves more disorder,
not less.

This explanation is mathematically consistent. It is experimentally confirmed. And it
is, in a very specific sense, unsatisfying — because it replaces one mystery (why
does time flow forward?) with a harder one: why did the universe start in such a
special, improbably ordered state?

The Unitary Manifold takes a different position: the statistical answer is correct as
an *effective* description but wrong as a *fundamental* one. The real reason time runs
forward is not probability. It is geometry.

## The shadow and the staircase

Consider a spiral staircase. If you photograph it from directly above, you see a
circle. The circle obeys its own laws — it has circumference, area, rotational
symmetry. A mathematician who only ever sees the photograph can build a perfectly
consistent theory of circles.

But the circle is a shadow. The staircase has a vertical direction that the photograph
cannot capture. And certain things that look mysterious in circle-land — why do some
circles seem to "prefer" going clockwise? why does travelling around the circle always
end up at a different height? — become obvious the moment you know about the staircase.

The Unitary Manifold proposes that our experience of time is the circle. The full
structure is the staircase.

The argument is precise. Standard physics describes the universe in four
dimensions — three of space and one of time. The proposal here is to add one more:
a compact fifth dimension, rolled up too small to observe directly, in the manner
developed by Theodor Kaluza and Oskar Klein over a century ago (more on that in a
later post).

When you write out the most general five-dimensional geometry and extract its
consequences for the four-dimensional world we inhabit, something new appears. There
is a geometric field — call it the irreversibility field — encoded in the off-diagonal
block of the five-dimensional metric tensor. When the fifth dimension is mathematically
integrated out, this field survives. It shows up as a new term in the field equations
that govern how spacetime evolves.

The result: the familiar equations of gravity pick up new contributions that encode
the direction of information flow and the growth of entropy. The Second Law of
Thermodynamics — previously a statistical postulate added *on top of* the fundamental
equations — is now *inside* the equations. It is as fundamental as the curvature that
causes gravity.

---

## What "inside the equations" means

The distinction matters. In the standard picture, you write down the laws of physics
(which are time-symmetric — they work the same whether you run time forward or
backward) and then add a boundary condition: "the universe started in a low-entropy
state." The Second Law is an additional assumption, imposed from outside.

In this framework, there is no boundary condition to impose. The geometry of the
five-dimensional spacetime selects the direction of time automatically. The irreversibility
field has a sign — it points from past to future — and that sign is not a choice. It
is a consequence of the geometry, in the same way that the fact that a ball rolls
downhill is a consequence of the curvature of the surface, not a separate rule you
have to specify.

This is the sense in which the arrow of time is a *shadow*: it is the four-dimensional
projection of a five-dimensional geometric structure that makes it inevitable. Someone
living in the four-dimensional world observes irreversibility as a law. A physicist
who knows about the fifth dimension sees why the law had to be that way.

## What you experience as a 4D being

If this picture is correct, then what you experience as:

- the flow of time (past → future),
- the growth of entropy (order → disorder),
- the irreversibility of events (eggs break; they do not unbreak),
- the conservation of information (nothing is truly erased from the universe),

...are all four-dimensional projections of a five-dimensional geometric structure.
The structure determines their character at the level of the field equations themselves,
not at the level of probability.

This does not make them illusions. The shadow cast by a three-dimensional object is
real. It has a real shape. It moves in real ways. The fact that its shape is determined
by the geometry of the object casting it does not make it any less real — it makes it
more explicable.

The Unitary Manifold says our experience of time is that shadow. Real, unavoidable,
and — if the theory is right — geometrically necessary rather than statistically
convenient.
