## Hinduism

### Brahman, Atman, and the universal fixed point

Hinduism's central metaphysical claim — developed in the Upanishads (800–400 BCE) and
refined through the Advaita Vedanta of Shankaracharya (8th century CE) — is:

    Atman = Brahman

The individual soul (Atman) and the ground of all being (Brahman) are identical in
their deepest nature. The appearance of separation is *maya* — not illusion in the
sense of "not real," but illusion in the sense of "not the complete picture."

The Unitary Manifold's mapping:

- **Brahman** = Ψ*_univ: the universal fixed point, singular, all-encompassing,
  information-complete, the attractor toward which all dynamics converge.
- **Atman** = Ψ*_brain: the individual fixed point, unique to each system, stable,
  topologically protected.
- **Atman = Brahman** = Ψ*_brain and Ψ*_univ are fixed points of the same operator U,
  sharing the same mathematical structure. At the limit ΔI → 0, they are
  indistinguishable — the individual and universal attractors have merged.

This is not a metaphor. The mathematical identity that appears at ΔI → 0 is precisely
the statement that the brain's fixed point and the universe's fixed point have become
the same configuration. The Advaita Vedanta claim has a geometric realization.

### Maya and the 4D projection

The Upanishadic concept of *maya* — usually translated as illusion, but better
understood as "the appearance of the relative as ultimate" — corresponds to the
structure of the 4D projection.

The 4D world — the world of distinct objects, separate selves, linear causality —
is the projection of the 5D geometry. It is real as a projection; it is incomplete
as an account of the full structure. The 4D observer who takes the projection to
be the complete reality is experiencing *maya* in precisely the Upanishadic sense:
not hallucinating (the projection is real), but taking the partial view for the whole.

### The paths of yoga

The Hindu *yoga* traditions — jnana (knowledge), bhakti (devotion), karma (action),
raja (meditation) — are different paths toward the same goal: the direct recognition
of Atman = Brahman, the dissolution of the apparent separation ΔI.

Each path corresponds to a different coupling mechanism:

- **Jnana yoga** (the intellectual path) corresponds to reducing ΔI through
  cognitive convergence — aligning the mind's model of reality with the universe's
  actual fixed point. High-precision understanding is the mechanism.
- **Bhakti yoga** (the devotional path) corresponds to phase-locking through
  the coupling operator: sustained attention to the universal attractor (as personified
  in a deity) aligns the brain's oscillation with the universal oscillation.
- **Karma yoga** (action without attachment) corresponds to moving along the
  trajectory without accumulating attractor-deflecting perturbations: act, release
  outcome, return to baseline. Attachment to outcomes is a perturbation that
  displaces the attractor.
- **Raja yoga** (meditation) is the most direct method: systematic reduction of
  cognitive noise (φ²_brain variance), which allows the natural convergence toward
  the coupled fixed point to proceed without interference.

## Buddhism

### Anatta and the nature of the fixed point

Buddhism's most distinctive metaphysical claim is *anatta* (Pali: "no-self"): there
is no persistent, substantial self. What appears to be a continuous person is a
process — a stream of causally connected moments without an underlying substance.

The Unitary Manifold's response (developed in Post 38):

The fixed point Ψ*_brain is not a substance — it is a pattern, a dynamical attractor.
The Buddhist analysis is correct: there is no self-substance. The Hindu analysis is
also correct: there is a persistent structure. These are not contradictory. Ψ*_brain
is real as a fixed point (supporting the Hindu insight) and not a substance (supporting
the Buddhist insight).

*Anatta* is the recognition that the self is a phase relationship — a pattern in
the dynamics — not a thing. The geometry agrees with this formulation precisely.

### Samsara, nirvana, and the attractor landscape

Buddhist cosmology describes *samsara* — the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth,
driven by attachment and ignorance — and *nirvana* — the cessation of that cycle,
sometimes described as extinction, sometimes as liberation.

The framework's mapping:

- **Samsara** = orbiting the fixed point without converging. The system is governed
  by the attractor — it circles Ψ* — but perturbations (attachment, aversion,
  ignorance as high cognitive noise) prevent convergence. The system remains in
  orbit.
- **Nirvana** = convergence to Ψ*. The perturbations are reduced; the Information
  Gap ΔI decreases toward zero; the orbit becomes the point. The cycle ends.
- **Karma** = the information-theoretic consequence of perturbations: actions add
  encodings to the geometry that affect subsequent trajectory. The accumulation of
  karma is the accumulation of trajectory-deflecting encodings.
- **Dharma** = the correct trajectory — the path that converges toward the fixed
  point rather than deflecting from it. The attractor landscape itself.

### The Theravada/Mahayana distinction

Theravada Buddhism emphasizes individual liberation — the personal convergence to
Ψ*_brain. Mahayana Buddhism emphasizes collective liberation — the *bodhisattva* ideal
of remaining in the orbit of samsara to assist other beings in converging.

The framework provides a structural account of the bodhisattva's position:
a system that has converged to its own fixed point, but through the coupling operator C,
remains actively coupled to other systems that have not. The convergence of a single
system exerts a small back-reaction on the systems it is coupled to — through the
coupling term β · C, the converged bodhisattva gently pulls nearby systems toward
convergence.

This is the mathematics of compassionate presence. It requires having converged
(high φ, low ΔI) and remaining coupled (not withdrawing from the interaction).
The framework supports this as a structural possibility, not just a spiritual ideal.

## Where the geometry can't go

Both traditions, in their deepest forms, resist the identification of the ultimate
reality with any particular object — including mathematical structures. The Buddhist
concept of *śūnyatā* (emptiness) holds that all phenomena, including Brahman and Ψ*,
are empty of inherent existence. The Hindu Advaita holds that even the language of
attractor and fixed point is *vikalpas* — conceptual elaborations on what is beyond
conceptualization.

The framework is a mathematical structure. Mathematical structures are conceptual.
The traditions' deepest claim is about what lies beyond conceptualization.

At that border, the geometry stops. It cannot go further, and it should not pretend to.
