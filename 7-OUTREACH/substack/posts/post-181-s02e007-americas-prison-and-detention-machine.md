## Three hard findings

### 1) The pretrial machine is the pressure point

When most jail detention is pretrial and many people are unconvicted, the front-end legal process is the system’s central harm generator.

If your pretrial design is wrong, everything downstream worsens.

### 2) Conditions are not a “local aberration” problem

Patterns repeat nationally:

- delayed medical response,
- safety failures,
- weak abuse detection,
- unstable care continuity,
- weak independent correction loops.

That is structural, not anecdotal.

### 3) Reform success is real when design is real

Jurisdictions that reduce unnecessary detention, tighten accountability, and center rehabilitation over warehousing show better safety and better reentry outcomes.

Reform is possible. The constraint is governance discipline.

---

## Immediate blueprint (0–24 months)

- End cash bail dependence for low/moderate-risk categories.
- Mandate robust detention hearings with evidence standards.
- Impose enforceable care minimums and heat safety standards.
- Strictly cap solitary and ban it for highly vulnerable populations.
- Upgrade PREA audits from checkbox compliance to detection-grade accountability.
- Publish real-time custody outcomes (deaths, injuries, abuse findings, grievance closure).

---

## Structural blueprint (2–10 years)

- Sentencing and admissions redesign.
- Mental health system coupling outside jail walls.
- Budget incentives tied to outcome quality, not occupancy.
- Independent inspectorates with subpoena and publication authority.
- Reentry treated as critical infrastructure (ID, meds, housing, employment continuity).

---

## Why this matters now

A system that can take liberty must meet a higher burden of transparency and competence than almost any other state function.

Right now, in too many places, it does not.

If we keep calling that normal, we are not defending public safety. We are normalizing preventable institutional harm.
