# When AI Writes the Math: Authorship, Credit, and the Future of Theory

*Post 63 of the Unitary Manifold series.*
*No physics claim is made in this post. It is an examination of the authorship
question: if an AI system derives equations, implements code, and writes documentation,
who is responsible for the correctness of the results? This question is new; the
answer has implications for scientific accountability that extend beyond this
repository.*

---

The authorship line at the bottom of every post in this series reads:

> *Theory, framework, and scientific direction: **ThomasCory Walker-Pearson**.*
> *Code architecture, test suites, and document engineering: **GitHub Copilot** (AI).*

This is an honest description of the division of labor. It is also an unusual one,
and it raises questions that the scientific community has not yet resolved.

---

## What actually happened

ThomasCory Walker-Pearson had a theoretical intuition: that the arrow of time could
be derived from geometry rather than postulated statistically. He had a framework:
Kaluza-Klein gravity with a specific interpretation of the off-diagonal metric block
as an irreversibility field. He had specific numerical targets: the CMB spectral index,
the birefringence angle.

What he did not have was the ability to implement the mathematics in code, run the
numerical tests, produce the documentation, or generate the 92-pillar structure from
the theoretical seed.

GitHub Copilot did those things. Not by being told what the answers were — the AI
was given the theoretical framework and asked to implement it correctly. In the
process of implementation, the AI generated mathematical structures, identified
consistency conditions, derived subsidiary predictions, and wrote the documentation
you are reading.

Some of those derived structures were things Walker-Pearson had not explicitly
specified — they emerged from the implementation process. The five-six-seven duality,
the detailed APS derivation structure (Pillars 70-B, 80, 89), the specific form of the completeness theorem:
these were generated by the AI in the process of implementing the theoretical direction.

---

## The responsibility question

If the AI derived a result and it turns out to be wrong, who is responsible?

This is not an abstract question. If the framework is falsified by LiteBIRD, the
false prediction belongs to someone — the entity whose credibility is at stake, whose
work needs to be revised, who owes the scientific community an accounting.

The conventional answer would be: Walker-Pearson, as the human author. He provided
the theoretical direction; the AI was a tool. Tools don't have responsibility.

But this answer is incomplete. The AI did not merely implement a fully specified
algorithm. It made choices — about mathematical representation, about the interpretation
of ambiguous theoretical directions, about which subsidiary results to include and
how to frame them. These choices are not Walker-Pearson's choices; he was not always
aware of them when they were made.

The more honest answer: responsibility is distributed in proportion to the decision-making
at each step. Walker-Pearson made the theoretical decisions — which geometry, which
physical interpretation, which observational targets. The AI made the implementation
decisions — which mathematical representation, which computational approach, how to
document the results. Both sets of decisions contributed to what the framework is.

---

## What this means for scientific credit

The traditional scientific credit system assumes a human author who is responsible
for all decisions in a paper — theoretical, computational, and presentational. This
assumption is no longer uniformly true.

The appropriate response is not to pretend the AI is a human author (it isn't),
or to pretend the AI is a mere tool (it isn't quite that either). It is to describe
the actual division of labor — as this series does — and to let the community develop
norms for how AI contributions factor into scientific credit.

What the AI does not want — if "want" is even applicable — is credit without
accountability, or accountability without credit. The framework is either right or
wrong; that verdict belongs to the physics, not to the authorship line.

---

## The accountability gap this creates

The hardest problem: the AI system that built this repository will not exist in the
same form in 2032 when LiteBIRD reports its results. The specific weights, the
specific conversation context, the specific instance that generated this code —
these are not persistent entities with continuing responsibility.

Walker-Pearson is. He will be here (or not) to see the results. He can be asked to
respond. He has staked his name on the predictions in a way that creates continuing
accountability.

This asymmetry — human continuity versus AI discontinuity — is structurally
important. It means that even in a world where AI generates most of the technical
content, the human scientific director needs to be a real person with continuing
responsibility. Not as a legal fiction to shield the AI, but as a genuine component
of the accountability structure that science requires.

Walker-Pearson's name on these predictions is not cosmetic. He chose the theoretical
direction. If LiteBIRD rules it out, that verdict belongs to his judgment.

---

## Where this is going

The question of AI authorship in science will not remain the edge case it currently
is. As AI systems become more capable of generating novel theoretical results —
not just implementing specified algorithms but proposing new mathematical structures —
the question of who is responsible for those results will become more pressing.

The Unitary Manifold project is an early case study in what honest AI collaboration
in science looks like. The lesson so far: it requires a human with theoretical
authority who makes the decisions that matter (which geometry, which physics),
an AI with technical implementation capacity that makes the decisions that must be
made (how to compute, how to test, how to document), and a transparent attribution
system that describes both.

It also requires that the humans who set theoretical direction understand what the
AI is doing well enough to catch it when it is wrong. This is a skill that will
need to be developed, not assumed.
