The new question is simple: if `(5,7,74)` is our stable scaffold, where does it
actually touch hardware build decisions?

Pillar 250 answers that by creating a practical inverse-design surface. It does
not claim a guaranteed device. It produces a deterministic, testable priority map
across eight bottleneck domains: coherence, fidelity, thermal margin, fabrication
repeatability, control latency, error-correction overhead, defect suppression,
and braid-geometry alignment.

The core shift is from narrative to allocation. We compute readiness, rank
architecture families, and distribute budget where gap×coupling impact is highest.

That is what engineering needs: ordered moves, not slogans.

### What this pillar computes

- Domain-level adequacy scores in `[0,1]`
- Composite inverse-design readiness with dispersion penalty
- Architecture alignment ranking (topological/superconducting/neutral-atom/photonic)
- Budget allocation by impact concentration
- Deterministic uncertainty bands via Monte Carlo

### Why this matters now

This repository already had a serious quantum lane and a serious materials lane.
The missing piece was a bridge to fabricable systems. Pillar 250 is that bridge:
it takes constants seriously, but it also takes wiring diagrams, cleanrooms,
latency loops, and defect maps seriously.

### Honest boundary

This is an adjacent track, not a hardgate physics promotion and not a guarantee
of product outcomes.

### Falsification condition

If pre-registered multi-platform fabrication campaigns consistently fail to show
any correlation between Pillar 250 readiness ordering and observed benchmark
performance, the pillar fails and should be revised or retired.
