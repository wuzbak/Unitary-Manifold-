# Thermoplastics — Full Guide
## What They Are, How They Behave, and How to Actually Recycle Them

> group running a Precious Plastic operation.

## Part 1 — What Is a Thermoplastic?

### The fundamental distinction

All plastics divide into two fundamental categories:

**Thermoplastics** — melt when heated, re-solidify when cooled.  Can be
melted and re-formed repeatedly.  This means they are, in principle, recyclable.

**Thermosets** — undergo an irreversible chemical reaction when cured.  They
do not melt; they char or degrade.  Cannot be mechanically recycled.

| Thermoplastics (recyclable in principle) | Thermosets (not mechanically recyclable) |
|---|---|
| PET, HDPE, PVC, LDPE, PP, PS, ABS, nylon | Epoxy resin, polyurethane foam, phenolic (Bakelite), vulcanised rubber, most composites |

**This guide covers thermoplastics only.**  Thermosets are a separate problem
requiring chemical or energy-recovery approaches.

### The polymer chain model

A thermoplastic is a very long chain of repeating molecular units (monomers)
bonded end-to-end.  The chain:

- Is **long** (molecular weight 10,000–1,000,000+ g/mol)
- Consists of identical or near-identical repeat units
- Interacts with neighbouring chains through van der Waals forces and,
  in some cases, hydrogen bonds

When heated above the **glass transition temperature (Tg)** the chains
gain mobility and the material softens.  Above the **melting temperature (Tm)**
the material flows.  On cooling, chains re-entangle and solidify.

**This is the physical basis of thermoplastic recycling:** the material can
be melted, re-formed, and re-solidified.

**This is also the physical basis of degradation:** every melt cycle exposes
the chains to thermal and mechanical stress that shortens them.  Shorter
chains mean lower molecular weight, lower melt viscosity, lower mechanical
strength.  This is the quality-loss mechanism (A-score reduction) that
limits mechanical recycling cycles.

## Part 2 — The Seven Resin Codes Explained in Full

### Resin Code 1 — PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate)

**Chemical structure:** Condensation polymer of ethylene glycol and
terephthalic acid.  Repeat unit: -[OC₂H₄-OOC-C₆H₄-CO]ₙ-

**Properties:**
- Density: 1.33–1.39 g/cm³ (sinks in water — important for float-sink sorting)
- Glass transition: 75–80°C
- Melting point: 250–260°C
- Clear to translucent in film form; opaque when foamed
- Good gas barrier (CO₂, O₂) — key for carbonated drink bottles
- Good chemical resistance
- FDA/EFSA food contact approved

**Where you find it:**
- Clear drinks bottles (all carbonated drinks, water, juice)
- Food trays (ready meals, berries, salad)
- Polyester textile fibres (most "fleece" garments are PET)
- Strapping and rope
- Photographic film and Mylar film

**Recycling profile:**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Global collection infrastructure | Excellent for bottles in most developed countries |
| Recycling technology maturity | High — bottle-to-bottle well established |
| Typical A-score (mechanical, 1 cycle) | 0.75–0.88 |
| Typical A-score (chemical recycling) | 0.90–0.98 |
| Per-cycle quality decay (alpha) | 0.12–0.18 |
| Maximum useful mechanical cycles | 4–6 |
| Critical contaminant | PVC (density overlap; yellow/brown discolouration of melt) |
| Critical sorting challenge | Opaque/coloured PET (black PET invisible to NIR) |

**Recycling process (mechanical — bottle-to-fibre or bottle-to-packaging):**

1. Sort by colour (clear, green, amber) — NIR + optical
2. Remove labels, caps (PP/HDPE caps float; PET bottle sinks — float-sink separation)
3. Granulate to 10–15 mm flake
4. Hot wash (90°C, 2% NaOH caustic, 15 min) — removes adhesives, food residue
5. Rinse and centrifuge to <0.5% moisture
6. Optional: Solid State Polycondensation (SSP) to restore intrinsic viscosity
   for food-contact applications
7. Pelletise

**Chemical recycling options for PET:**

| Process | Reagent | Temperature | Output | A-score |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Glycolysis | Ethylene glycol, Zn acetate catalyst | 190–200°C | BHET monomer | 0.90–0.98 |
| Methanolysis | Methanol, pressure | 180–280°C | DMT + EG monomers | 0.93–0.99 |
| Hydrolysis (acid) | HNO₃ or H₂SO₄, high temp | 120–300°C | TPA + EG | 0.90–0.97 |
| Hydrolysis (base) | NaOH | 90–200°C | disodium terephthalate | 0.85–0.93 |
| Hydrolysis (neutral) | Steam/water, pressure | 200–300°C | TPA + EG | 0.90–0.96 |

**Key fact:** Chemical recycling of PET back to monomers has been commercial
since the 1990s.  The barrier is cost and collection volume, not technology.

### Resin Code 2 — HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)

**Chemical structure:** Linear polyethylene, minimal branching.
Repeat unit: -[CH₂-CH₂]ₙ-

**Properties:**
- Density: 0.94–0.97 g/cm³ (floats in water — float-sink separation)
- Glass transition: -100°C (flexible at all normal temperatures)
- Melting point: 120–140°C
- Opaque white or translucent in thick section; coloured when pigmented
- Good chemical resistance (resists most acids, bases, solvents)
- Good low-temperature impact resistance
- FDA/EFSA food contact approved

**Where you find it:**
- Milk bottles, juice containers
- Shampoo, conditioner, body wash bottles
- Cleaning product bottles
- Carrier bags (thicker gauge)
- Agricultural film
- Pipes, fittings, drainage
- Wheelie bins, crates, pallets
- Children's toys

**Recycling profile:**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Global collection infrastructure | Good — included in most kerbside schemes |
| Recycling technology maturity | High — well-established wash-granulate-extrude |
| Typical A-score (mechanical, 1 cycle) | 0.78–0.90 |
| Per-cycle quality decay (alpha) | 0.12–0.18 |
| Maximum useful mechanical cycles | 4–7 |
| Critical contaminant | Food residue (odour and colour in melt) |
| Critical sorting challenge | Coloured HDPE — mixed colours produce grey/brown recyclate |

**The colour problem with HDPE:**

Natural (uncoloured, translucent) HDPE recyclate is worth significantly
more than mixed-colour HDPE:

| Material | Market value (indicative) |
|---|---|
| Natural HDPE pellet (clear) | $400–$700/tonne |
| Mixed colour HDPE pellet | $150–$350/tonne |
| Black HDPE pellet | $100–$200/tonne |

Black HDPE is coloured with carbon black pigment — which also blocks NIR
sensors used for sorting.  Black plastic containers are essentially invisible
to automated sorting lines.  They are a product design failure.

**Do not buy black plastic packaging if you have any alternative.**

**Applications for recycled HDPE:**

| Recyclate quality | Application |
|---|---|
| Natural HDPE, A-score ≥ 0.85 | New milk bottles, food packaging (with virgin blend) |
| Mixed colour, A-score 0.65–0.80 | Cosmetic bottles, cleaning product containers |
| Lower grade | Pipes, agricultural drainage, construction sheeting |
| Black HDPE | Limited — some construction products; mostly downcycled |

### Resin Code 3 — PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

**Chemical structure:** Vinyl polymer.  Repeat unit: -[CH₂-CHCl]ₙ-

**Properties:**
- Density: 1.30–1.45 g/cm³
- Melting point: 160–210°C (depends on plasticiser content)
- **Contains approximately 57% chlorine by mass**
- Rigid (unplasticised, uPVC) or flexible (plasticised with phthalates or
  DINP/DIDP)

**Where you find it:**
- Window frames, doors (rigid uPVC)
- Water pipes, drainage pipes (rigid uPVC)
- Electrical cable insulation (flexible PVC)
- Flooring (vinyl floor tiles, sheet vinyl)
- Medical tubing and blood bags
- Credit cards, ID cards
- Some food packaging (increasingly replaced)
- Cling film (increasingly replaced by PE-based)

**Why PVC is the most problematic thermoplastic for recycling:**

1. **Chlorine content:** At temperatures above 200°C, PVC decomposes
   releasing hydrogen chloride (HCl) — a corrosive gas that damages
   processing equipment and produces dioxins in the flue gas if combusted
   improperly.

2. **Plasticiser contamination:** Flexible PVC contains phthalate or other
   plasticisers — many of which are endocrine disruptors (DEHP, DBP —
   restricted in EU since 2015 for consumer products).  These migrate into
   recyclate and into the environment.

3. **Density overlap:** PVC (1.30–1.45 g/cm³) has nearly identical density
   to PET (1.33–1.39 g/cm³) — both sink in water.  Traditional float-sink
   cannot separate them.  Even a small amount of PVC contamination in a
   PET stream ruins the melt (yellowing, HCl degradation, equipment damage).

4. **Additive diversity:** Different PVC products contain wildly different
   formulations — lead stabilisers (older pipes, cables), organotin
   stabilisers, calcium-zinc stabilisers.  Cannot mix formulations.

**Recycling options:**

| Stream | Approach | Feasibility |
|---|---|---|
| uPVC window profiles | Industry take-back (VinylPlus scheme, EU) | Good — closed-loop |
| uPVC pipes | Construction waste segregation; specialist recycler | Limited |
| Flexible PVC (cable) | Specialist cable recycler | Good — copper recovery drives economics |
| Flexible PVC (medical) | Specialist medical waste stream | Limited |
| Mixed PVC consumer packaging | Effectively not recyclable in general stream | Poor |

**Practical guidance:**
- PVC in a recycling bin contaminates the PET stream.  Do not put PVC
  in kerbside recycling unless your council explicitly states it is accepted.
- Look for the "3" code and the letters "PVC" or "V" on the product.
- Rigid uPVC window frames and pipes can be returned to specialist
  recyclers — check with your installer.

### Resin Code 4 — LDPE (Low-Density Polyethylene)

**Chemical structure:** Branched polyethylene.  More branches than HDPE →
lower crystallinity → lower density, softer, more flexible.

**Properties:**
- Density: 0.91–0.94 g/cm³ (floats)
- Melting point: 105–115°C (lower than HDPE)
- Soft, flexible, transparent in film form
- Poor gas barrier vs. PET
- Good chemical resistance

**Where you find it:**
- Plastic bags, carrier bags, bin liners
- Cling film (food wrap)
- Bread bags, produce bags
- Squeezable bottles (ketchup, shampoo when soft)
- Bubble wrap
- Agricultural mulch film
- Shrink wrap on pallets and packages
- The inner lining of paper cups (thin LDPE laminate)

**The soft plastics recycling problem:**

LDPE in film form — bags, cling film, bread bags — is commonly not accepted
in kerbside recycling bins because:

1. It wraps around sorting machinery and causes jams — operationally disruptive
2. It is volumetrically large but low mass — economics are poor at sorting scale
3. It is easily contaminated with food residue
4. Mixed PE films (LDPE + LLDPE + some HDPE) require compatible sorting

**What happens instead:**

- Soft plastics collection points at **supermarkets** are the primary route
  in many countries (REDcycle in Australia, Bags2School in UK, drop-offs
  at many retailers)
- Collected soft plastics → mechanical recycling → composite lumber, decking,
  park furniture, bin liners — most applications A-score 0.50–0.70
- Some programmes → injection moulding for construction products

**The paper cup problem:**

Paper cups are laminated with a thin LDPE film to prevent leaks.  This
makes them:
- Not recyclable in paper streams (LDPE contaminates the pulp)
- Not recyclable in plastic streams (too thin, too contaminated)
- Effectively unrecyclable in most existing infrastructure

Some specialist cup recyclers exist (James Cropper in the UK).  Otherwise,
the practical answer is to bring a reusable cup.

### Resin Code 5 — PP (Polypropylene)

**Chemical structure:** Vinyl polymer with methyl side groups.
Repeat unit: -[CH₂-CH(CH₃)]ₙ-

**Properties:**
- Density: 0.89–0.92 g/cm³ (lightest of the common thermoplastics; floats)
- Melting point: 160–175°C
- Glass transition: 0°C (may become brittle in cold)
- Good chemical resistance
- Good heat resistance (highest of commodity thermoplastics)
- Can be made transparent or opaque
- FDA/EFSA food contact approved

**Where you find it:**
- Yoghurt pots, margarine tubs, ice cream containers
- Food containers, deli containers, dips, sauces
- Bottle caps (the caps on PET bottles are almost always PP)
- Microwaveable containers
- Car bumpers, dashboards
- Carpets and rugs
- Medical syringes, pill blister packs
- Ropes and twine
- Straws (increasingly used as paper straw alternative)

**Recycling profile:**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Collection infrastructure | Growing — less established than PET/HDPE |
| Technology maturity | Good for rigid containers; limited for thin-wall |
| Typical A-score (mechanical, 1 cycle) | 0.75–0.88 |
| Per-cycle quality decay (alpha) | 0.13–0.20 |
| Maximum useful mechanical cycles | 4–6 |
| Sorting challenge | Similar NIR signature to HDPE; colour sorting important |

**The bottle cap question:**

For decades, advice varied on whether to leave caps on PET bottles.
Current guidance in most countries: **leave the cap on.**

Reason:
- Loose caps are too small for sorting lines and fall through
- Caps on bottles can be separated at the float-sink stage (PP floats,
  PET sinks)
- Empty bottles without caps can squash on the sorting belt, causing jams

**Always check your local council guidance** — it varies by facility.

### Resin Code 6 — PS (Polystyrene)

**Chemical structure:** Vinyl polymer with phenyl (benzene ring) side groups.
Repeat unit: -[CH₂-CH(C₆H₅)]ₙ-

**Properties:**
- Density: 1.04–1.10 g/cm³ rigid; ~0.01–0.05 g/cm³ foam (EPS)
- Glass transition: 100°C
- Melting point: 240°C
- Rigid PS: hard, clear (amorphous), brittle
- EPS (expanded PS, Styrofoam): foam, excellent thermal insulation
- HIPS (high-impact PS): toughened with rubber; opaque white; impact-resistant

**Where you find it:**
- Disposable cups, plates, cutlery (increasingly banned)
- Yoghurt pots (some — often HIPS rather than PP)
- CD cases, jewel cases
- Meat trays (EPS foam)
- Takeaway containers (EPS foam)
- Building insulation (EPS foam board)
- Packaging foam and void fill (EPS)
- Coat hangers
- Refrigerator linings

**Why PS is one of the hardest to recycle:**

1. **EPS is 98% air** — transport cost per useful mass is enormous.
   A lorry full of EPS foam carries almost no material mass; economics
   only work if foam is densified first (compacted 40:1 ratio).

2. **Styrene monomer:** PS can degrade back to styrene monomer during
   heating.  Styrene is a potential carcinogen and has a distinctive
   unpleasant odour.  Recycled PS products can outgas styrene.

3. **Infrastructure gap:** Most kerbside schemes do not accept EPS.
   A small number of specialist EPS collection and densification schemes
   exist.

4. **Brominated flame retardants (BFRs):** Many electronics applications
   of PS (TV housings, computer equipment) use HIPS with BFR additives —
   notably HBCD (hexabromocyclododecane, now restricted as a POP under
   the Stockholm Convention).  BFR-contaminated PS cannot be mechanically
   recycled safely.

**Practical options:**

| Stream | Best option |
|---|---|
| Clean EPS building insulation | Specialist EPS take-back (insulation manufacturers) |
| EPS packaging from appliances | Find local foam densification point; some councils |
| Food-contaminated EPS trays | General waste — contamination prevents recycling |
| Rigid PS (clear cups, cases) | Very limited kerbside acceptance |
| HIPS electronics (check for BFR) | E-waste stream, not plastic recycling |

**Chemical recycling:** PS depolymerises relatively easily to styrene monomer
at 300–350°C.  Commercial pyrolytic depolymerisation of PS to styrene
is technically feasible and operated at small scale in some countries.

### Resin Code 7 — Other

Resin code 7 is a catch-all for everything not covered by codes 1–6.
This includes:

| Polymer | Common uses | Recyclability |
|---|---|---|
| **PC (Polycarbonate)** | Safety glasses, baby bottles, CDs, phone cases, car headlights | Specialist; concerns about BPA migration |
| **ABS (Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene)** | Lego, car interiors, electronics housings, keyboards | Limited — often BFR-contaminated |
| **Nylon (PA, polyamide)** | Engineering components, textiles, toothbrush bristles | Limited — specialist textile recycling |
| **PLA (Polylactic acid)** | "Compostable" packaging, 3D printing filament | Industrial compost only (not home compost, not plastic recycling) |
| **Bioplastics (PHA, PBAT, starch blends)** | Biodegradable packaging | Industrial compost; NOT conventional plastic recycling |
| **Multilayer/barrier films** | Juice cartons, pouches, medical packaging | Effectively not recyclable — layers cannot be separated |
| **Acrylic (PMMA)** | Perspex, signage, car tail lights | Specialist only |

**Critical warning on bioplastics and "compostable" plastics:**

Do NOT put PLA or other bioplastics in conventional plastic recycling bins.
They contaminate PET streams (similar NIR signature) and cause processing
problems.

"Compostable" means industrially compostable at temperatures >60°C — not
home compostable and absolutely not biodegradable in the ocean or landfill.
Most home and municipal composting facilities do not accept them.

If in doubt: general waste.  Contaminating a recycling stream with
bioplastics causes more harm than the marginal landfill impact of a single
container.

## Part 3 — The Degradation Science: Why Quality Decreases

### The molecular mechanism

Each mechanical recycling cycle subjects the polymer to:

1. **Thermal degradation:** Sustained heat above Tm causes:
   - Chain scission (breaking of main chain) — reduces molecular weight
   - Oxidation (in presence of oxygen) — introduces oxygen-containing groups
     that weaken the chain and discolour the material
   - Volatile generation (styrene from PS, acetaldehyde from PET)

2. **Mechanical degradation:** Shear stress in the extruder screw and die:
   - Breaks inter-chain entanglements
   - Can cause further chain scission at high shear rates
   - Generates localised hot spots

3. **Contamination accumulation:**
   - Residual food components, inks, adhesives, additives
   - Each cycle concentrates non-plastic components in the recyclate
   - Cannot be removed by physical processing once mixed at molecular level

**The result is exponential quality decay:**

```
phi_n = phi_0 * exp(-alpha * n)
```

Where:
- phi_0 = quality of virgin or freshly recycled material
- n = number of cycles
- alpha = per-cycle decay coefficient (material-specific)

**Per-cycle decay coefficients (alpha) by material:**

| Material | Alpha range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| PET | 0.12–0.18 | Higher if moisture not controlled; IV loss primary metric |
| HDPE (natural) | 0.12–0.18 | Colour contamination often limits before property degradation |
| HDPE (coloured) | 0.15–0.22 | Darker colours degrade faster optically |
| PP | 0.13–0.20 | Oxidation discolouration common |
| PS | 0.15–0.25 | Styrene off-gas increases with cycles |
| LDPE film | 0.18–0.28 | Extensive degradation per cycle due to high surface area processing |

**Practical limits:**

| Alpha value | 3-cycle quality | 5-cycle quality | 8-cycle quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.12 | 70% of virgin | 55% | 38% |
| 0.18 | 58% | 40% | 21% |
| 0.25 | 47% | 29% | 13% |

After 5 mechanical cycles at typical alpha values, most plastics are at
30–55% of their original quality.  They are still "recycled content" by
any current reporting standard — but they are not equivalent to virgin
material or to first-cycle recyclate.

## Part 4 — Chemical Recycling: Resetting Quality to Zero-Cycle

Chemical recycling breaks the polymer all the way back to its monomers,
allowing repolymerisation to virgin-equivalent quality.

### Why it matters

Chemical recycling is the only way to close the loop permanently.
Mechanical recycling is a declining spiral (quality lost each cycle).
Chemical recycling resets the spiral to the top.

### Technologies by maturity

**Commercially proven:**
- PET glycolysis and methanolysis (operating at scale in several countries)
- PET hydrolysis (some commercial operations)
- Polystyrene depolymerisation to styrene (small scale commercial)

**Pilot / pre-commercial:**
- Polyolefin (PE, PP) cracking to naphtha/wax (several facilities operating)
- Enzymatic PET depolymerisation (Carbios process — first commercial plant
  France, 2025)
- Pyrolysis of mixed plastics to pyrolysis oil (many facilities operating
  but COP and output quality variable)

**Research stage:**
- PE glycolysis / solvolysis
- PP methanolysis
- Mixed-stream catalytic cracking to specific monomers

### The COP requirement

For chemical recycling to be genuinely beneficial (not entropy-laundering):

```
COP = energy_recovered / energy_input > 1
```

Several current pyrolysis-to-fuel operations run at COP 0.7–0.9 — they
consume more energy than they recover.  They are not recycling; they are
subsidised disposal with an energy penalty.

Before supporting or investing in any chemical recycling operation, ask:
- What is the measured COP?
- What is the purity and specification of the output?
- What is the mass balance (input tonnes → output tonnes)?
- Is the output used to make the same product type, or downgraded to fuel?

## Part 5 — Design for Recyclability: What Products Should Look Like

Products designed with recyclability in mind have:

### 1. Single material or easily separated materials

**Good:** A clear PET bottle with a PP cap and a paper label (all three
separated mechanically in existing infrastructure).

**Bad:** A multi-layer pouch with PET/LDPE/foil laminate (inseparable;
effectively unrecyclable).

**Bad:** A black HDPE bottle (carbon black pigment blocks NIR — invisible
to sorting).

### 2. Removable labels

Paper labels that peel cleanly.  Sleeve labels that slide off.

Not: heat-shrink labels that wrap the full bottle body (must be removed
before sorting; many facilities cannot do this).

Not: glued-on printed labels with aggressive adhesive (leaves residue
after mechanical removal).

### 3. Minimal additive diversity

Products made with standard grades of one polymer with stable pigmentation
and conventional additive packages are more recyclable than products with
proprietary additives, reactive coatings, or multi-functional treatments.

### 4. Consistent density (for float-sink)

Products should be made from materials whose density is distinct from
contaminants to allow float-sink separation.

### 5. No black carbon-black pigment

Replace with alternative pigments detectable by NIR.  This is technically
solved: near-IR reflective black pigments exist and are commercially available.

### 6. No unnecessary barriers or coatings

Where barrier properties are genuinely required (gas barrier for carbonated
drinks), design so the barrier layer is separable or is the same material
as the structural layer (e.g., PET EVOH PET where EVOH is a separate layer
in the float-sink step, or mono-PET with higher barrier vs. laminate).

Where barrier properties are not required (rigid HDPE for laundry detergent),
do not add them.

## Part 7 — The Practical Limits and Honest Priorities

1. **Separation at source is still the most important step.**  All the
   material science above is irrelevant if the input stream is contaminated.

2. **Mechanical recycling of PET and HDPE is mature and worth using** — but
   only for 3–5 cycles.  After that, quality is too low for most applications.

3. **PVC should not be in general recycling streams.**  Its presence contaminates
   PET irrecoverably at the melt stage.

4. **Chemical recycling is the right long-term answer for PET** and is
   commercially proven.  It needs scale.  Scale needs collection volume.
   Collection volume needs deposit-return or equivalent.

5. **Polyolefins (HDPE, PP, LDPE) need chemical recycling routes** that are
   not yet commercially mature at scale.  Mechanical recycling is the current
   best option.  Design for recyclability (single material, no black pigment,
   compatible additives) extends the useful mechanical cycle count.

6. **PS and PVC are legacy problems.**  Phase them out in new products wherever
   technically feasible.  Recycle existing stock through specialist streams.

7. **Bioplastics and "compostable" plastics contaminate plastic recycling.**
   They are not a solution to the plastic recycling problem.  They are a
   solution to the littering problem (slow breakdown in open environments)
   at the cost of complicating recycling.  Use with clear eyes about the
   trade-off.
