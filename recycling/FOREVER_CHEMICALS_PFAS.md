# Forever Chemicals (PFAS) — Full Guide
## What They Are, Where They Hide, Why They Matter, and What Can Be Done

> **Who this is for:** Everyone.  PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances)
> are present in the blood of virtually every person on Earth.  Understanding
> them is not optional — it is a matter of personal and collective health.

**Author:** ThomasCory Walker-Pearson  
**Date:** April 2026  
**Version:** 1.0

## Part 1 — What Are Forever Chemicals?

### The basic chemistry (plain English)

PFAS are a family of more than **10,000 synthetic chemicals** built around
chains of carbon atoms with fluorine atoms bonded to them.  The carbon-
fluorine bond is the strongest bond in organic chemistry.  It does not break
under heat, light, acid, base, or biological attack.

This is why they were invented: a material that nothing can destroy is
extremely useful for industry.  It is also catastrophic for the environment,
because a material nothing can destroy **never breaks down**.

Hence the name: **forever chemicals**.

### The two key properties that make PFAS dangerous

**1. Persistence:** PFAS do not degrade in the environment under any normal
conditions.  They accumulate in soil, water, and living tissue indefinitely.
A PFAS molecule released in 1950 is still present somewhere in the biosphere
today.

**2. Bioaccumulation:** Organisms absorb PFAS faster than they can excrete
them.  Concentration increases up the food chain (biomagnification).
A fish in a PFAS-contaminated river has higher PFAS than the water.
A predatory bird eating those fish has higher still.  Humans at the top
of the food chain accumulate the highest concentrations of anything we
eat or drink.

### The scale of the problem

- PFAS have been detected in:
  - Drinking water sources on every continent
  - Arctic and Antarctic ice and soil (nowhere is remote enough)
  - Rainwater globally — even in the most pristine locations, PFAS in
    rainwater now exceeds safe drinking water limits set by the EU and US EPA
  - The blood of 97–99 % of people tested in developed countries
  - Human breast milk
  - Polar bears, eagles, dolphins — apex predators far from any industrial use

- The US EPA revised its lifetime health advisory for PFOA and PFOS in 2022
  from 70 parts per trillion to **0.004 and 0.02 parts per trillion** —
  a reduction of more than 99 % because research showed harm at far lower
  levels than previously understood.

## Part 2 — Where PFAS Are Used (And Where They End Up)

### In products you own or use

| Product | PFAS used for | PFAS type | Exposure route |
|---|---|---|---|
| Non-stick cookware (PTFE/Teflon) | Coating release surface | PTFE (polymer, low mobility) + PFOA used in manufacture | Negligible at cooking temps; high if overheated >260°C |
| Waterproof clothing (Gore-Tex, etc.) | Durable water repellency (DWR) | PFAS-based DWR coatings | Skin contact; shed to wash water; leach to environment |
| Food packaging (pizza boxes, microwave popcorn bags, fast food wrappers) | Grease resistance | Short-chain PFAS | Migrate to food under heat; ingested |
| Carpets and furniture (Scotchgard) | Stain resistance | PFAS treatments | Dust ingestion; skin contact; outgassing indoors |
| Makeup and cosmetics (foundations, mascaras, sunscreens) | Texture, longevity | Various PFAS | Skin absorption; ingested from lipstick |
| Firefighting foam (AFFF) | Fire suppression | PFOS, PFOA, PFAS mixture | Groundwater contamination at airports, military bases, fire stations |
| Medical devices (implants, tubing, coatings) | Biocompatibility, chemical resistance | PTFE and others | Systemic from implant |
| Dental floss (some brands, Oral-B Glide) | Smoothness | PFAS coating | Oral absorption; environmental disposal |
| Ski wax | Low friction on snow | Fluorinated waxes | Melt water runoff; inhalation during application |
| Outdoor gear (tents, sleeping bags) | Water and stain resistance | DWR | Environmental shedding; skin contact |

### In industrial and commercial settings

- **Semiconductor manufacturing:** PFAS are used as etchants and cleaning
  agents in chip fabrication.  No current technical substitute for some steps.
- **Pharmaceutical manufacturing:** PFAS used in processing and as inactive
  ingredients in some drug formulations.
- **Aerospace and aviation:** PFAS coatings on components; hydraulic fluids.
- **Electroplating:** Used as mist suppressants in chrome plating.
- **Oil and gas:** PFAS used in drilling fluids and surfactants.
- **Mining:** Used in ore processing.

## Part 3 — Health Effects: What the Science Shows

### Established associations (strong evidence)

| Health outcome | PFAS associated | Evidence level |
|---|---|---|
| Kidney cancer | PFOA, PFOS | Strong; IARC Group 1 (carcinogenic) for PFOA since 2023 |
| Testicular cancer | PFOA, PFOS | Strong |
| Thyroid disruption | Multiple PFAS | Strong |
| Immune suppression | PFOS, PFOA, PFNA | Strong — reduced vaccine response in children |
| Elevated cholesterol | Multiple PFAS | Strong |
| Liver damage | PFOA, PFOS | Strong |
| Reduced fertility (both sexes) | Multiple PFAS | Moderate-strong |
| Pre-eclampsia in pregnancy | PFOA, PFOS | Moderate |
| Low birth weight | Multiple PFAS | Moderate |
| Developmental delays in children | PFOS, PFOA | Moderate |
| Ulcerative colitis | PFOA | Moderate |

### The dose problem

The dose-response relationship for PFAS is not linear.  Many effects occur
at very low concentrations — the EPA's revised health advisories reflect
this: PFOA is harmful at 0.004 parts per trillion, which is below the
detection limit of most analytical methods used before 2020.

This means **there is no established "safe" level** for PFOA and PFOS —
the EU and US regulatory positions are that any detectable exposure carries
some risk and the goal is elimination, not management.

## Part 4 — How PFAS Enter Recycling Streams

This is where the intersection with recycling becomes critical and dangerous.

### Route 1 — Paper and cardboard recycling

Food-service paper (pizza boxes, burger wrappers, coffee cups, microwave
containers) is treated with PFAS for grease resistance.  When this paper
enters the paper recycling stream:

1. During the pulping process, PFAS dissolve into the process water
2. PFAS-contaminated sludge is generated — often spread on agricultural
   land (direct soil contamination)
3. PFAS transfer into the recycled paper product — so "recycled" paper
   can contain PFAS from the original treated packaging
4. Process water PFAS eventually reaches waterways

**Result:** Paper recycling, without PFAS controls, becomes a vector for
spreading PFAS contamination from one product category (food packaging)
into an entirely different product category (office paper, tissue, packaging).

**Scale:** The paper industry processes billions of tonnes of potentially
PFAS-contaminated paper annually.  The contamination of recycled paper
streams with PFAS is documented and widespread.

### Route 2 — Textile recycling

Waterproof and stain-resistant clothing treated with PFAS-based DWR coatings
enters textile recycling.  Shredding and reprocessing releases PFAS into
process water and generates PFAS-contaminated fibres.

Workers at textile recycling facilities have been found to have elevated
PFAS blood levels.

### Route 3 — Compost contamination

Sewage sludge, sometimes used as an agricultural soil amendment, is
heavily contaminated with PFAS from all the consumer product sources above.
Biosolids from wastewater treatment plants applied to farmland have created
significant agricultural PFAS hotspots in multiple countries.

PFAS also enter compost from food packaging that was composted with food
waste.

### Route 4 — Plastics recycling

Some plastic packaging contains PFAS processing aids or barrier coatings.
When recycled mechanically, these PFAS migrate into the recycled plastic
pellet.  Research published in 2022–2024 found PFAS in recycled plastic
packaging at levels higher than in virgin plastic — because recycling
concentrates the contaminants from multiple sources.

## Part 5 — What Can Be Done: The Practical Reality

### The honest constraint

PFAS cannot be recycled away — they survive every recycling process.
They cannot be composted away.  They cannot be biodegraded.

There are currently only two proven methods for destroying PFAS:

**1. High-temperature incineration (>1,100°C with good combustion control)**
At sustained temperatures above 1,100°C with adequate oxygen and residence
time, PFAS molecules are broken down.  However:
- Most municipal waste incinerators do not sustain 1,100°C uniformly
- Incomplete combustion produces hazardous breakdown products
- Incinerator residues and scrubber water can still contain PFAS
- Requires specialist facility design

**2. Advanced electrochemical oxidation**
Emerging technology (not yet at full commercial scale) that uses
electrochemistry to break the C-F bond.  Several companies have pilot
plants operational as of 2025–2026.  Not yet cost-effective at scale.

**Other technologies in development:**
- Sonochemical destruction (ultrasound-driven radical reactions)
- Photocatalytic destruction (UV + catalysts)
- Bioremediation (engineered microorganisms — very limited strains shown
  to break C-F bonds; nascent field)
- Supercritical water oxidation (SCWO)

**None of these are widely available or affordable at community scale today.**

The practical implication: **the priority for PFAS is prevention and
containment, not recycling or destruction.**

## Part 6 — What to Do Now: The Prevention Hierarchy

### Tier 1 — Stop the input (most impactful)

**For individuals:**

1. **Replace non-stick cookware** with:
   - Cast iron (no PFAS; lasts decades; improves with use)
   - Carbon steel (same benefits as cast iron, lighter)
   - Stainless steel (requires more oil; no PFAS; fully recyclable)
   - Ceramic-coated (low-PFAS or PFAS-free; replace when coating chips)
   Note: Standard PTFE-coated non-stick is low-hazard at normal cooking
   temperatures but manufacturing uses PFAS and disposal puts PFAS
   into waste streams.

2. **Replace PFAS food packaging where possible:**
   - Avoid microwave popcorn bags — use a plain bag with loose kernels
   - Avoid fast food packaging when possible (or don't heat food in the wrapper)
   - Choose uncoated paper/cardboard packaging over grease-resistant coatings
   - Use reusable containers for takeaway where available

3. **Replace PFAS personal care products:**
   - Check cosmetics ingredient lists for "fluoro" — any ingredient with
     this prefix contains PFAS (PTFE, perfluorooctyl triacrylate, etc.)
   - Use the EWG Skin Deep database (ewg.org/skindeep) to check products
   - Look for "PFAS-free" labelling — increasingly available

4. **Replace PFAS textiles where possible:**
   - For waterproofing, choose PFC/PFAS-free DWR (available from Patagonia,
     Paramo, and others; look for "PFC-free" label)
   - Wash PFAS-coated clothing as infrequently as possible — washing is the
     primary route of environmental release
   - Do not tumble-dry PFAS-coated items — heat releases more PFAS

5. **Reduce indoor PFAS exposure:**
   - Use a HEPA vacuum and damp-mop hard floors — PFAS accumulate in dust
   - Ventilate during cooking, especially with non-stick cookware
   - Filter drinking water with a reverse osmosis system or activated carbon
     filter certified for PFAS removal (NSF/ANSI 58 for RO; 53 for carbon)
   - Replace stain-resistant carpet treatments when carpets are replaced

**For businesses:**

6. **Audit product specifications** for PFAS content — many manufacturers
   now provide declarations of absence; require them from suppliers.

7. **Substitute PFAS in food contact materials** — paper and board grease
   barriers can be replaced with starch-, protein-, or clay-based coatings.
   Several food manufacturers have completed transitions (McDonald's, Whole
   Foods, IKEA among others).

8. **Phase out AFFF firefighting foam** where not legally required —
   fluorine-free alternatives are available for most applications and are
   now required or recommended in many jurisdictions.

### Tier 2 — Contain what already exists

**For households:**

9. **Filter drinking water.**  Activated carbon filters (pitcher filters,
   under-sink units) certified to NSF 53 remove many PFAS.  Reverse osmosis
   (NSF 58) removes >90% of PFAS including short-chain compounds that carbon
   filters miss.

10. **Do not spread sewage sludge/biosolids on gardens.**  Many municipal
    biosolids are PFAS-contaminated.  Use certified compost instead.

11. **Dispose of PFAS-containing products appropriately:**
    - Non-stick cookware: municipal hazardous waste (not general recycling
      or landfill) — most councils accept small quantities
    - Old fire extinguishers (AFFF type): contact the manufacturer or
      specialist hazardous waste contractor
    - Gore-Tex and similar: many clothing retailers accept for specialist
      textile recycling that handles PFAS; do not put in general textile
      recycling

**For municipalities and water utilities:**

12. **Advanced water treatment:** Granular activated carbon (GAC) filtration
    is the current standard for PFAS removal from drinking water sources.
    Ion exchange resin is more effective for short-chain PFAS.  Both are
    being retrofitted to water treatment plants globally.

13. **Soil and groundwater remediation:** At contaminated sites (military
    bases, airports, industrial sites), PFAS removal from soil and groundwater
    is possible via pump-and-treat + advanced oxidation or activated carbon
    adsorption — but expensive ($50–$500M+ for large sites) and incomplete.

14. **Sludge management:** Thermal treatment of PFAS-contaminated sludge
    at high temperature (>1,100°C) before land application removes PFAS.
    Requires specialist facility.

### Tier 3 — Policy and legal action

15. **The Stockholm Convention** lists PFOS and PFOA as persistent organic
    pollutants for global elimination.  PFNA and PFHxS added 2022.
    Support national ratification and enforcement.

16. **EU restriction:** The EU proposed a universal restriction on all
    non-essential PFAS uses in 2023 (REACH restriction proposal) — the most
    comprehensive regulatory action globally.  Support equivalent legislation
    in your jurisdiction.

17. **Hold polluters accountable:** Major litigation is ongoing in the US
    (3M, DuPont/Chemours), EU, and Australia.  3M agreed to a $10.3B
    settlement in 2023 for drinking water contamination.  DuPont and
    Chemours settled for $1.18B in 2023.  These settlements fund remediation
    but do not address ongoing contamination.

18. **Right to know:** Demand PFAS disclosure in products.  Several US states
    have enacted ingredient disclosure laws for food packaging and cosmetics.
    Support federal equivalents.

## Part 7 — PFAS in Recycled Products: The Contamination Loop

This is the critical practical fact that most guides omit:

**Buying recycled paper does not guarantee you are avoiding PFAS.**  
**Buying recycled plastic does not guarantee you are avoiding PFAS.**

Research published in 2023 found PFAS in 65 % of recycled paper samples
and in a significant fraction of recycled plastic samples tested.
The concentrations were in some cases higher than in virgin materials.

Until the paper and plastic recycling industries implement PFAS testing
and segregation of PFAS-contaminated streams, recycled content in
food-contact materials should be treated with caution, particularly for:
- Children's products (higher PFAS sensitivity, higher ingestion from mouthing)
- Food-contact paper and board
- Drinking cups and food packaging made from recycled content
- Recycled plastic food containers

**This does not mean recycling is bad** — it means the industry needs to:
1. Test incoming streams for PFAS contamination
2. Segregate PFAS-contaminated feedstocks to appropriate treatment pathways
3. Not use PFAS-contaminated recyclate in food-contact products
4. Phase PFAS out of products that enter recycling streams

## Part 8 — PFAS Regulations by Country (Summary)

| Jurisdiction | PFOA status | PFOS status | General PFAS regulation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **EU** | Banned 2020 (some derogations) | Banned 2010 | Universal restriction proposed 2023; drinking water limit 0.1 µg/L total PFAS |
| **USA** | Phase-out since 2006; use ending | Phase-out since 2002; use ending | EPA MCL for PFOA/PFOS: 4 ppt (2024) |
| **UK** | Follows EU restrictions (as of exit) | Follows EU restrictions | Reviewing national PFAS strategy |
| **Australia** | Regulated under Industrial Chemicals Act | Regulated | PFAS National Environmental Management Plan |
| **Canada** | Listed as toxic under CEPA | Listed as toxic | Risk management measures |
| **China** | Restricted | Restricted | Implementing Stockholm obligations |
| **Japan** | Restricted | Restricted | Water quality standards |

## Summary: The Ten Things to Know About Forever Chemicals

1. PFAS are in virtually everyone's blood.  This is not theoretical.
2. They do not break down.  There is no natural process that removes them.
3. They are in your food, water, cookware, clothing, and cosmetics.
4. They cause cancer, immune suppression, thyroid disruption, and developmental harm.
5. There is no safe level — regulatory limits keep being lowered as research reveals harm at lower concentrations.
6. Recycling spreads them — PFAS-contaminated feedstocks contaminate recycled products.
7. Incineration at >1,100°C is the only proven destruction method currently available at scale.
8. Prevention is the only practical strategy available to individuals now.
9. Regulation is advancing — but lobbying has slowed it for 50 years.
10. The manufacturers knew about the hazards decades before the public did.  The litigation record makes this clear.
