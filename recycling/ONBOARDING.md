# Onboarding Guide — Starting a Recycling Initiative
## Step-by-Step Checklists for Every Scale

> **How to use this guide:** Find the scale that matches where you are
> starting.  Work through the checklist in order.  Each completed item
> unlocks the next.  Do not skip to a later step — the earlier ones are
> prerequisites, not suggestions.

**Author:** ThomasCory Walker-Pearson  
**Date:** April 2026  
**Version:** 1.0

## Scale A — Household / Individual

**Goal:** Achieve A-score ≥ 0.75 for your personal waste stream.  
**Time to achieve:** 1–2 weeks to establish habits; permanent thereafter.

### Week 1 Checklist

- [ ] **Find your local collection rules.**  
  Search "[your council name] recycling" or check the recycling label on
  your bin.  Write down or photograph the list of what is and is not accepted.
  Stick it somewhere visible (fridge, cupboard door).

- [ ] **Set up three streams.**  
  Dry recyclables / food and organic waste / residual.
  - Food caddy: any container with a lid, 5–10 litres, ideally near the
    kitchen bench.  Compostable liners make emptying easy.
  - Dry recycling: a bag or box placed close to where you generate it
    (kitchen, office desk, bathroom for cosmetics packaging).
  - Residual: your existing bin.

- [ ] **Learn the seven resin codes.**  
  Take 5 minutes with the resin code table in THERMOPLASTICS.md.
  Focus on knowing:  #1 and #2 = yes in almost all kerbside.
  #6 and #7 = no in almost all kerbside.  #3, #4, #5 = check locally.

- [ ] **Find your nearest specialist drop-off points:**
  - Soft plastics (LDPE bags and film): usually a supermarket
  - Batteries: usually a supermarket, DIY store, or electronics retailer
  - Small electronics: council recycling centre or electronics retailer
  - Medicines: pharmacy (never bin or flush)

### Week 2 Checklist

- [ ] **Implement rinse-before-recycling.**  
  One cold-water rinse for every container before it goes in the dry bin.
  Make it automatic — same as rinsing a plate before the dishwasher.

- [ ] **Audit your waste for one week.**  
  At the end of the week, before putting bins out, spend 5 minutes
  looking at what is in each bin.  Ask:
  - Is there anything in the residual bin that could have been recycled?
  - Is there anything in the dry recycling that should not be there?
  - Is there anything that could be repaired or donated rather than binned?

- [ ] **Identify your top three avoidable waste items.**  
  What appears repeatedly in your residual bin that you could eliminate
  with a simple substitution?  Common answers:
  - Coffee pods → reusable pod or cafetière
  - Cling film → beeswax wraps or reusable containers
  - Black plastic ready-meal trays → alternatives in other packaging
  - Single-use batteries → rechargeables

- [ ] **Set up a battery collection container.**  
  A small tin or jar for spent batteries.  Empty to drop-off point when full.
  Takes 10 seconds to set up; prevents batteries entering landfill permanently.

## Scale B — School

**Goal:** Achieve consistent source separation across classrooms, canteen,
offices, and outdoor areas.  Reduce landfill waste by ≥ 50%.  
**Time to achieve:** 4–8 weeks to set up; 1 term to embed in culture.

### Step 1 — Audit (Week 1)

- [ ] **Walk the school and count all bins.**  
  Note location, size, current labelling, current contamination.

- [ ] **Conduct a waste composition audit on general waste.**  
  Empty one or two general waste bags (with gloves) onto a tarp.  Sort
  contents into categories and estimate percentages by volume:
  - Food waste
  - Dry recyclables (could have been recycled)
  - True residual waste
  - Batteries / electronics (should be specialist stream)
  This gives your baseline.  A typical school before source separation:
  60–70% of landfill waste is either food or dry recyclables.

- [ ] **Identify your canteen and kitchen waste composition.**  
  Food waste is usually the largest single category and the easiest to
  divert to compost.

### Step 2 — Infrastructure (Weeks 2–3)

- [ ] **Design your bin station layout.**  
  Each location needs a matching set: food / dry recyclables / residual.
  Bins must be the same colour scheme at every station — visual consistency
  is critical for habit formation.

- [ ] **Order or repurpose bins.**  
  Budget: £5–15 per bin.  A school of 500 pupils typically needs
  15–25 bin stations.  Total bin cost: £300–£1,000.
  
  **Cost reduction:** Contact your waste contractor — many will supply
  recycling bins at no charge in exchange for the material.

- [ ] **Create signage with pictures only.**  
  No text required — pictures work for all ages and all literacy levels.
  Use large, clear images of accepted items on each bin lid.
  Print on A4, laminate, attach with cable ties.

- [ ] **Set up food waste collection.**  
  Options:
  - On-site composting (if space available — see IMPLEMENTATION_SPECS.md Tier 1F, 2D)
  - Council collection (many councils collect school food waste; call to confirm)
  - Commercial collection (food waste contractor — may be free if volume
    sufficient for biogas operator)

### Step 3 — Education (Weeks 3–4)

- [ ] **Deliver one assembly or class session on the three streams.**  
  Use GUIDE_FOR_EVERYONE.md Part 1 as the source.  Key messages:
  1. We throw away things that still have value
  2. Clean and sorted = recovered; dirty and mixed = landfill
  3. Here is our new system

- [ ] **Train every member of staff** (5-minute briefing at a staff meeting).  
  Staff are the most important habit models for pupils.  If a teacher
  puts a yoghurt pot in the wrong bin, a class of 30 sees it.

- [ ] **Create "bin monitor" roles in each class.**  
  Pupils aged 7+ can effectively monitor bin use and gently correct peers.
  This also creates ownership and investment in the system.

### Step 4 — Launch and Monitor (Weeks 4–8)

- [ ] **Formal launch day.**  
  Announce the new system; celebrate.  Consider a competition between
  classes for cleanest sorted waste stream in the first month.

- [ ] **Weekly spot-check** by a designated pupil or staff member:
  - Is food contamination in dry recycling below 5%?
  - Are batteries appearing in general waste?
  - Are bins being used correctly?
  
- [ ] **One-month repeat of the waste composition audit.**  
  Compare to baseline.  Communicate the improvement to the school community.
  (Example: "We diverted 120 kg of food waste from landfill last month.")

- [ ] **Set up electronics collection** for staff and families:
  - Collection box in reception for old phones, tablets, cables, batteries
  - Contact a certified e-waste recycler (see EWASTE_AND_BATTERIES.md Part 6)
    — many supply boxes free in exchange for collected material

## Scale C — Workplace / Small Business

**Goal:** Zero contamination in dry recycling stream; food waste to compost
or collection; paper stream protected from PFAS contamination.  
**Time to achieve:** 2–4 weeks.

### Step 1 — Assess (Days 1–3)

- [ ] **Meet with your current waste contractor.**  
  Ask:
  - What streams do they currently collect?
  - What contamination rates are they seeing in your dry recycling?
  - What is the cost per tonne for each stream?
  - Will they supply additional bins or collection bags?

- [ ] **Identify your specific waste streams:**
  - Office paper (high value if clean; contaminated by food = zero value)
  - Food waste from kitchen/canteen
  - IT equipment and batteries
  - Packaging from deliveries (corrugated cardboard + LDPE film)
  - Printer cartridges (manufacturer take-back schemes exist for all major brands)

- [ ] **Check for PFAS in your paper stream.**  
  If your office handles food-contact paper (catering, food service), be aware
  that food-service paper may be PFAS-treated and should be separated from
  clean office paper.  See FOREVER_CHEMICALS_PFAS.md Part 4.

### Step 2 — Set Up (Days 4–10)

- [ ] **Desk-side bins for paper only.**  
  Clean paper is your highest-value recyclable by weight.  A dedicated
  A4 paper tray at every desk prevents paper contamination.
  Collect separately from packaging.

- [ ] **Central bin stations** (food / dry / residual) in kitchen, print room,
  any area with mixed waste generation.  Same picture-based labelling as
  the school approach.

- [ ] **Cardboard collection point** — flat-packed boxes near delivery entrance.
  Cardboard bales have positive market value; your waste contractor may
  collect separately at reduced or zero cost.

- [ ] **IT asset register + disposal protocol:**
  - All IT equipment must go through a documented disposal process
  - Data destruction before any recycling or donation
  - Use an R2 or e-Stewards certified IT Asset Disposal (ITAD) company
  - Retain certificate of data destruction for GDPR compliance
  - All batteries removed and sent to battery collection before IT equipment
    goes to ITAD

### Step 3 — Supplier and Procurement Audit (Weeks 2–3)

- [ ] **Review packaging on all deliveries.**  
  For each key supplier: is packaging recyclable?  Could it be replaced
  with recyclable alternatives?  Many suppliers will shift to corrugated
  cardboard or kraft paper on request.

- [ ] **Review consumables:**
  - Switch from single-use plastic cups to reusable or compostable
  - Replace non-stick cookware in canteen (cast iron or stainless)
  - Replace plastic cling film with reusable alternatives

- [ ] **Printer cartridges:**  
  HP, Epson, Canon, Brother all operate take-back schemes.  Register with
  your brand's scheme — collection is free and reduces waste costs.

## Scale D — Community Group / Neighbourhood

**Goal:** Establish a community-level collection and processing system that
bridges the gap between household behaviour and municipal infrastructure.  
**Time to achieve:** 1–3 months to first operational event; 6–12 months to
regular operation.

### Phase 1 — Foundation (Month 1)

- [ ] **Identify your core group** — minimum 3–5 committed people.  
  Roles needed:
  - Coordinator (communicates, schedules, handles admin)
  - Materials expert (knows what goes where; can answer questions)
  - Venue contact (access to community hall, school, or market pitch)
  - Driver / logistics (if materials need transporting)

- [ ] **Map your material pathways before collecting anything.**  
  For each material type you plan to handle, answer:
  - What is your collection point?
  - Where does collected material go?  Who accepts it?  Do you need to book?
  - What condition does it need to be in?  (clean?  sorted?  minimum volume?)
  - Is there a cost or a payment?
  
  Do not collect materials you have no pathway for.  This is the most
  common failure mode for community recycling initiatives.

- [ ] **Find your key downstream contacts:**
  - Local authority waste officer (they want to hear from community groups;
    often have grant funding for community waste initiatives)
  - Regional recycler for your target materials
  - Charity refurbishers for working electronics
  - Local community garden or allotment for food waste / compost output

- [ ] **Set up a legal structure if handling money or equipment.**  
  Options: unincorporated association (simplest), Community Interest
  Company (limited liability, suitable for trading activities),
  charity registration (tax benefits, grant eligibility).
  Many community recycling groups begin as informal groups and formalise
  if and when they need to handle significant funds or equipment.

### Phase 2 — First Event (Month 1–2)

- [ ] **Run a take-back table event** (see IMPLEMENTATION_SPECS.md Tier 1D).  
  Target: one material stream for the first event (e.g., soft plastics
  only, or batteries only).  Simple scope = reliable execution = trust built.

- [ ] **Before the event:**
  - Confirm downstream pathway is ready to receive your material
  - Prepare clear labelling and an information sheet (one A4 page)
  - Brief all volunteers on what is accepted and why

- [ ] **At the event:**
  - Record: how many items collected, what types, any issues
  - Talk to participants: what other materials do they struggle to dispose of?
  - Hand out information about your next event / how to find you

- [ ] **After the event:**
  - Transport or arrange collection of materials within 48 hours
  - Write a short summary (numbers collected, materials, outcome)
  - Share with local community channels — social media, newsletter, noticeboard

### Phase 3 — Regular Operation (Months 3–6)

- [ ] **Establish a regular schedule** — monthly minimum.  
  Consistency is more important than frequency.  Participants need to know
  when they can come.  An irregular schedule means people give up and
  return to binning.

- [ ] **Expand material streams one at a time.**  
  Add a new material stream only when you have:
  - A confirmed downstream pathway
  - Sufficient volunteer capacity
  - Clear participant communication about what is and is not accepted

- [ ] **Connect with neighbouring groups** — avoid duplication;
  share transport costs; share material volumes to meet minimum quantities
  for collectors.

- [ ] **Apply for grant funding** if you need equipment:
  - Local authority community fund
  - National recycling / environment grants (check your country's
    environment agency website)
  - Supermarket community funds (many have small grants for local groups)
  - Crowdfunding for specific equipment items

### Phase 4 — Infrastructure (Months 6–12)

Once you have established regular operation with clean, sorted material
streams, you have the credibility and the feedstock to consider:

- [ ] **Fixed collection points** (a box or bin at a local shop or community
  hall, permanently sited)

- [ ] **Processing equipment** if volume justifies it
  (see IMPLEMENTATION_SPECS.md Tier 2)

- [ ] **Deposit-return trial** with a local retailer
  (see IMPLEMENTATION_SPECS.md Tier 1E)

- [ ] **Engagement with local authority** to formalise your role — some
  councils contract community groups to provide specialist collection
  services that supplement the kerbside scheme

## Scale E — Municipal / Regional Authority

**Goal:** System-level A-score ≥ 0.75 across all collected material streams.
Contamination rate ≤ 5% in dry recycling.  Separate food waste collection.
EPR compliance and reporting.

### Phase 1 — Baseline Assessment (Months 1–2)

- [ ] **Commission a waste composition analysis** of current residual and
  dry recycling streams.  A proper compositional analysis costs £5,000–£25,000
  depending on sampling size; it is the essential foundation for everything else.

- [ ] **Audit current MRF performance:**
  - Input tonnage per material type
  - Reject rate (material received but sent to landfill/incineration)
  - A-score per output stream (if measured; if not, start measuring)
  - Contamination rate

- [ ] **Map all existing collection contracts** — what is being collected,
  how frequently, at what cost, to where.

- [ ] **Identify the three largest improvement opportunities** from the data.
  Common findings:
  - Food waste heavily contaminating dry recycling (solution: separate collection)
  - High reject rate from MRF due to contamination (solution: source separation
    communication; deposit scheme for bottles)
  - Low electronics collection rate (solution: more drop-off points; retailer
    take-back enforcement)

### Phase 2 — Source Separation and Communication (Months 3–6)

- [ ] **Standardise bin colours and labels across the authority.**  
  Inconsistent labelling is the leading cause of household confusion.
  One clear, picture-based system, identical for every household.

- [ ] **Introduce kerbside food waste collection** if not already in place.  
  A separate food caddy + fortnightly or weekly food waste collection
  reduces dry recycling contamination dramatically and enables composting
  or anaerobic digestion of food waste.

- [ ] **Resident communication programme:**
  - What has changed and why
  - What is accepted in each bin with pictures
  - Where to take items not accepted at kerbside
  - Why contamination matters (the batch effect — one dirty item spoils
    a whole lorry load)

- [ ] **Set measurable targets:**
  - Contamination rate target (e.g., reduce from 15% to 5% within 12 months)
  - Food waste diversion rate (e.g., increase from 20% to 60% within 18 months)
  - Electronics collection rate (e.g., double collection points within 6 months)

### Phase 3 — Infrastructure Investment (Months 6–18)

- [ ] **Evaluate deposit-return scheme** — either implement locally, join a
  regional scheme, or formally advocate for national implementation.

- [ ] **Evaluate MRF upgrade or replacement** if current facility is below
  performance targets.  Modern NIR-equipped MRF can achieve 95%+ material
  purity from well-separated input.

- [ ] **Commission food waste processing infrastructure** — either on-site
  anaerobic digester or contract with regional facility.

- [ ] **Set up e-waste and battery collection network:**
  - Fixed collection points at council facilities and libraries
  - Retailer take-back enforcement (coordinate with trading standards)
  - Bulky e-waste collection slots

- [ ] **Implement EPR data collection** — track material by type, quantity,
  and A-score for EPR reporting compliance.

### Phase 4 — Reporting and Continuous Improvement

- [ ] **Annual public A-score report** — not just tonnes diverted but quality
  of recovered material.  This is the accountability mechanism.

- [ ] **Annual waste composition re-survey** to measure progress against
  baseline.

- [ ] **Contractor performance monitoring** tied to A-score outputs, not
  just collection tonnage.

- [ ] **Engage with EPR scheme operators** — as EPR fees become more
  widespread, the authority's role shifts from funder of recycling to
  recipient of producer-funded recycling revenue.

## Common Failure Modes and How to Avoid Them

| Failure mode | What happens | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| Collecting material with no pathway | Warehouse fills; material goes to landfill; participants lose trust | Map ALL downstream pathways before first collection |
| Inconsistent bin labelling | Contamination rises; effort is wasted | Standardise labelling before launch; never change it without communication |
| Volunteer burnout | Initiative collapses | Share responsibilities; rotate roles; thank people; celebrate milestones |
| Mission creep (trying to do everything) | Nothing done well | Start with one material, one event, one route.  Scale when stable. |
| Counting tonnes rather than A-score | "Success" that is actually entropy laundering | From the start, record and report quality, not just quantity |
| Starting too big | Infrastructure without feedstock; economics collapse | Start at the smallest viable scale; grow with demand |
| No communication to participants | People stop coming; no trust built | Regular updates via every available channel |
