# E-Waste and Batteries — Full Guide
## Electronics, Batteries, and Hazardous Consumer Technology

> **Who this is for:** Anyone who owns a phone, laptop, TV, smoke detector,
> power tool, cordless toothbrush, EV, or any device with a battery.
> That is everyone.

**Author:** ThomasCory Walker-Pearson  
**Date:** April 2026

## Why E-Waste Is Different from Plastic

Plastic is primarily a contamination and sorting problem.  
E-waste is primarily a **toxicity, hazard, and precious-material extraction** problem.

A typical smartphone contains:
- **60+ elements** from the periodic table
- **Gold, silver, palladium, platinum** (precious metals, recoverable)
- **Cobalt, lithium, nickel** (critical battery minerals, severely supply-constrained)
- **Copper, tin, tantalum** (electronic function metals)
- **Lead, mercury, cadmium, arsenic, beryllium** (hazardous, must not enter landfill)
- **Brominated flame retardants** in circuit boards (toxic combustion products if incinerated)

Sending a phone to landfill does two things simultaneously:
1. **Loses** the precious and critical minerals forever
2. **Releases** the hazardous materials slowly into soil and groundwater

Both are serious.  Together they are unacceptable.

## Part 1 — The E-Waste Crisis in Numbers

- **62 million tonnes** of e-waste generated globally in 2022 — the fastest-growing
  waste stream in the world, growing at ~2.5 million tonnes per year.
- **Only 22 %** formally collected and recycled.  The rest: dumped, burned in
  informal processing, or sent to landfill.
- **$91 billion** worth of recoverable materials in the 2022 global e-waste stream
  — gold alone accounts for ~$20 billion.
- **40–50 times** more gold per tonne in circuit boards than in gold ore.
- Informal burning of circuit boards for copper recovery releases dioxins, furans,
  and brominated compounds — some of the most toxic substances on Earth.

## Part 2 — The Seven Categories of E-Waste

The UN defines six categories; this guide uses seven for clarity.

### Category 1 — Temperature exchange equipment
Air conditioners, refrigerators, freezers, heat pumps.

**Hazard:** Refrigerants (HFCs, HCFCs, CFCs) — potent greenhouse gases
(GWP 1,000–10,000× CO₂) and, in older units, ozone-depleting substances.
**Rule:** Never crush or puncture.  Refrigerant must be recovered by a
licensed technician before any other processing.  This is law in the EU,
US, Australia, and most jurisdictions.

**Where to take it:** Local council bulk waste collection; appliance retailer
take-back (legally required in EU, increasingly required elsewhere); licensed
white goods recycler.

---

### Category 2 — Screens and monitors
TVs, computer monitors, tablets, phones, laptops.

**Hazard:** Older CRT screens contain 1–4 kg of **lead** in the glass.
Fluorescent backlit LCDs contain **mercury**.  Modern LED-backlit screens
are less hazardous but contain indium (rare, valuable) in the ITO layer.

**Value:** The precious metals in circuit boards, the glass, aluminium,
and copper are all recoverable.

**Rule:** Never break CRT screens — the lead dust is immediately hazardous.
Never incinerate fluorescent backlights — mercury vapour.

**Where to take it:** Electronics retailer take-back; specialist e-waste
collector; municipal hazardous waste facility.

---

### Category 3 — Lamps and lighting
Fluorescent tubes, compact fluorescents (CFLs), mercury vapour lamps, LEDs.

**Hazard:** Fluorescent and CFL lamps contain **mercury** — typically
3–5 mg per lamp, but collectively significant given global volumes.
One broken CFL requires careful ventilation; a broken box of 50 in a
small room is a genuine health hazard.

**LEDs** contain no mercury but contain gallium, indium, and rare earth
elements in the phosphors — valuable and recoverable.

**Rule:** Never put fluorescent or CFL lamps in household recycling bins.
Never put them in general waste bags — breakage releases mercury.

**Where to take it:** Dedicated lamp recycling collection points at
electrical retailers (legally required in EU, available in most countries).

**DIY safe handling if a CFL breaks:**
1. Ventilate the room immediately — open windows, leave for 15 minutes.
2. Do not vacuum (spreads mercury particles).
3. Damp-wipe surfaces; collect fragments in a sealed bag.
4. Take bag to household hazardous waste facility.

---

### Category 4 — Large equipment
Washing machines, dryers, dishwashers, electric cookers, large industrial machines.

**Hazard:** PCBs (polychlorinated biphenyls) in transformers and capacitors
in older equipment (pre-1980s manufacture).  Asbestos insulation in older
ovens and boilers.  Freon in older refrigerated appliances.

**Value:** Steel, copper, aluminium — high volume, established recycling
markets.  Electric motors are particularly valuable (copper windings).

**Where to take it:** Local authority bulky waste collection; appliance
retailer take-back; scrap metal dealer (confirm they handle electrical
components correctly).

---

### Category 5 — Small equipment
Toasters, kettles, phones, cameras, power tools, toys with electronics,
medical devices, small appliances.

**This is the category most people get wrong.**

Most of these items go into general household waste because people don't
know there's an alternative, or find take-back inconvenient.  Yet they
contain the highest concentrations of precious metals, critical minerals,
and hazardous substances per kilogram of any e-waste category.

**The phone in your drawer:**
The global stockpile of unused mobile phones is estimated at **5 billion units**.
Each phone contains approximately:
- 0.034 g gold
- 0.34 g silver
- 0.015 g palladium
- 9 g copper
- Various rare earth elements

At scale: 5 billion phones × 0.034 g gold = **170 tonnes of gold** sitting
in drawers and landfills.  At $60,000/kg: $10.2 billion in gold alone.

**Where to take it:**
- Mobile phone retailers (legally required to accept old phones in EU, UK;
  widely available elsewhere)
- Dedicated e-waste collection points at councils and recycling centres
- Mail-in schemes operated by manufacturers (Apple GiveBack, Samsung
  trade-in programmes, etc.)
- Charity refurbishers (if device is working): phones, laptops, tablets
  are desperately needed in developing-world education programmes

---

### Category 6 — IT and telecoms equipment
Computers, servers, printers, network equipment, hard drives.

**Data security note:** Before any disposal or recycling, ensure data is
properly destroyed.
- For hard drives: physical shredding (professional services available,
  certificate of destruction issued) or certified data wiping
- For SSDs: physical destruction is more reliable than wiping
- For phones: factory reset + remove SIM and memory card
- For printers: clear memory (check manufacturer guide — many store print
  jobs internally)

**Legal requirement:** In many jurisdictions (EU GDPR, US state laws)
failure to properly destroy personal data before disposal is a legal
violation, not just a security risk.

**Value:** Servers and business IT equipment are high-value for precious
metals and are often refurbishable — a working server has orders of
magnitude more value than its scrap metal.

**Where to take it:**
- IT asset disposal (ITAD) companies — provide data destruction
  certificates, often offset cost through material value
- Manufacturer take-back programmes (HP, Dell, Lenovo all operate these)
- Certified e-waste recyclers (look for R2, e-Stewards certification)

## Part 3 — Batteries: A Complete Guide

Batteries are the single most hazardous, most valuable, and most widely
mishandled e-waste subcategory.  They also represent the material foundation
of the entire clean energy transition.

### 3A — Why batteries are critical and hazardous simultaneously

**Critical:** The clean energy transition depends on lithium, cobalt,
nickel, and manganese.  These elements are geographically concentrated
(lithium: Chile/Australia/Argentina; cobalt: Democratic Republic of Congo),
politically sensitive, and in growing supply constraint.  Recycling them
from end-of-life batteries is the primary alternative to new mining.

**Hazardous:**
- Lithium-ion batteries contain flammable electrolyte.  If punctured,
  short-circuited, or thermally stressed they can **thermal runaway** —
  a self-sustaining, extremely hot (up to 700°C) exothermic reaction
  that is difficult to extinguish and releases toxic hydrogen fluoride
  gas.
- Lead-acid batteries contain **concentrated sulphuric acid** and lead
  — both immediately hazardous.
- Nickel-cadmium batteries contain **cadmium**, a highly toxic heavy
  metal that accumulates in soil and the food chain.

**Most landfill fires that persist and are difficult to extinguish involve
lithium-ion batteries.**  This is not a rare event — it is a growing
cause of fires at recycling facilities globally as battery disposal into
general waste increases.

---

### 3B — Battery types: recognition, hazard, and recycling route

#### Lithium-ion (Li-ion) / Lithium polymer (LiPo)
**Found in:** Phones, laptops, tablets, e-cigarettes, e-bikes, electric
vehicles, power tools, earbuds, anything rechargeable.

**Recognition:** Usually labelled Li-ion, LiPo, Li-Pol.  Flat or
cylindrical cells.  May say "18650" (cylindrical standard size) or
"21700".

**Hazard level:** HIGH.  Thermal runaway risk if damaged, punctured, or
overcharged.

**Do not:**
- Put in household recycling bins (causes fires at MRFs)
- Put in general waste bins
- Crush, puncture, or disassemble
- Store damaged or swollen batteries — a swollen battery is near failure

**If a battery is swollen:**
1. Do not charge it.
2. Place in a metal container (tin can with sand) or fire-proof bag.
3. Take to a specialist recycler or battery collection point immediately.

**Do:**
- Take to dedicated battery collection points (supermarkets, DIY stores,
  electronics retailers — all legally required to accept in EU/UK)
- Return entire device to manufacturer take-back
- For EVs and e-bikes: contact dealer or manufacturer — these require
  specialist logistics

**What happens when recycled correctly:**
Hydrometallurgical processing recovers lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese
at >90% purity.  These become battery-grade materials for new cells.
This is the most important closed loop in the clean energy transition.

#### Lead-acid (Pb-acid)
**Found in:** Car batteries, motorcycle batteries, UPS systems, some
mobility scooters.

**Recognition:** Heavy, large.  Usually labelled "LEAD ACID" or "Pb".
Vent caps on top.

**Hazard level:** HIGH.  Sulphuric acid electrolyte.  Lead (toxic metal).

**Do not:**
- Tip on its side — acid can spill
- Put near heat sources
- Disassemble

**Do:**
- Take to any car parts retailer (most accept old car batteries free —
  it is legally required and they have value)
- Scrap metal dealer (lead-acid has positive scrap value)
- Council household hazardous waste

**What happens when recycled correctly:**
Lead-acid is the most recycled product in the world by percentage — 97–99%
recycling rate in most developed countries because:
1. The lead has clear economic value
2. The battery designs are standardised
3. Collection networks are well established (car parts supply chain)

This is the template for how battery recycling should work for all types.

#### Nickel-cadmium (NiCd)
**Found in:** Older cordless power tools, older cordless phones, some
emergency lighting, older portable electronics.

**Recognition:** Labelled NiCd.  Cylindrical cells (similar size to
Li-ion but heavier).

**Hazard level:** HIGH (cadmium).  Cadmium is a Group 1 carcinogen.
NiCd batteries are banned from sale in the EU for most consumer
applications (replaced by NiMH or Li-ion) but billions remain in use.

**Do:** Same battery collection routes as above.

#### Nickel metal hydride (NiMH)
**Found in:** AA and AAA rechargeable batteries, hybrid car battery packs
(Toyota Prius early models), some power tools.

**Hazard level:** LOW compared to other chemistries.  Still must not
go to landfill — nickel is toxic and recoverable.

**Do:** Battery collection points; retail take-back.

#### Alkaline (AA, AAA, C, D, 9V disposable)
**Found in:** Remote controls, torches, clocks, toys.

**Hazard level:** LOW.  No heavy metals in modern alkaline batteries
(manganese, zinc — both recoverable and relatively benign).

**Legal situation:** In many jurisdictions alkaline batteries can legally
go in general waste, but in EU they must be separately collected.

**Best practice:** Collect in a small container and take to a battery
collection point when full.  Prevents landfill of zinc and manganese.

#### Button/coin cells (CR2032, etc.)
**Found in:** Watches, hearing aids, car key fobs, medical devices, calculators.

**Hazard level:** MEDIUM-HIGH.  Lithium button cells (CR series) are
a significant child safety hazard — if swallowed they can cause
chemical burns within 2 hours.  Keep away from children and store
securely.

Mercury button cells (now largely phased out but still found in old
stock) contain mercury.

**Do:** Collect separately; take to any battery collection point.

#### EV / large format battery packs
**Found in:** Electric cars, electric buses, stationary energy storage.

**Hazard level:** VERY HIGH if mishandled.  Enormous energy storage.
High-voltage (400–800V DC).  Thermal runaway of a large pack is a major
emergency.

**This is specialist equipment — do not attempt DIY handling.**

**Second life before recycling:** Many EV battery packs retain 70–80% of
their original capacity when the vehicle reaches end of life (usually
at 100,000–200,000 km).  This residual capacity makes them suitable for:
- Stationary energy storage (homes, commercial buildings, grid services)
- Second-life EV programmes (reused in lower-demand applications)
Second-life deployment typically extends pack life by 5–10 years before
hydromet recycling.

**Recycling process:**
1. Discharge to safe residual voltage (specialist equipment)
2. Manual disassembly (semi-automated lines being developed)
3. Cell-level sorting by chemistry and condition
4. Pyrometallurgical pre-treatment to remove organics
5. Hydrometallurgical recovery of Li, Co, Ni, Mn

### 3C — Battery collection: where to go in your country

**European Union:** Any retailer selling batteries must accept used batteries
at point of sale, free of charge, regardless of brand.  Dedicated battery
bins at supermarkets, DIY stores, electronics retailers.  Municipal
collection points.

**United Kingdom:** Same retailer take-back obligation (Battery Regulations
2009, extended).  Most large supermarkets, councils, and retailers have
collection boxes.

**United States:** No federal mandate but most states have requirements.
Call2Recycle (call2recycle.org) operates 16,000+ collection locations
nationally.  Many big-box retailers participate.

**Australia:** B-cycle scheme (b-cycle.com.au) — nationwide collection
at participating retailers and councils.  Launched 2022.

**Canada:** Call2Recycle (call2recycle.ca) — 6,500+ drop-off locations.

**Global directory:** Recycle Your Electrics (recycleyourelectrics.org)
maintains a country-by-country directory.

## Part 4 — The Refurbishment Path (Before Recycling)

**Before any device is recycled, ask: could it be repaired or refurbished?**

The most resource-efficient outcome for electronics is always:
1. **Repair** (extend life; no new material needed)
2. **Reuse / resell** (second owner; amortises manufacturing impact)
3. **Refurbish** (restore function; charity or second-hand market)
4. **Recycle** (recover materials; manufacturing impact only partially offset)
5. **Landfill / incineration** (worst; all manufacturing impact wasted)

### Right to Repair

The EU Right to Repair Directive (2024) requires manufacturers of
large appliances, TVs, and phones to:
- Make spare parts available for at least 10 years after sale
- Provide repair documentation
- Not design products to prevent repair

Similar legislation is advancing in the US (state level), UK, and Australia.

**What you can do today:**
- For phones and laptops: iFixit (ifixit.com) provides free repair guides
  and sells spare parts for hundreds of models
- For appliances: Repair Café network (repaircafe.org) — 2,500+ volunteer
  repair events in 35 countries
- For any device: Spare parts are available via manufacturer websites,
  eBay, AliExpress, and specialist suppliers

**Economic case:** Repairing a cracked phone screen typically costs $50–$150.
Buying a new equivalent phone: $300–$1,500.  The repair is almost always
the economically rational choice, quite apart from the environmental one.

## Part 5 — What You Can Do Right Now

### Immediate (zero cost):

1. **Do not put any battery in a general waste bin.**  Find your nearest
   battery collection point at batterybox.co.uk (UK), call2recycle.org (US/CA),
   or your local council website.

2. **Find your nearest e-waste collection point** at recyclenow.com (UK),
   epa.gov/recycle (US), or your local council.

3. **Check your junk drawer and loft** for unused phones, tablets, chargers.
   Box them up and take them to a collection point or charity refurbisher.

4. **Label your battery storage** — keep a small labelled container for
   spent batteries so they are collected and not accidentally binned.

### Short-term (small effort):

5. **Register your appliances** — manufacturer registration enables recall
   notifications and sometimes qualifies for repair support.

6. **Buy rechargeable where possible** — NiMH or Li-ion rechargeable AA/AAA
   batteries have 500–1,000 charge cycles.  A set of 4 replaces 2,000–4,000
   disposable batteries over their life.

7. **When buying new electronics**, check repairability ratings:
   - iFixit repairability scores (0–10, higher = more repairable)
   - EU Energy Label (includes repairability from 2021)
   - Fairphone (phones designed explicitly for user repair, modular, 10-year
     software support)

### Community and policy:

8. **Support Right to Repair legislation** in your jurisdiction.

9. **Ask retailers where their e-waste goes** — many manufacturers have
   commitments; some are meaningful, some are not.  Ask for specifics:
   what percentage of collected devices are refurbished vs. shredded?
   What is the recovery rate for cobalt and lithium from their recyclers?

10. **Set up an e-waste collection point** at your school, workplace, or
    community group — contact a certified e-waste recycler who can supply
    collection boxes at no charge in exchange for the material.

## Part 6 — Certified Recyclers: What to Look For

Not all e-waste "recycling" is genuine.  A significant fraction of
collected e-waste is exported to developing countries where informal
processing — manual disassembly, open burning of circuit boards for copper,
acid baths without protective equipment — causes severe health impacts on
workers and communities.

**Certifications to look for:**

| Certification | What it covers | Where recognised |
|---|---|---|
| **R2 (Responsible Recycling)** | Facility standards, downstream tracking, data security, worker safety | US, global |
| **e-Stewards** | Higher standard than R2; prohibits export to developing countries | US, global |
| **WEEE compliance** | EU Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment regulations compliance | EU |
| **ISO 14001** | Environmental management system (process standard, not outcome guarantee) | Global |
| **BS 8887** | Design for manufacture, assembly, disassembly, end-of-life | UK |

**Red flags in an e-waste recycler:**
- No certification
- No data destruction documentation
- Unwilling to specify downstream processing partners
- Primarily advertising "export for reuse" without monitoring
- No chain-of-custody documentation

**Finding certified recyclers:**
- R2 directory: sustainableelectronics.org/r2-certified-facilities
- e-Stewards directory: e-stewards.org/find-a-recycler
- UK WEEE registered: environment.data.gov.uk/your-environment/compliance

## Summary: The Three Rules for E-Waste

1. **Never bin it.** No battery, no screen, no circuit board, no appliance
   belongs in a general waste bin or landfill.  Ever.  The hazards are real
   and the lost value is enormous.

2. **Repair before recycling.** The embodied energy and materials in a
   device are far greater than the recovered value from recycling it.
   Every year of extended use is the best possible environmental outcome.

3. **Use certified routes.** The difference between a certified recycler
   and an uncertified one is the difference between materials recovered
   safely and workers being poisoned while burning your circuit boards
   in an open field.  Certification matters.
