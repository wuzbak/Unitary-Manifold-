# A Plain-English Guide to Fixing Recycling

> **Who this is for:** Anyone who has ever put something in a bin and wondered
> whether it actually gets recycled.  No science background required.

**Author:** ThomasCory Walker-Pearson  
**Date:** April 2026  
**Version:** 1.0

---

## Part 1 — Why Most Recycling Doesn't Work

### The honest picture

Globally, around **9 % of all plastic ever made has been genuinely recycled.**
The rest has been landfilled, incinerated, or leaked into the environment.
Even items that are "collected for recycling" frequently end up in landfill
because the collected material is too contaminated to process economically.

This is not primarily because people don't care.  It is because **the system
is designed incorrectly** — and once you understand exactly how it fails,
fixing it becomes straightforward.

---

### The five failures explained simply

#### Failure 1 — We measure the wrong thing

Every government report on recycling leads with **tonnes diverted from
landfill**.  This metric lies.

A lorry full of mixed, food-contaminated plastic that arrives at a sorting
facility and is sent to landfill anyway was still "collected for recycling" —
so it counts positively in the statistics.  Contaminated material that gets
processed into very low-grade pellets that nobody can use also counts.

**What we should measure instead: quality recovered, not mass collected.**

Think of it like money.  If someone owes you £1,000 and pays you back with
£950 in monopoly money plus £50 real cash, they have not paid you back.
They have paid you £50.  The current system counts "£1,000 exchanged" and
calls it a success.

The correct measure is called the **alignment score** (A-score):

```
A-score = quality of recovered material / quality of original material
```

| A-score | Plain English |
|---|---|
| 1.0 | Material came back exactly as good as it left. Fully repaid. |
| 0.7 – 0.95 | Good recycling. Most value returned. Acceptable. |
| 0.4 – 0.7 | Downcycling. Mass recovered; quality lost. Half the value gone. |
| 0.0 – 0.4 | Waste in disguise. Nearly worthless recovered material. |
| 0.0 | Landfill or burning. Nothing returned. |

**The global average A-score today is approximately 0.3 – 0.4.**  
We are repaying less than half of what we owe.

#### Failure 2 — Contamination destroys entire batches

When food-dirty bottles mix with clean ones on a sorting belt, it is not
just the dirty bottle that is lost.  The residue spreads.  Optical sensors
that can normally distinguish plastic types cannot distinguish them when the
surfaces are coated in tomato sauce or milk residue.

The result: **one contaminated item in a batch can cause the whole batch to
fail quality standards and be sent to landfill.**

Sorting machines are sophisticated.  Contamination defeats them not through
mechanical failure but because the material genuinely becomes indistinguishable
from waste once the surface signal is masked.  The machines are working
correctly — they are correctly identifying that the contaminated batch is no
longer high-quality material.

**The fix is not better machines.  The fix is clean input.**

A clean bottle sorted at home is worth more to the recycling system than
any amount of improved sorting technology.

---

#### Failure 3 — Mechanical recycling has a quality limit

Every time plastic is mechanically recycled — shredded, melted, re-formed —
the polymer chains get shorter.  They degrade.  The material becomes weaker,
more brittle, and less pure.

After **one** mechanical recycling pass, most plastics are at 75–85 % of their
original quality.  After **five** passes they are at 40–55 %.  After **ten**
they are essentially useless as structural material.

This means mechanical recycling is not a loop — it is a **declining spiral**.
Each cycle extracts some value and discards the rest.

Industry rarely discloses how many times a "recycled content" product has
been cycled.  A product labelled "made from 100 % recycled plastic" could
be made from material on its sixth mechanical cycle — quality at roughly
40 % of virgin.

**What is needed for a genuine loop: chemical recycling back to basic
building blocks** (monomers), which resets the quality to near-virgin.
Chemical recycling is more expensive and energy-intensive than mechanical
recycling, but it is the only path to infinite, closed-loop recovery.

---

#### Failure 4 — The wrong people pay for waste

When a manufacturer designs packaging that cannot be recycled — multi-layer
films, black plastic that optical sensors cannot read, mixed-material laminates
glued together — they face no direct financial cost for that choice.

The cost falls on:
- **Municipal councils** that have to collect and pay for disposal
- **Taxpayers** who fund those councils
- **The environment** when disposal fails

The manufacturer has already been paid.  They have no incentive to design
differently.

This is the fundamental economic failure.  **The person who creates the
problem is not the person who pays for it.**

The solution — Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) — requires producers
to fund the end-of-life management of their products.  The higher the quality
of their product's recovery, the lower their payment.  The more unrecyclable
their packaging, the higher their payment.  It turns recyclability from
a cost (because it requires better design) into a profit motive (because
it reduces the EPR levy).

Thirty-plus countries have EPR laws.  The ones that work — Germany, Sweden,
South Korea, Belgium — charge fees tied to **actual recycling quality**, not
just weight placed on market.

---

#### Failure 5 — Returns require incentives

Research is unambiguous: voluntary return of packaging without a financial
incentive achieves 20–40 % return rates.  Deposit-return schemes — where
you pay a small deposit on a beverage container and get it back when you
return the empty — achieve **85–95 % return rates in every country where
they are well implemented**.

The economics are straightforward.  The deposit is the explicit financial
value of the container's recyclability.  Without it, that value is invisible
to the consumer.  With it, that value becomes a reason to act.

Germany's deposit system (Pfand) achieves 98 % return for PET bottles.
Norway achieves 97 %.  Both started within a decade of implementation.
Neither required public campaigns, recycling education, or infrastructure
subsidies beyond the deposit mechanism itself.

**The deposit scheme works because it prices the thing correctly.**

## Part 2 — The Solutions, Explained Simply

### Solution 1 — Separate at source (the most important step)

**What:** Use separate bins for different material types.  The minimum useful
separation is three streams:

1. **Dry recyclables** — clean plastic, glass, metal, paper, cardboard
2. **Organic waste** — food scraps, garden waste
3. **Residual waste** — everything else

**Why it matters more than anything else:**  Every step downstream — sorting
machines, chemical plants, composting facilities — works dramatically better
when the input material is already sorted.  Mixing organic waste with dry
recyclables contaminates both streams simultaneously.  Separating them at
the moment of disposal costs nothing beyond a second bin.

**Common objection:** "We don't have space for multiple bins."

**Answer:** A 10-litre caddy under the kitchen sink for food waste is 25 cm ×
25 cm × 30 cm.  A recycling bag hung on the inside of a cupboard door.
These are not significant space commitments.

---

### Solution 2 — Rinse before you recycle

**What:** Before placing a container in the recycling bin, rinse out food
residue with cold water.  It takes 5–10 seconds.

**Why:** One tablespoon of yoghurt left in a pot is enough to contaminate
20–30 plastic items around it on the sorting belt, making the whole batch
potentially unsortable.  The cost-benefit is extreme: 10 seconds of your
time saves 20–30 items from landfill.

**Common objection:** "But washing things uses water and energy."

**Answer:** A cold-water rinse uses roughly 200 ml of water and negligible
energy.  Manufacturing virgin plastic from fossil fuel uses 50–100 litres
of water per kilogram of plastic.  The calculation is not close.

---

### Solution 3 — Return what you can

**What:** Use every available return scheme.  Bottle deposits.  Electronics
take-back.  Shop return points for batteries and light bulbs.  Clothing return
schemes at retailers.

**Why:** The infrastructure for closed-loop recovery — where the material
genuinely comes back to the manufacturer and is turned back into the same
product — cannot exist without a reliable supply of returned material.
Deposit and take-back schemes are the mechanism that creates that supply.

**What to look for:**
- The recycling triangle with a number (1–7) on plastic containers — this
  tells you what type of plastic it is.  Type 1 (PET) and type 2 (HDPE) have
  the best-developed recycling infrastructure in most countries.  Type 3–7
  are harder; look for specialist collection points.
- Electronics retailers offering collection of old devices.  In the EU this
  is a legal obligation; retailers must accept end-of-life devices regardless
  of brand.
- Clothing brands offering return or repair services.

---

### Solution 4 — Demand better design from producers

**What:** Buy from companies that publish clear recycling information and use
mono-material packaging (one type of material, not a laminate of several).
Avoid black plastic packaging (cannot be read by NIR sensors).  Avoid
multi-layer film pouches (almost never recyclable).

**Why:** Your purchasing decision is the most direct signal to producers that
recyclability matters.  Companies respond to purchasing data faster than to
regulation.

**How to check:**
- Look for "mono-material" on packaging
- Check the resin code triangle — one number means one material; multiple
  codes on the same item mean layers you cannot separate
- Look for "made with X % genuinely recycled content" along with a number
  for what recycling cycle (first cycle? third cycle?)

---

### Solution 5 — Support deposit-return legislation

**What:** Write to your representatives.  Sign petitions.  Support
organisations campaigning for deposit-return legislation in your jurisdiction.

**Why:** Individual action raises A-scores at the household level.  System
change — deposit-return, EPR fees, chemical recycling infrastructure mandates
— raises A-scores at the national level.  Both are necessary.  Neither alone
is sufficient.

The political barrier to deposit-return is primarily industry lobbying by
beverage manufacturers and retailers who do not want to operate the return
logistics.  The technical and economic case for deposit-return is settled —
every country that has implemented it well has achieved high return rates
within five years.

## Part 3 — The Five Numbers to Know

These five numbers summarise the state of recycling in any system.  Ask for
them from any company or council claiming recycling success.

| Number | What it measures | What a good score looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **A-score** | Quality of recovered material vs. original quality | ≥ 0.75 for mechanical recycling; ≥ 0.95 for chemical |
| **Return rate** | Fraction of products actually collected at end of life | ≥ 85 % with deposit; ≥ 50 % without |
| **Contamination rate** | Fraction of collected material too contaminated to process | ≤ 5 % for a well-run scheme |
| **Cycle count** | Average number of times "recycled content" has been recycled | Should be reported; ≤ 3 for mechanical, unlimited for chemical |
| **COP** | Energy recovered ÷ energy put in to the recycling process | Must be > 1 to be genuine recycling, not entropy-laundering |

No current system publicly reports all five.  The ones that come closest —
Germany, Sweden, Finland — have the best real-world outcomes.

## Part 4 — What Each Person Can Do Right Now

### Zero budget, immediate impact

1. Separate dry recyclables from food waste.
2. Rinse containers before recycling.
3. Check the resin code number; know what your local system accepts.
4. Return batteries, electronics, and light bulbs to collection points.
5. Refuse black plastic packaging and multi-layer film pouches at the point
   of purchase whenever possible.

### Small investment ($5–50), within a month

6. Buy a small food-waste caddy for the kitchen bench.
7. Put a labelled bag or box in a convenient place for dry recyclables —
   closer to where you use things means better habit formation.
8. Find your nearest specialist collection points for items your kerbside
   scheme does not accept (soft plastics, polystyrene, textiles).

### Community action, any scale

9. Set up a take-back table at a market, school, or community event.
10. Organise a neighbourhood group to understand local kerbside rules and
    share correct separation practice.
11. Contact your local council and ask for A-score or contamination rate
    data from the local Materials Recovery Facility.
12. Advocate for a deposit-return scheme if your jurisdiction lacks one.

## Part 5 — The Honest Long-Term Picture

Fixing recycling fully requires action at three levels simultaneously:

**Level 1 — Individual behaviour:** Clean sorting at source.  High-quality
input to every downstream process.  This is achievable today and is the
single highest-leverage action per person per hour.

**Level 2 — System design:** Deposit-return schemes.  EPR fees tied to
genuine A-score outcomes, not mass statistics.  Standardised resin codes
and collection rules across jurisdictions.  Chemical recycling infrastructure
for materials that cannot be mechanically recycled cleanly.  This requires
policy and industry investment.

**Level 3 — Product design:** Mono-material packaging.  Design for
disassembly in electronics.  No black plastic.  No multi-layer laminates
except where functionally irreplaceable.  This requires purchasing pressure
and, ideally, regulatory standards.

None of these three levels can substitute for the others.  Individual
behaviour without system design means clean material sent to contaminated
batches and wasted.  System design without individual behaviour means
well-designed infrastructure running on poor-quality input.  Product design
without the other two means recyclable products that never get recycled.

The good news: all three are known, tested, and working in various places
already.  This is not an unsolved problem.  It is an **implementation problem**
— and the implementation specifications are in the next document.
