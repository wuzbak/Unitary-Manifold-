# Recycling Implementation Specifications
## Realistic Budgets · Available Materials · Human-Scale Skills

> **Purpose:** This document gives everyone — a household, a school, a
> community group, a small business, or a regional authority — everything
> they need to implement a functioning recycling scheme at their scale.
> Each tier is self-contained.  Find your tier and start there.

| Your situation | Go to |

| Household, school, small group | Tier 1 — Zero Budget |
| Community group with a small budget | Tier 1+ — Community Upgrades |
| Workshop, makerspace, small business | Tier 2 — Small Capital |

0.0 = landfill equivalent.

## Tier 1 — Zero Budget ($0 per household)

These require no equipment, no money, and no technical skills.
They are also the highest-leverage interventions in this document because
they raise the quality of input to every downstream process.

---

### 1A — Three-stream separation at source

**Principle:** Separate dry recyclables / food waste / residual waste
at the moment of disposal, before anything else.

**What you need:**
- Two or three receptacles per household (bags, boxes, bins — anything)
- A consistent colour code matching your local kerbside scheme
- A printed reference card showing what goes where

**Reference card template (print, laminate, stick to bin lid):**

```
GREEN / FOOD CADDY        BLUE / DRY RECYCLING       BLACK / RESIDUAL
──────────────────        ────────────────────        ────────────────
All food scraps           Clean plastic bottles       Everything else
Fruit & veg peel          Glass bottles & jars        Nappies / nappies
Cooked food               Metal tins & cans           Crisp packets
Coffee grounds            Paper & cardboard           Black plastic
Tea bags (paper)          Cardboard boxes             Polystyrene
                          *** RINSE FIRST ***         Foil trays (dirty)
```

**A-score impact:** Separating food from dry recyclables is the single
largest quality improvement available.  It raises the dry-stream A-score
from 0.35–0.45 (mixed) to 0.65–0.80 (clean, separated).

**Time investment:** 30 seconds per household per day.

**Failure modes and fixes:**

| Failure | Why it happens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Wrong items in dry bin | Confusion about what is accepted | Replace text card with pictures only |
| Food caddy smells | Too infrequent emptying | Empty every 2–3 days; use compostable liner |
| Family members don't sort | No shared understanding | One 10-minute conversation with pictures |

### 1B — Rinse before recycling

**Principle:** Remove food residue with a cold-water rinse before recycling.

**Five-step protocol:**

1. Empty the container completely (scrape residue into food caddy)
2. Add a small amount of cold water
3. Swirl 5–10 seconds
4. Tip out rinse water
5. Place container upside down to drain, then into dry recycling

**What does NOT need rinsing:** Cardboard, paper, already-clean glass.

**What MUST be rinsed:** Containers that held food, drink, dairy, sauce,
oil, cosmetics.

**A-score impact:** Clean containers in a sorted stream achieve A-score
0.75–0.85 at the sorting facility.  Unrinsed containers in the same stream
reduce the whole-batch A-score to 0.40–0.55 through cross-contamination.

---

### 1C — Know your resin codes

Every plastic container has a triangle with a number (1–7).

| Code | Name | Common uses | Widely recycled? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 PET** | Polyethylene terephthalate | Drinks bottles, food trays | Yes — best infrastructure |
| **2 HDPE** | High-density polyethylene | Milk bottles, shampoo | Yes — most kerbside |
| **3 PVC** | Polyvinyl chloride | Pipes, window frames | Rarely — specialist only |
| **4 LDPE** | Low-density polyethylene | Bags, cling film, pouches | Limited — soft plastics collection |
| **5 PP** | Polypropylene | Yoghurt pots, bottle caps, food boxes | Growing — check locally |
| **6 PS** | Polystyrene | Foam packaging, coffee cups | Rarely — almost nowhere |
| **7 Other** | Mixed / multilayer / bioplastics | Barrier films, some bottles | Almost never |

**Rule of thumb:** #1 and #2 almost always have a route.  #6 and #7 almost
never do.  #3, #4, #5 depend on your area — check your council website.

## Tier 1+ — Community Upgrades ($50–$500 total)

### 1D — Manual take-back table

**What it is:** A table at a community event, school, or market where people
bring clean, dry containers.  A volunteer sorts them on the spot into
clearly labelled bags by resin code.

**What you need:**
- One folding table (~$30 new, free from a community hall)
- Seven clear LDPE bags labelled 1–7 (~$5)
- A resin code reference poster (print free from any recycling authority)
- One or two volunteers for a 2-hour session

**Total cost:** $35–$50

**Output:** Clean, sorted, labelled bags of single-resin plastic.
This is the same function as a Materials Recovery Facility sorting line —
done manually.

**Where does the sorted material go?**
- Resin #1 (PET) and #2 (HDPE): many regions have commercial collectors
  who will pick up or accept sorted bales at no charge because they have
  real market value
- Resin #5 (PP): growing network of collectors
- Resin #4 (LDPE, soft plastics): REDcycle and equivalent schemes in
  supermarkets in many countries
- Resin #6 (PS): specialist waste contractors; may require a collection fee

**A-score:** Clean, sorted, single-resin material = A-score 0.80–0.90.
This is better than almost any kerbside-collected mixed-stream material.

### 1E — Simple deposit-return for a local shop or school canteen

**What it is:** A retailer or canteen offers a small refund (10–25 cents/pence)
for every clean container returned to a collection point in the shop.

**What you need:**
- A locked collection bin (wooden crate with padlock, ~$30)
- Labels identifying accepted container types
- A simple accounting entry: deposit received at purchase, refunded on return
- A collection arrangement for full bins (your local recycler, or connection
  to one of the routes above)

**Total cost:** $30–$60 setup.  Net cash cost: near zero.  The deposit is
paid by the customer and returned on container return; the operator holds
the float on unreturned containers, which covers collection costs.

**Expected return rate:** 60–80 % in small-scale pilots.
(National schemes with good infrastructure: 85–98 %.)

**A-score:** Returned containers are typically rinsed at point of return and
sorted on the spot = A-score 0.82–0.92.

### 1F — Small anaerobic digester for food waste

**What it is:** A sealed tank where food waste decomposes anaerobically,
producing biogas (mostly methane) for cooking, and digestate (liquid
fertiliser) for gardens.

**Build materials and sources:**

| Item | Source | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| 1000 L IBC tote (intermediate bulk container) | Agricultural suppliers, eBay, Gumtree | $50–$100 used |
| Modified lid fittings | Plumbing merchant | $20–$30 |
| Gas collection bag (inner tube or LDPE bag) | Bicycle shop / builder's merchant | $5–$15 |
| Low-pressure flexible hose to stove | Hardware store | $10–$20 |
| Stove jet enlargement (to run on low-pressure biogas) | Drill bit 0.5 mm larger than existing jet | $5 |

**Total cost:** $90–$170

**Build complexity:** Basic plumbing — push-fit fittings, no welding.
A person who can fit a new tap can build this in one day.

**Capacity and output:**
- Feedstock: 3–7 kg food waste per day
- Biogas output: 0.3–0.7 m³/day
- Equivalent cooking energy: 45–90 minutes on a standard burner
- Digestate: 1–3 litres/day of high-nitrogen liquid fertiliser

**Operating requirements:**
- Keep temperature above 15°C for reliable digestion (above 35°C for
  faster "mesophilic" operation — a sunny location or simple insulation)
- Do not add meat in large quantities until system is established
- Stir or agitate weekly to prevent crust formation
- Check pH monthly — target 6.8–7.5 (add sodium bicarbonate if acidic)

**What this achieves:** Food waste in landfill generates methane
uncontrolled (the worst outcome — methane is 28x more potent greenhouse
gas than CO2 over 100 years, and it generates zero useful energy).  The
anaerobic digester captures that methane as useful cooking energy and
converts the remaining nutrients back to productive use in soil.

These require modest capital and basic technical skills — welding,

**Total material cost:** $790–$1,330

(first cycle from virgin-quality input).

### 2D — Community composting system

**What it is:** Centralised composting for food and garden waste from a
community of 20–200 households.  Range of build complexity from simple
to in-vessel.

**Option A — Three-bay open bay composter (simplest)**

Materials:
- Twelve 2.4 m posts (treated softwood or recycled plastic lumber)
- 12 m of 1 m high weld mesh or pallet wood
- Corrugated metal or polycarbonate roof panels

Cost: $200–$500 in materials, one day to build with 2 people and basic
carpentry tools.

Capacity: Each 1.2 m x 1.2 m bay handles approximately 50–100 kg/week
of mixed green and brown waste.

Process: Fill Bay 1 for 4–6 weeks.  Turn to Bay 2.  Wait 8–12 weeks.
Turn to Bay 3 for final maturation 4–8 weeks.  Finished compost from Bay 3.

**Option B — In-vessel tumbler (medium, faster, less odour)**

Materials:
- 200 L steel drum with lid
- Axle shaft and bearings to allow rotation
- Frame from angle iron

Cost: $100–$300.  One person with basic metalwork skills, one day.

Throughput: 20–30 kg/week.  Compost ready in 3–6 weeks (vs. 3–6 months
for static bay).

**What you get:** Finished compost worth $0.15–$0.50/kg as a soil amendment.
For 50 kg/week throughput: $400–$1,300/year value.  More importantly:
organic waste diverted from landfill, where it generates methane without
producing any useful material.

### 2E — Small-scale biochar retort

**What it is:** A simple kiln that converts woody garden waste (branches,
hedge cuttings, straw) into biochar — a highly stable form of carbon that
improves soil structure and locks carbon for hundreds to thousands of years.

**The barrel-in-barrel design (proven, open-source):**

Outer barrel (200 L steel drum): combustion zone.
Inner barrel (100–120 L steel drum): feedstock zone, oxygen-starved pyrolysis.

Materials:
- Two nested steel drums with lids ($30–$80 for a pair, or free from
  industrial surplus)
- Steel pipe nipples for gas outlet from inner to outer barrel
- Basic hand tools for cutting and fitting

Cost: $30–$120

Build time: 4–8 hours for a competent person.

**Process:**
1. Fill inner barrel with dry woody material (max 20 % moisture)
2. Seal inner barrel with lid; connect gas outlet pipe to outer barrel
3. Light fire in outer barrel using kindling
4. The heat from outer barrel drives pyrolysis in inner barrel
5. Pyrolysis gases vent to outer barrel and combust (self-sustaining after
   30 minutes)
6. Run 2–3 hours until gas flow stops
7. Cool completely (do not open hot — risk of char re-igniting in air)
8. Crush and apply biochar to soil at 0.5–2 kg/m2 per year

**Output:** 15–25 % by weight of input as biochar.  For 100 kg woody waste:
15–25 kg biochar.

**Carbon value:** One tonne of biochar contains 700–850 kg locked carbon.
On voluntary carbon markets this has value of $30–$200/tonne CO2-equivalent.
At small scale the direct soil-improvement benefit is more significant.

## Tier 3 — Medium Capital ($150,000–$2,000,000)

These require project management skills, modest engineering expertise,
and commercial-scale procurement.  They are appropriate for a municipality,
a regional cooperative, or a social enterprise.

---

### 3A — PET bottle wash and flake line

**What it is:** A semi-automated line that takes collected clean PET bottles
(ideally from a deposit-return scheme) and outputs food-grade rPET flakes
ready for re-pelletising and use in new packaging.

**Line configuration (500–1,500 kg/hour capacity):**

| Stage | Function | Equipment | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 Bale breaker | Open incoming bales | Conveyor + trommel | $15,000–$30,000 |
| 2 Manual pre-sort | Remove non-PET, metals, large contaminants | 4-person sorting cabin | $10,000–$25,000 |
| 3 Label remover | Friction-strip paper and sleeve labels | Friction washer unit | $15,000–$40,000 |
| 4 Metal detection | Remove ferrous and non-ferrous metal | Belt magnet + eddy-current | $8,000–$20,000 |
| 5 Granulator | Shred bottles to 10–15 mm flake | 75 kW granulator | $30,000–$80,000 |
| 6 Float-sink tank | Separate PET (sinks) from PP/HDPE caps (floats) | 4 m tank + screw conveyor | $10,000–$25,000 |
| 7 Hot wash | 90°C, 2% caustic NaOH, 15-minute dwell | Jacketed stainless tank | $25,000–$60,000 |
| 8 Rinse | Clean water rinse, pH neutral | Rinse tank + centrifuge | $15,000–$35,000 |
| 9 NIR colour sorter | Separate clear/green/amber flake | NIR optical sorter | $40,000–$100,000 |
| 10 Dryer | Reduce moisture below 0.5% for pelletising | Crystalliser + dryer | $20,000–$50,000 |
| 11 Conveyors and structure | — | Steel conveyors, platforms | $15,000–$40,000 |

**Total line cost:** $203,000–$505,000

**Staffing:** 4–8 FTE operators.  Skills required: industrial machinery
operation, basic maintenance, chemical handling for NaOH.  No chemistry
degree required.  Typical supplier training: 2–3 days on-site.

**A-score output:** Clean clear PET flake, IV 0.72–0.78 dL/g = A-score
0.75–0.88.  Suitable for non-food-contact packaging, strapping, polyester
fibre.

**Upgrade to food contact (additional):**

Add Solid State Polycondensation (SSP) reactor to restore intrinsic
viscosity to food-grade levels (IV ≥ 0.80 dL/g):
- Equipment cost: $80,000–$180,000
- A-score with SSP: 0.90–0.98
- Certifications required: FDA (US), EFSA (EU), or equivalent

**Payback calculation (example at 500 kg/hour, 6,000 hours/year):**

- Input volume: 3,000 tonnes/year PET bales
- Input cost: $200–$400/tonne = $600k–$1.2M/year
- rPET flake output: ~2,400 tonnes/year (20% yield loss to labels, caps,
  moisture, losses)
- Output revenue: $400–$700/tonne for non-food rPET flake = $960k–$1.68M/year
- Operating costs (labour, energy, chemicals, maintenance): $400k–$700k/year
- Net margin: $260k–$780k/year
- Payback period: 1–2 years at median assumptions

### 3C — Centralised food waste biogas plant

**What it is:** An industrial anaerobic digester that processes food waste
from 500–5,000 households, producing biomethane for grid injection or CHP
(combined heat and power), plus digestate for agricultural use.

**Key equipment:**

| Item | Scale | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-treatment (screening, depackaging) | 5–50 tonnes/day | $50,000–$200,000 |
| Digester vessel (thermophilic, 55°C) | 500–5,000 m3 | $150,000–$800,000 |
| Biogas upgrading (biomethane for grid) | — | $300,000–$1,500,000 |
| OR CHP unit (electricity + heat) | 100–500 kWe | $100,000–$500,000 |
| Digestate management | — | $30,000–$150,000 |

**Total plant cost:** $630,000–$3,150,000 (wide range by scale and
configuration)

**For a 1,000-household community (realistic scope for a municipal project):**
- Food waste generated: ~1.5 tonnes/day (1.5 kg/household/day)
- Biogas yield: ~200 m3/day at 60% CH4
- CHP output: ~500 kWh/day electricity + 600 kWh/day heat
- Annual electricity value at $0.15/kWh: $27,375
- Annual heat value at $0.08/kWh: $17,520
- Digestate value: $5,000–$15,000/year
- Annual revenue: ~$50,000–$60,000

At this scale a CHP unit ($200,000–$400,000) is more cost-effective than
grid biomethane injection.  Payback: 4–8 years.  Eligible for various
renewable energy subsidies in most jurisdictions.

### 3D — Community chemical recycling pilot (PET glycolysis)

**What it is:** A small glycolysis reactor that converts post-consumer PET
back to BHET (bis-hydroxyethyl terephthalate) monomer, which can be
repolymerised to virgin-quality PET — genuine closed-loop recycling.

**Why glycolysis for a pilot:**
- No solvent toxicity issues (ethylene glycol — common, safe to handle)
- Operates at 180–220°C (no exotic temperatures)
- Equipment is standard industrial glass-lined or stainless vessel
- Output is the same BHET monomer used in virgin PET synthesis

**Equipment:**
- 500 L glass-lined reactor with agitator and condenser: $40,000–$120,000
- Heating system (thermal oil or electric): $15,000–$40,000
- Distillation column for glycol recovery: $20,000–$60,000
- Filtration (for insoluble contaminants): $10,000–$30,000
- Analytical equipment (IV, colour measurement): $15,000–$30,000

**Total pilot cost:** $100,000–$280,000 for 50–200 kg/hour capacity

**A-score output:** Glycolysis-route rPET monomer = A-score 0.90–0.98.
This is genuine closed-loop recovery — the material is back at monomer level.

**Operating conditions:**
- Temperature: 190–200°C
- Catalyst: zinc acetate or sodium carbonate (both safe, cheap)
- Glycol:PET ratio: 2:1 by mass
- Reaction time: 3–5 hours
- Required input: clean, sorted PET flake only (contamination stops reaction)

**Caution:** This is a chemical process requiring qualified operators,
correct safety training, and appropriate permitting.  Contact your
regional environmental and chemical safety authority before commissioning.

## What to Do First: The Decision Tree

```
Do you have organised source separation (3 streams)?
  NO  →  Start with Tier 1A today.  Nothing else matters until this is done.
  YES →

Is collected material being rinsed before recycling?
  NO  →  Implement Tier 1B.  Two minutes, near-zero cost.
  YES →

Is there a deposit-return scheme in your area?
  NO  →  Advocate for one (Tier 1E at local scale; policy campaign at regional).
  YES →

Is there a market for sorted, clean materials in your area?
  NO  →  Build Tier 1D (take-back table) to aggregate and route manually.
  YES →

Do you have a workshop and $2,000–$40,000?
  YES →  Tier 2 machinery opens local manufacturing from recyclate.
  NO  →

Do you have municipal authority and $150,000+?
  YES →  Tier 3 professional processing infrastructure.
  NO  →  Partner with neighbouring authorities; share Tier 3 infrastructure.
```
