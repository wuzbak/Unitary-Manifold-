## Regional Reality Check — What Wins in Duvall

The PNW does not reward the same crops that thrive in hot continental climates. In Duvall, the big pressures are usually:

- **winter wet feet**
- **fungal disease from humidity and dense growth**
- **summer drought after shallow spring rooting**
- **slugs, snails, and browsing wildlife**
- **heavy soil compaction**

So the most reliable crops usually share several traits:

- deep or persistent root systems
- tolerance for cool springs
- ability to survive partial neglect after establishment
- multi-season harvest value
- ability to work inside polycultures rather than isolated rows

**Three working rules for this region:**

1. **Drainage before fertility.** Many failures blamed on "bad luck" are actually root rot.
2. **Mulch before irrigation expansion.** A mulched acre behaves differently from a bare acre.
3. **Perennials first, annual insurance second.** Build the skeleton, then add seasonal muscle.

## Regional Data Snapshot

Using western Washington gardening guidance and regional climate references, Duvall is best approached as:

- **zone:** generally **8b**, with colder pockets
- **annual precipitation:** roughly **46-50 inches**
- **dry season:** usually **July-August**, often extending into September
- **typical frost-free window:** about **150-180 days**
- **planting implication:** warm-season crops should usually wait until true spring warmth; perennial crowns should be protected from winter saturation

## Quick Site Prescription for Duvall

Before planting anything on the lists below, treat the site in one of four categories:

| Site type | Typical Duvall problem | Best response |
|-----------|------------------------|---------------|
| **Heavy flat clay** | Winter waterlogging; summer cracking | Broadfork, compost, mulch, raised berms, avoid crowns sitting in cold water |
| **South / west dry slope** | Summer drought, reflected heat | Deep mulch, establish with drip, favor hazelnut, goumi, aronia, herbs, amaranth |
| **Woodland edge** | Lower light, root competition, fungal humidity | Use currants, gooseberries, evergreen huckleberry, salal, Caucasian spinach |
| **Seep / wet margin** | Standing water in winter-spring | Use wapato, salmonberry, elderberry, camas in appropriate microsites |

# LIST A — Backbone Long-Lived, Low-Fuss Food Crops for Duvall

These are the crops I would use to build the permanent skeleton of a PNW survival garden.

## A1 · Hazelnut / Filbert (*Corylus avellana* hybrids; disease-resistant cultivars)

- **Why it belongs:** One of the best long-term PNW staple crops for calories, fats, protein, and storage.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to light shade; pH 6.0-7.5; well-drained loam or clay loam with organic matter; moderate summer water during establishment; zone 5-8.
- **Guide:** Plant at least two compatible pollination partners unless using proven self-fertile selections. Space generously for airflow. Mulch heavily, keep grass away from the root zone, and water deeply through the first 2-3 summers. For western Washington, prioritize **eastern filbert blight-resistant cultivars** such as *Jefferson*, *Yamhill*, *Dorris*, *Wepster*, and compatible resistant pollen-producing cultivars.
- **Longevity / yield:** 30+ years; stable annual nut production once mature.
- **Pests / diseases:** Eastern filbert blight is the major disease pressure; use resistant cultivars and prune for airflow. Squirrels and jays will compete hard for the crop.
- **Best guild partners:** Currants, gooseberries, clover, comfrey, chives, yarrow, daffodils on margins for vole discouragement.

## A2 · Black Currant / Red Currant (*Ribes nigrum*, *R. rubrum*)

- **Why it belongs:** Reliable small-fruit crop with strong cold tolerance; black currants are excellent vitamin C and anthocyanin plants.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to half shade; pH 6.0-6.8; rich moisture-retentive but drained soil; zone 3-8.
- **Guide:** Great under open hazelnut or on east-facing edges. Feed yearly with compost. Prune out oldest wood on a rotating basis to keep young fruiting stems coming.
- **Longevity / yield:** 10-20+ years.
- **Pests / diseases:** Powdery mildew, aphids, currant fruit fly, and birds. Avoid stagnant humid thickets.
- **Best guild partners:** Hazelnut, gooseberry, chives, thyme, strawberries, white clover.

## A3 · Gooseberry / Jostaberry (*Ribes uva-crispa*, *Ribes* hybrid)

- **Why it belongs:** Tough, productive berry shrub that tolerates some shade and cool weather better than many mainstream fruits.
- **Ideal conditions:** Sun to part shade; pH 6.0-6.8; evenly moist soil; zone 3-8.
- **Guide:** Plant where summer light is good but roots stay cool. Mulch deeply. Jostaberries are often easier and less thorny than classic gooseberries.
- **Longevity / yield:** 15+ years.
- **Pests / diseases:** Mildew, sawfly larvae, birds. Open pruning matters.
- **Best guild partners:** Currants, chives, sorrel, yarrow, calendula.

## A4 · Elderberry (*Sambucus nigra* ssp. *canadensis* / adapted cultivars)

- **Why it belongs:** Heavy crops, strong medicinal value, good habitat function, and excellent adaptation to the moist PNW.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to partial shade; pH 5.5-7.5; moisture-retentive soil; tolerates wetter winter ground better than many fruits; zone 3-9.
- **Guide:** Plant as a shrub-row, wet-edge anchor, or wildlife hedge. Coppice or renewal-prune to manage size and stimulate strong fruiting wood.
- **Longevity / yield:** 20+ years.
- **Pests / diseases:** Birds are the biggest issue. Cane borers and mildew can appear in neglected dense growth.
- **Best guild partners:** Mint family herbs on edges, clover, yarrow, camas nearby in moist sites, N-fixing shrubs if space allows.

## A5 · Asparagus (*Asparagus officinalis*)

- **Why it belongs:** Classic perennial vegetable that returns every spring when annual beds are barely awake.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 6.5-7.5; deep, well-drained soil; zone 3-8.
- **Guide:** Drainage is critical. Use raised beds or ridged rows if your soil stays cold and wet. Do not harvest heavily for the first 2 years after planting crowns. Let ferns mature yearly to recharge roots.
- **Longevity / yield:** 15-25 years.
- **Pests / diseases:** Asparagus beetles, rust, Fusarium in stressed or soggy ground.
- **Best guild partners:** Strawberries at margins, parsley, dill, calendula, shallow-rooted alliums.

## A6 · Rhubarb (*Rheum rhabarbarum*)

- **Why it belongs:** One of the easiest and longest-lasting cool-climate perennial food plants.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to light shade; pH 6.0-6.8; fertile deep soil; regular moisture without standing water; zone 3-8.
- **Guide:** Plant crowns high enough that winter wet does not sit directly in the bud. Top-dress annually with compost. Divide old crowns if productivity declines.
- **Longevity / yield:** 10-20+ years.
- **Pests / diseases:** Crown rot in saturated soils; occasional slugs on young growth.
- **Best guild partners:** Chives, strawberries, comfrey, yarrow, spring bulbs on edges.

## A7 · Jerusalem Artichoke / Sunchoke (*Helianthus tuberosus*)

- **Why it belongs:** One of the most resilient calorie crops for the region; tolerates drought, cold, mediocre soil, and partial neglect.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 5.5-7.5; average to decent soil; zone 3-9.
- **Guide:** Give it its own patch or boundary because it spreads. Harvest after frost for sweeter tubers. Leave some tubers in the ground to maintain the stand.
- **Longevity / yield:** Essentially permanent once established.
- **Pests / diseases:** Very few serious issues; slugs can hit young shoots; voles may take tubers.
- **Best guild partners:** Walking onion, comfrey, clover, yarrow, pollinator flowers.

## A8 · Egyptian Walking Onion / Welsh Onion (*Allium × proliferum*, *Allium fistulosum*)

- **Why it belongs:** Perennial onion greens with near-zero drama; highly useful in daily kitchen use.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to half shade; pH 6.0-7.0; drained fertile soil; zone 3-9.
- **Guide:** Plant clumps around fruit shrubs, path edges, and annual beds. Divide periodically. Walking onions can naturalize into a useful defensive ring.
- **Longevity / yield:** Decades.
- **Pests / diseases:** Onion maggot, rust, and rot mostly under poor drainage or crowding.
- **Best guild partners:** Currants, gooseberries, strawberries, brassicas, rhubarb.

## A9 · Sorrel (*Rumex acetosa* and related species)

- **Why it belongs:** A hardy, early, nearly foolproof green when little else is producing.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to part shade; pH 5.5-7.0; average fertile soil; regular spring moisture; zone 4-9.
- **Guide:** Harvest outer leaves continuously. Cut flowering stalks if leaf production is the priority.
- **Longevity / yield:** 8-10+ years with occasional division.
- **Pests / diseases:** Minimal; slugs on young leaves; bitterness rises in heat or drought.
- **Best guild partners:** Currants, gooseberries, strawberries, chives.

## A10 · Sea Kale (*Crambe maritima*)

- **Why it belongs:** A remarkable perennial brassica with edible shoots, leaves, flower buds, and occasional roots.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 6.5-8.0; sharply drained soil; tolerant of poor sandy or gravelly ground; zone 4-8.
- **Guide:** In heavy PNW soils, mound the planting area or use a gravelly raised bed. Do not let winter water sit around the crown.
- **Longevity / yield:** 10+ years.
- **Pests / diseases:** Flea beetles, slugs, cabbage pests, crown losses in wet soil.
- **Best guild partners:** Thyme, chives, calendula, yarrow, low clovers kept back from crown.

## A11 · Haskap / Honeyberry (*Lonicera caerulea*)

- **Why it belongs:** An early-bearing cool-climate berry with excellent hardiness and good fit for maritime northern conditions.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to light shade; pH 5.5-7.0; moist but well-drained soil; zone 2-8.
- **Guide:** Plant two compatible cultivars for pollination. Mulch heavily. Best where roots stay cool and moisture remains even.
- **Longevity / yield:** 20+ years.
- **Pests / diseases:** Birds are the main problem; netting may be needed because fruit ripens early.
- **Best guild partners:** Strawberries, chives, clover, yarrow, spring flowering pollinator plants.

## A12 · Serviceberry / Saskatoon (*Amelanchier alnifolia* and relatives)

- **Why it belongs:** Fruiting shrub / small tree that combines toughness, wildlife value, and quality berries.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to part shade; pH 6.0-7.5; adaptable soil with good drainage; zone 2-9.
- **Guide:** Useful in hedgerows and mixed native-edible systems. Prune lightly for form and renewal.
- **Longevity / yield:** 20+ years.
- **Pests / diseases:** Cedar-apple rust in some contexts, birds, occasional fire blight.
- **Best guild partners:** Native strawberries, clover, yarrow, nodding onion, currants.

# LIST B — Alternate and Non-Standard Crops Worth Serious Trial

These are not gimmicks. They are the second-wave crops for growers who want resilience, diversity, and less dependence on mainstream grocery assumptions.

## B1 · Good King Henry (*Blitum bonus-henricus*)

- **Why it belongs:** A classic perennial spinach substitute for cool climates.
- **Ideal conditions:** Sun to part shade; pH 6.0-7.5; fertile moisture-retentive soil; zone 3-9.
- **Guide:** Slow to establish but durable once settled. Harvest leaves moderately and allow crowns to bulk up.
- **Pests / diseases:** Slugs and crown stress in compacted wet soil.
- **Best guild partners:** Sorrel, chives, currants, strawberries.

## B2 · Caucasian Spinach / Hablitzia (*Hablitzia tamnoides*)

- **Why it belongs:** One of the best shade-tolerant perennial greens for woodland edge systems.
- **Ideal conditions:** Part shade to bright shade; rich humus soil; pH 6.0-7.0; zone 3-9.
- **Guide:** Give it a trellis or shrub to scramble through. Excellent for north edges and fruit-tree understories.
- **Pests / diseases:** Slugs on spring growth; otherwise generally easy.
- **Best guild partners:** Currants, gooseberries, native strawberries, woodland herbs.

## B3 · Daubenton's Kale / Perennial Kale

- **Why it belongs:** Almost endless leafy production in mild climates without yearly reseeding.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to light shade; pH 6.0-7.5; fertile drained soil; zone 7-10, with better performance in protected microclimates.
- **Guide:** Propagate by cuttings. Protect from standing winter water and severe cold snaps. Feed modestly but regularly.
- **Pests / diseases:** Slugs, cabbage worms, aphids, clubroot in poorly rotated diseased soils.
- **Best guild partners:** Chives, dill, calendula, nasturtium as trap crop, thyme on margins.

## B4 · Skirret (*Sium sisarum*)

- **Why it belongs:** Historic perennial root crop with sweet clustered roots and very low attention needs.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to part shade; pH 6.0-7.0; fertile moisture-retentive soil; zone 5-9.
- **Guide:** Better in cooler sites than many exotic tubers. Harvest older crowns in late autumn and replant side shoots.
- **Pests / diseases:** Root competition, slugs, occasional rot if planted too low in wet soils.
- **Best guild partners:** Chives, sorrel, calendula, currants.

## B5 · Groundnut / Apios (*Apios americana*)

- **Why it belongs:** Nitrogen-fixing edible tuber vine; one of the more intriguing backup staple crops.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to part shade; pH 5.5-7.0; moisture-retentive soil; zone 3-8.
- **Guide:** Needs vertical support and patience. Yields improve over time. Best in moist but not stagnant ground.
- **Pests / diseases:** Rodents may dig tubers; vines can tangle if unmanaged.
- **Best guild partners:** Shrubs or trellis supports, elderberry edge, clover, wet-margin species.

## B6 · Yacon (*Smallanthus sonchifolius*)

- **Why it belongs:** Large sweet storage tuber and edible crisp roots; useful carbohydrate crop with good summer biomass.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 6.0-7.0; fertile loose soil; frost-free summer growth period; zone 8 with winter crown protection or lift-and-store.
- **Guide:** Treat as a lift-and-replant perennial in colder pockets. Mulch heavily and start early indoors if needed.
- **Pests / diseases:** Slugs on young shoots; crown rot in wet cold storage soil.
- **Best guild partners:** Calendula, alliums, basil, low clovers.

## B7 · Oca (*Oxalis tuberosa*)

- **Why it belongs:** An Andean tuber crop with good cool-season appeal and strong novelty value.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 5.5-7.0; loose soil; even moisture; long season; frost-sensitive tops.
- **Guide:** Best for experimentation in sheltered warm microclimates. Mulch and start early. Tubers bulk late.
- **Pests / diseases:** Slugs, tuber predation by rodents, poor yields in short cool summers.
- **Best guild partners:** Alliums, calendula, thyme on edges.

## B8 · Udo (*Aralia cordata*)

- **Why it belongs:** Giant perennial vegetable producing edible spring shoots; excellent for high-biomass shade-edge systems.
- **Ideal conditions:** Part shade to full sun with moisture; rich deep soil; zone 4-9.
- **Guide:** Give it space. Harvest blanched young shoots sparingly. Valuable more for diversity and biomass than for staple calories.
- **Pests / diseases:** Slugs, wind damage, occasional root stress in compacted ground.
- **Best guild partners:** Woodland strawberries, Caucasian spinach, currants, mint family edge plants.

## B9 · Goumi (*Elaeagnus multiflora*)

- **Why it belongs:** Drought-tolerant, nitrogen-fixing fruit shrub with broad guild utility.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 5.5-7.5; adaptable soil, even poor soil; zone 4-8.
- **Guide:** Excellent nurse shrub for food forests. Prune lightly to control size. Once established, handles summer dryness well.
- **Pests / diseases:** Few serious issues; birds may take berries.
- **Best guild partners:** Hazelnut, serviceberry, currants, strawberries, amaranth on sunny edges.

## B10 · Aronia / Chokeberry (*Aronia melanocarpa*)

- **Why it belongs:** Extremely hardy, productive, medicinally rich shrub with strong antioxidant fruit.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to part shade; pH 5.0-7.0; tolerates wet and dry swings better than many fruits; zone 3-8.
- **Guide:** Useful in hedgerows, wet margins, and resilience plantings. Fruit is best processed, dried, juiced, or blended.
- **Pests / diseases:** Generally low pressure; birds can be intense.
- **Best guild partners:** Elderberry, native pollinator plants, clover, yarrow.

## B11 · Hardy Kiwi (*Actinidia arguta* / *A. kolomikta*)

- **Why it belongs:** Very productive vine fruit for western Washington if you are willing to manage structure and pruning.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 5.5-7.0; rich drained soil; even moisture; sheltered from late frost on blooms.
- **Guide:** Requires strong trellis and pruning discipline. Usually needs male/female pollination planning.
- **Pests / diseases:** Spring frost to young growth, vine damage from cats on some species, overgrowth if neglected.
- **Best guild partners:** Comfrey near but not on trunk, clover, alliums, strawberries under trellis edge.

## B12 · Daylily (*Hemerocallis* spp.)

- **Why it belongs:** Edible buds, flowers, shoots, and tuber-like roots; durable and adaptable.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to part shade; pH 6.0-7.0; average drained soil; zone 3-9.
- **Guide:** A support edible more than a staple crop, but superb for border resilience and biomass.
- **Pests / diseases:** Crown rot in wet heavy soils; deer browsing.
- **Best guild partners:** Rhubarb, alliums, serviceberry, yarrow.

# LIST C — High-Nutrient and Apocalypse-Resilient Crops for the PNW

This list is not ideologically pure. It includes both **permanent crops** and **annual insurance crops** because real resilience depends on calories and nutrients, not romance.

## C1 · Hazelnut

- **Why it belongs here:** Dense calories, oils, protein, storage ability, and strong regional fit.
- **Ideal conditions:** See A1; prioritize sun, drainage, and resistant cultivars.
- **Collapse value:** A mature nut crop gives stored food without annual tillage.
- **Main risks:** Disease-resistant planting stock and wildlife competition are the key make-or-break issues.

## C2 · Chestnut (*Castanea* spp., blight-aware selection)

- **Why it belongs here:** One of the few temperate tree crops that behaves like a starch staple.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 5.5-6.5 preferred; deep well-drained soil; room to grow; zone 5-8.
- **Guide:** Best for larger properties. Plant two or more for pollination. Avoid poorly drained heavy basins.
- **Main risks:** Chestnut blight, wildlife pressure, long juvenile period.
- **Collapse value:** High carbohydrate nut crop with serious food-security potential.

## C3 · Jerusalem Artichoke

- **Why it belongs here:** Almost absurdly forgiving tuber calorie crop.
- **Ideal conditions:** See A7.
- **Collapse value:** Can remain in-ground food storage and replant itself.
- **Main risks:** Spread, voles, and inulin sensitivity for some eaters.

## C4 · Stinging Nettle (*Urtica dioica*)

- **Why it belongs here:** One of the best survival greens in the region for iron, calcium, minerals, and protein-rich leaf matter.
- **Ideal conditions:** Sun to partial shade; moist fertile soil; zone 3-10.
- **Guide:** Give it a defined patch. Cut young growth repeatedly. Dry for storage powder or tea.
- **Main risks:** Spread, sting, and nitrate accumulation in over-rich waste ground.
- **Collapse value:** Free micronutrient engine; also excellent compost tea crop.

## C5 · Perennial Kale / Tree Kale

- **Why it belongs here:** Long-season micronutrient production from a small footprint.
- **Ideal conditions:** See B3; give drainage and moderate fertility.
- **Collapse value:** Fresh greens through shoulder seasons when annual systems fail.
- **Main risks:** Brassica pests and winter crown stress in cold wet pockets.

## C6 · Black Currant

- **Why it belongs here:** Vitamin C, anthocyanins, preserves, syrup, drying, and cold-climate reliability.
- **Ideal conditions:** See A2.
- **Collapse value:** Preservable berry with strong medicinal and nutritional overlap.
- **Main risks:** Birds and mildew in stagnant planting layouts.

## C7 · Elderberry

- **Why it belongs here:** Biomass, fruit, medicine, hedge function, and wet-site utility.
- **Ideal conditions:** See A4.
- **Collapse value:** Fruit, flowers, habitat, privacy, and edge stabilization all in one shrub.
- **Main risks:** Bird pressure and poor pruning discipline.

## C8 · Walking Onion

- **Why it belongs here:** Continuous onion greens and self-propagation with minimal labor.
- **Ideal conditions:** See A8.
- **Collapse value:** A perennial seasoning and mineral crop that keeps reproducing itself.
- **Main risks:** Waterlogging and neglect so extreme that clumps are smothered.

## C9 · Amaranth (*Amaranthus* spp.)

- **Why it belongs here:** Edible grain + edible leaf + heat/drought resilience.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 6.0-7.5; fertile but drained soil; warm sowing window after frost.
- **Guide:** Best used as a high-summer annual resilience crop between perennial systems and sunny margins.
- **Main risks:** Cold spring stagnation, slugs early, lodging under excess nitrogen, wet-season fungal pressure.
- **Collapse value:** Seed protein plus leaf greens from one crop.

## C10 · Fava Bean (*Vicia faba*)

- **Why it belongs here:** Cool-season protein crop exceptionally suited to maritime climates.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 6.0-7.5; moisture-retentive soil; sow very early or in autumn in mild sites.
- **Guide:** Excellent between young perennials or in annual support beds.
- **Main risks:** Black aphids, chocolate spot in dense wet growth.
- **Collapse value:** Protein, fresh green matter, nitrogen fixation.

## C11 · Potato (*Solanum tuberosum*)

- **Why it belongs here:** If the question is survival rather than purity, potatoes belong in the answer.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; pH 5.0-6.5; loose soil; regular moisture; disease-aware rotation.
- **Guide:** Grow as an annual insurance staple. Use mulch systems or ridged rows in heavier soils.
- **Main risks:** Late blight, scab, wireworms, volunteer disease carryover.
- **Collapse value:** High return per square foot when well managed.

## C12 · Winter Squash / Storage Pumpkin (*Cucurbita maxima* and related)

- **Why it belongs here:** Dense calories, long storage, large biomass, and broad utility in a survival garden.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun; fertile soil; pH 6.0-7.5; deep mulch and summer moisture.
- **Guide:** Use on compost mounds, young orchard edges, or broad annual patches.
- **Main risks:** Slugs on seedlings, powdery mildew late, rodents on ripening fruit.
- **Collapse value:** Shelf-stable calories and seed value.

# LIST D — Twelve Native Edible Crops for a PNW-Rooted System

These are not just "foraging curiosities." They are candidates for a regionally literate food landscape. Use them with correct identification, ethical harvest, and respect for Indigenous stewardship traditions.

## D1 · Camas (*Camassia quamash*, *C. leichtlinii*)

- **Why it matters:** Historically one of the great root staples of the Northwest.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to open meadow; moisture in winter-spring, drier in summer dormancy; zone 4-8.
- **Guide:** Plant in meadows, orchard openings, or native food beds. Bulbs require patience and correct species identification.
- **Critical warning:** **Death camas is poisonous.** Never eat any camas without absolute identification.
- **Best guild partners:** Nodding onion, yarrow, native strawberries, serviceberry edges.

## D2 · Wapato / Broadleaf Arrowhead (*Sagittaria latifolia*)

- **Why it matters:** True native wetland starch crop.
- **Ideal conditions:** Pond edge, swale ponding zone, shallow water or saturated mud; full sun.
- **Guide:** Best treated as a managed wetland crop. Tubers are harvested from mud after tops die back.
- **Pests / diseases:** Waterfowl and muskrats may compete more than diseases do.
- **Best guild partners:** Wetland mint relatives, cattail edge systems, elderberry on higher margins.

## D3 · Salal (*Gaultheria shallon*)

- **Why it matters:** Durable evergreen berry shrub well adapted to acidic woodland edges.
- **Ideal conditions:** Part shade to sun; acidic humus-rich soil; zone 6-9.
- **Guide:** Useful under open conifer edges and in native hedges. Fruit is best for drying, leather, or preserves rather than raw luxury eating.
- **Pests / diseases:** Generally low pressure; chlorosis in unsuitable alkaline ground.
- **Best guild partners:** Evergreen huckleberry, red huckleberry, Oregon grape, native strawberry.

## D4 · Evergreen Huckleberry (*Vaccinium ovatum*)

- **Why it matters:** One of the best native berry shrubs for year-round structure and edible yield.
- **Ideal conditions:** Acidic soil, good organic matter, part shade to sun, zone 7-9.
- **Guide:** Slow and steady plant. Avoid lime and ash around it. Mulch with leaf mold, needles, or acidic organic matter.
- **Pests / diseases:** Birds; nutrient issues if pH is too high.
- **Best guild partners:** Salal, red huckleberry, native strawberry, sword fern as ecological companion.

## D5 · Red Huckleberry (*Vaccinium parvifolium*)

- **Why it matters:** Very PNW, very useful, and excellent in moist woodland edge systems.
- **Ideal conditions:** Acidic, humus-rich, often decaying wood-associated soil; light shade preferred; zone 6-8.
- **Guide:** Better for forest-edge planting than exposed hot fields. Let it occupy the fungal places.
- **Pests / diseases:** Bird competition more than disease.
- **Best guild partners:** Salal, Oregon grape, evergreen huckleberry, decaying wood mulch.

## D6 · Salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*)

- **Why it matters:** Vigorous native caneberry for moist margins and wildlife-rich hedges.
- **Ideal conditions:** Moist soils; sun to partial shade; zone 5-9.
- **Guide:** Best for wet-margin production, living fence function, and ecological abundance rather than tidy formal berry rows.
- **Pests / diseases:** Cane crowding, mildew, bird pressure.
- **Best guild partners:** Elderberry, wapato margins, nettle patches, red flowering currant in mixed native hedges.

## D7 · Thimbleberry (*Rubus parviflorus*)

- **Why it matters:** Soft flavorful berry with strong habitat value and broad regional adaptation.
- **Ideal conditions:** Sun to part shade; average to moist soil; zone 3-9.
- **Guide:** Excellent at woodland edge and for soft-fruit diversity. Not a long-distance shipping fruit; perfect for garden-scale immediate use.
- **Pests / diseases:** Bird pressure, cane crowding, deer browse.
- **Best guild partners:** Serviceberry, native strawberry, nodding onion, yarrow.

## D8 · Oregon Grape (*Mahonia aquifolium* and relatives)

- **Why it matters:** Native evergreen shrub with tart fruit, medicinal roots, and strong drought resilience once established.
- **Ideal conditions:** Sun to partial shade; adaptable soil if drained; zone 5-9.
- **Guide:** Superb hedge and understory structure plant. Fruit is acidic but excellent in jelly, blended preserves, and syrups.
- **Pests / diseases:** Generally low pressure; root stress in long waterlogged soil.
- **Best guild partners:** Salal, huckleberries, yarrow, nodding onion.

## D9 · Nodding Onion (*Allium cernuum*)

- **Why it matters:** Native perennial allium offering edible leaves, flowers, and bulbs.
- **Ideal conditions:** Full sun to light shade; well-drained soil; zone 4-8.
- **Guide:** Ideal for meadow edges, orchard herb layers, and native pollinator strips.
- **Pests / diseases:** Same broad allium concerns as cultivated onions, but usually minor.
- **Best guild partners:** Camas, serviceberry, yarrow, native strawberries.

## D10 · Pacific Crabapple (*Malus fusca*)

- **Why it matters:** Native tart apple for preserves, ferments, and wildlife value.
- **Ideal conditions:** Sun; moisture-retentive but drained soil; tolerant of seasonally wet sites; zone 6-9.
- **Guide:** Better as a native food-tree component than as a dessert apple substitute. Useful in moist hedges and mixed orchard genetics.
- **Pests / diseases:** Apple scab, birds, codling-like issues, browsing when young.
- **Best guild partners:** Nodding onion, yarrow, clover, serviceberry.

## D11 · Beaked Hazelnut / Native Hazelnut (*Corylus cornuta*)

- **Why it matters:** Native nut shrub with ecological value and edible kernels.
- **Ideal conditions:** Sun to part shade; adaptable soils; zone 3-8.
- **Guide:** Smaller nuts than improved filberts, but better ecological fit in native hedges and restoration-minded systems.
- **Pests / diseases:** Wildlife pressure is intense; blight considerations still matter.
- **Best guild partners:** Serviceberry, Oregon grape, thimbleberry, yarrow.

## D12 · Wild Strawberry (*Fragaria vesca*, *F. virginiana*, regional natives)

- **Why it matters:** Groundcover food crop, pollinator support, living mulch, and native aesthetic all at once.
- **Ideal conditions:** Sun to light shade; well-drained humus soil; regular moisture; zone 4-9.
- **Guide:** Use aggressively as understory between shrubs and along paths. More valuable as a system plant than as a bulk fruit crop.
- **Pests / diseases:** Slugs, birds, drying in unmulched hot edges.
- **Best guild partners:** Almost every native shrub and orchard edge plant on this list.

# Soil Strategy for These Crops

The lists above only work if the soil plan is realistic.

## 1 · Start with Drainage

In Duvall, drainage often matters more than raw fertility. The crops most likely to fail in winter wet are:

- asparagus
- rhubarb crowns planted too low
- sea kale
- perennial brassicas
- chestnut
- hardy kiwi
- oca / yacon crowns left in cold soggy ground

**Practical fixes:**

- broadfork rather than rototill when possible
- add compost, leaves, and wood chips steadily
- create gentle mounds, ridges, or raised beds for crown plants
- keep mulch a few inches away from crowns in winter

## 2 · pH Bands That Matter

- **Acid lovers (roughly 4.5-5.8):** evergreen huckleberry, red huckleberry, salal
- **Broadly adaptable (about 5.5-7.0):** currants, gooseberries, elderberry, serviceberry, amaranth, aronia
- **Prefer near neutral (about 6.5-7.5):** asparagus, sea kale, many alliums

## 3 · Organic Matter Is Water Insurance

From the soil and climate guides in this folder, the most important long-game move is building soil organic matter and structure. That improves:

- summer water retention
- winter infiltration
- nutrient buffering
- disease resistance

Use:

- finished compost
- leaf mold
- wood-chip fungal mulch around shrubs and trees
- living covers like white clover where competition is tolerable

## 4 · Mycorrhizae, Rotation, and Exceptions

[SOIL_AND_EARTH.md](SOIL_AND_EARTH.md) notes that most food crops benefit strongly from mycorrhizal networks, with important exceptions including **brassicas and the chenopod / amaranth family group**. In practical terms, that means these crops do not lean on fungal symbiosis the way many berries, shrubs, and trees do; they respond more directly to drainage, temperature, aeration, and nutrient balance. This matters because:

- hazelnut, berries, many natives, and tree/shrub guilds benefit from fungal-friendly soil
- perennial brassicas need less emphasis on mycorrhizal inoculation and more on drainage, disease control, and fertility balance
- amaranth should not be treated like a fungal-dependent crop; keep its soil warm, open, and aerated instead

# Water Strategy for Duvall and the PNW

## 1 · The Seasonal Pattern

The region often has plenty of annual rainfall but not enough **summer availability** where crops need it. So the question is not only "How much rain falls?" but "How much remains in the root zone by August?"

## 2 · Watering Rules That Matter

Adapted directly from [GROWING_GUIDE.md](GROWING_GUIDE.md):

1. water deeply and infrequently
2. water at the base, not over leaves
3. morning is best
4. mulch everything you intend to keep
5. group by water need

## 3 · Hydrozone the Property

- **High moisture zone:** wapato, elderberry, salmonberry, camas in proper native meadow conditions
- **Moderate moisture zone:** currants, gooseberries, rhubarb, sorrel, skirret
- **Dry-summer tolerant zone after establishment:** hazelnut, goumi, aronia, Oregon grape, amaranth, Jerusalem artichoke

## 4 · Best Watering Methods for This Guide

- **Drip:** best for establishment years and targeted summer support
- **Soaker hose:** good for berry rows and perennial vegetable beds
- **Ollas / buried clay irrigation:** useful for amaranth, squash, yacon, and annual support beds
- **Swales / contour infiltration:** valuable on sloped sites with droughty summer soils

# Pest and Disease Strategy

No resilient system is pest-free. The real goal is **pressure reduction without system fragility**.

## 1 · Core PNW Pressure Set

- slugs and snails
- aphids
- flea beetles
- cabbage moth / caterpillar complex
- berry-eating birds
- deer and rabbits
- fungal leaf spots and mildews
- root and crown rots from drainage failure

## 2 · The First Defense Is Structure

Borrowing the logic of [GROWING_GUIDE.md](GROWING_GUIDE.md), pest management begins with:

- biodiversity
- airflow
- appropriate spacing
- no chronic overhead irrigation
- no giant overfed monocrop blocks

## 3 · Common Failures by Crop Type

| Crop group | Main failure pattern |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Berry shrubs** | crowded shade + humidity + bird loss |
| **Alliums** | poor drainage + neglect + rust |
| **Brassicas** | flea beetles, cabbage worms, slug pressure, clubroot where rotation is ignored |
| **Crowns / tubers** | planting too low in cold wet heavy soil |
| **Native acid-loving shrubs** | wrong pH and over-liming |

## 4 · Practical Controls

- ducks or deliberate slug control at establishment
- netting for currants, haskaps, and serviceberries if human harvest matters
- trap crops like nasturtium near brassicas
- allium rings and aromatic herbs in berry guilds
- yearly sanitation pruning
- mulch moderation around crowns to prevent rot

# Optimized Guilds for These Crops

Guilds matter because they turn isolated crops into self-supporting neighborhoods.

## Guild 1 · Hazelnut-Currant Resilience Guild

**Use when:** you want a dry-summer-capable nut and berry core.

- **Center / backbone:** hazelnut
- **Shrub ring:** currants, gooseberries, jostaberries
- **Herb layer:** chives, yarrow, thyme
- **Dynamic accumulator:** comfrey on the dripline, chopped for mulch
- **Groundcover:** white clover or native strawberry depending on aesthetics and moisture

**Why it works:** layered rooting, edible diversity, pollinator support, summer shade for berry roots, and lower bare-soil evaporation.

## Guild 2 · Elderberry Wet-Margin Guild

**Use when:** winter-spring moisture is high.

- **Backbone:** elderberry
- **Moist companions:** salmonberry, aronia
- **Herb / edge plants:** mint family herbs kept contained, yarrow, native grasses on margins
- **Understory / shoulder zone:** camas in higher drier edge, not in persistent saturation

**Why it works:** it accepts water instead of fighting it.

## Guild 3 · Perennial Vegetable Bed Guild

**Use when:** you want food close to the house.

- **Structural crops:** asparagus, rhubarb, sea kale
- **Support crops:** walking onion, sorrel, chives, parsley, calendula
- **Mulch crops nearby:** comfrey and nettle cut-and-carry, not crowding crowns

**Why it works:** stable harvest windows from early spring through summer and reduced annual bed prep.

## Guild 4 · Tuber-Calorie Boundary Guild

**Use when:** you need a resilient bulk-food strip.

- **Backbone:** Jerusalem artichoke
- **Companions:** walking onion, clover, yarrow, calendula
- **Optional seasonal fillers:** fava bean in front, winter squash at edge, amaranth in sun gaps

**Why it works:** tall biomass, tuber storage, pollinator support, and low care.

## Guild 5 · Native Berry Woodland-Edge Guild

**Use when:** you want a native edible zone.

- **Backbone:** evergreen huckleberry, red huckleberry, salal
- **Secondary shrubs:** Oregon grape, thimbleberry
- **Ground layer:** wild strawberry
- **Accent / repellents:** nodding onion, yarrow

**Why it works:** correct pH ecology, layered native adaptation, low aesthetic conflict.

## Guild 6 · Sunny Drought-Tolerant Annual-Perennial Interface

**Use when:** you have the hottest open patch.

- **Anchors:** goumi, hazelnut on north side, aronia if moisture is moderate
- **Annual resilience crops:** amaranth, fava, squash, potatoes in rotation
- **Insectary ring:** dill, calendula, basil, marigold, alyssum
- **Groundcover:** clover kept low

**Why it works:** the perennials stabilize edges while annuals exploit summer heat.

# Deep Dive — The Optimized Amaranth Guild for the Maritime PNW

Amaranth deserves its own section because it is one of the best **bridge crops** between perennial food-forest logic and annual survival-crop logic.

It is not a perfect PNW crop by default. It is a **warm-season opportunity crop**. If you place it badly, it sulks. If you place it correctly, it produces grain, greens, pollinator value, and dramatic biomass with relatively low irrigation.

## 1 · What Amaranth Is Best At

- warm-season grain and greens
- high mineral density
- drought tolerance after establishment
- productive use of sunny edges and annual clearings
- filling late-spring / summer gaps in otherwise perennial-heavy systems

## 2 · What Makes It Fail in Western Washington

- sowing too early into cold soil
- over-shaded placement
- slug pressure at seedling stage
- excessive nitrogen leading to lush weak stems and delayed seed set
- overcrowding and stagnant airflow in wet years

## 3 · Ideal Conditions for an Amaranth Guild

- **Sun:** absolute full sun
- **Soil:** loose, fertile, well-drained
- **pH:** roughly 6.0-7.5
- **Temperature:** sow after real warmth arrives; cold mud is not its friend
- **Water:** moderate and steady until roots are established, then lighter but deeper watering
- **Spacing:** enough for airflow; crowded amaranth invites disease and lodging

## 4 · The Guild Layout

### Core Layout

- **Center rows / clusters:** amaranth
- **Interplanted nitrogen support:** bush beans or low clover at margins rather than directly choking stems
- **Beneficial insect ring:** calendula, dill, basil, alyssum, marigold
- **Perimeter defense:** walking onion or garlic chives on outer edge
- **Ground moderation:** low thyme or oregano on the driest border, not under dense stem bases

### Timing Window for Duvall

- **seed starting window:** only after frost danger and cold-soil stagnation are genuinely passing
- **direct sowing window:** usually **late May into early June** for best emergence
- **maturity target:** use earlier-maturing selections so heads can finish before cold wet autumn
- **harvest window:** usually late summer into early autumn, with drying protected from rain

### Best Orientation

- put taller companion shrubs or trellis crops to the **north**
- keep the **south and west open**
- maintain wide enough paths that foliage dries quickly after dew

## 5 · Best Companion Functions

| Companion | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| **Bush bean** | Nitrogen support without heavy shading |
| **Calendula** | Beneficial insect attraction; long bloom window |
| **Dill** | Parasitoid wasps and hoverflies |
| **Basil** | Pollinator support; aromatic diversity |
| **Marigold** | Root-zone pest confusion and insect attraction |
| **Walking onion / garlic chive** | Border utility; allium chemistry; edible edge |
| **Clover** | Living mulch between paths if kept low |

## 6 · Crops to Keep Away from the Core

- sprawling squash that completely smothers airflow
- tall corn on the south side
- wet-loving crops that demand constant irrigation
- large brassica blocks that increase pest carryover and create shade turbulence

## 7 · Soil and Fertility Plan for the Amaranth Guild

Amaranth likes fertility, but not sloppy excess.

**Best approach:**

1. compost before planting
2. modest side-dressing if growth stalls
3. avoid huge nitrogen dumps late
4. keep soil warm with mulch applied after it has already heated

**Important nuance:** as noted earlier in the mycorrhizae section, amaranth belongs to a crop group that does not depend strongly on fungal symbiosis the way many woody perennials do, so the amaranth guild should emphasize:

- warmth
- aeration
- weed suppression
- beneficial insect support

more than fungal woodland mimicry.

### Nutrition Snapshot for Why Amaranth Is Worth the Space

Approximate grain values often cited in agronomic references:

| Nutrient (per 100 g grain) | Approximate value |
|----------------------------|-------------------|
| Protein | 13-15 g |
| Calcium | 159 mg |
| Iron | 7.6 mg |
| Magnesium | 248 mg |
| Phosphorus | 557 mg |
| Potassium | 508 mg |

That combination is why amaranth remains one of the best "dual-use" preparedness crops: it gives both **greens** and **seed food**.

## 8 · Watering the Amaranth Guild

- water consistently through emergence and early stem formation
- once rooted, shift to **deep, infrequent irrigation**
- never keep the soil saturated
- do not overhead-water late in the day

In drought years, amaranth will usually outperform many common annual greens and grains, but it still yields best with sensible summer support.

## 9 · Pest and Disease Profile

### Major pressures

- slugs at emergence
- aphids on lush growth
- flea beetle nibbling
- occasional leaf miners
- fungal spotting or mildew in wet crowded conditions

### Management

- protect the first few weeks aggressively from slugs
- thin plants for airflow
- keep weed pressure low so seedlings do not disappear
- use beneficial-attractor ring plants continuously
- avoid rich late-summer nitrogen that delays ripening

## 10 · Harvest Strategy

- **Leaf harvest:** early and moderate; do not weaken grain plants by stripping too hard
- **Grain harvest:** wait until seed heads mature and dry; bag or tarp under heads if birds become a problem
- **Seed saving:** save from the earliest, healthiest, best-standing plants adapted to your site

## 11 · Best Roles for Amaranth in a Resilient Homestead

- annual strip beside hazelnut rows
- hot south-facing garden edge
- backup grain in a mixed preparedness plot
- edible biomass crop in years when cool-season crops underperform

## 12 · Bottom Line on the Amaranth Guild

In the PNW, amaranth should not replace the perennial backbone. It should **complete** it.

Think of it as:

- the summer heat specialist
- the seed crop between shrubs
- the bridge between pantry resilience and garden beauty

# A Practical Deployment Order

If building this system from scratch, install in this order:

1. **Drainage, paths, mulch, and water access**
2. **Hazelnut / berry / native shrub backbone**
3. **Perennial vegetables** — asparagus, rhubarb, walking onion, sorrel
4. **Tuber resilience strip** — Jerusalem artichoke and other root crops
5. **Native edible patch** matched to correct microsites
6. **Annual resilience interface** — amaranth, fava, potato, squash

That order reduces rework and builds ecological stability first.

## Final Strategic Note

If the future becomes hotter, poorer, and less predictable, the best PNW food system will not be a monocrop field and it will not be a pure ornamental landscape. It will be a **layered edible ecology**:

- nuts for calories and fats
- berries for vitamins and preserves
- perennial greens for continuity
- tubers for bulk food
- annual heat crops like amaranth for adaptation
- native species for regional intelligence and ecological fit

That is the real aim of this guide: not just harvest, but durability.
