## Soil Is Not Dirt

Dirt is dead mineral substrate. **Soil is a living ecosystem** — the most species-rich habitat
on Earth. One teaspoon of healthy garden soil contains:
- 100 million to 1 billion bacteria (representing thousands of species)
- Several hundred metres of fungal hyphae
- Thousands of protozoa
- Hundreds of nematodes
- Numerous arthropods, earthworms, and their eggs

These organisms, interacting with each other and with plant roots, perform services no
synthetic input can replicate: nitrogen fixation, phosphorus solubilisation, pathogen
suppression, hormone production, water retention, and soil structure creation.

When we understand soil as a living community, chemical farming becomes obviously
self-defeating — it kills the community that makes fertility.

## 1 · Soil Composition

### Mineral Fraction (45-60% of soil volume)

Soil minerals derive from the weathering of parent rock — a process governed by geology,
hydrology, and biological activity. The mineral fraction determines:

**Texture** (proportions of sand, silt, clay):
- **Sand** (particles 0.05-2mm): fast drainage, low water/nutrient holding, easy to work
- **Silt** (0.002-0.05mm): silky texture, moderate drainage, moderate fertility
- **Clay** (<0.002mm): slow drainage, high water/nutrient holding, compacts when wet

**Ideal texture: loam** (roughly equal parts sand, silt, clay with organic matter)

**The "ribbon test":** Squeeze moist soil between thumb and forefinger.
- Falls apart → sandy
- Ribbons 2-5cm before breaking → clay loam or silt loam (ideal)
- Ribbons >5cm → heavy clay

### Organic Matter (1-10% in healthy soils; <1% in degraded)

Organic matter is the decomposed remnants of plants, animals, and microorganisms.
Its importance cannot be overstated:

| Function | Mechanism |
|---------|----------|
| Water retention | 1g OM holds 10× its weight in water |
| Cation exchange | OM holds positively charged nutrients (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺) against leaching |
| Slow-release fertility | Mineralisation releases nutrients as plants need them |
| Soil structure | Microbial metabolites (glomalin) bind aggregates; worm casts create macro-pores |
| pH buffering | Humic and fulvic acids buffer pH swings |
| Microbial habitat | The substrate in which soil life lives |

**Humus** = the stable, highly processed end-product of OM decomposition. Rich in humic acids,
fulvic acids, and humin. Lasts decades to centuries in soil.

### Air and Water (20-25% each in optimal soil)

- Root respiration requires oxygen — compacted, waterlogged soils starve roots and create anaerobic zones that favour pathogens
- Ideal soil has macro-pores (for drainage and air) and micro-pores (for water retention)
- **Earthworms** create this structure; one earthworm casts 5-10 tonnes of soil per acre per year

## 2 · Soil Chemistry

### pH — The Master Variable

Soil pH (measure of hydrogen ion concentration, 0-14 scale) affects:
- **Nutrient availability** — the single most important factor other than organic matter
- **Microbial community composition**
- **Toxicity** of certain minerals (aluminium, manganese become toxic below pH 5.5)

| pH range | Character | Affected nutrients |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| < 5.5 | Strongly acid | P, Ca, Mg, Mo unavailable; Al, Mn, Fe toxic |
| 5.5 – 6.0 | Moderately acid | Good for blueberry, potatoes, azalea |
| 6.0 – 7.0 | Ideal for most plants | Maximum availability of most nutrients |
| 7.0 – 7.5 | Neutral to slightly alkaline | Still good; lime-loving plants thrive |
| > 7.5 | Alkaline | Fe, Mn, Zn, B unavailable; chlorosis common |

**Lowering pH:** Sulfur, peat, pine needles, organic matter, acidifying fertilisers  
**Raising pH:** Lime (calcium carbonate), dolomitic lime (+ magnesium), wood ash

### Essential Plant Nutrients

**Macronutrients (N, P, K, Ca, Mg, S):**

| Nutrient | Role in plant | Deficiency sign | Best organic source |
|---------|--------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Nitrogen (N)** | Protein, chlorophyll, growth | Yellowing from oldest leaves | Compost, legume green manure, blood meal |
| **Phosphorus (P)** | Energy transfer (ATP), root development, flowering | Purple/reddish leaves; poor root growth | Bone meal, rock phosphate, mycorrhizae |
| **Potassium (K)** | Enzyme activation, water regulation, disease resistance | Brown leaf edges; weak stems | Wood ash, kelp, granite dust, compost |
| **Calcium (Ca)** | Cell wall structure, blossom end rot prevention | Growing tips die; blossom end rot in tomatoes | Lime, gypsum, eggshells |
| **Magnesium (Mg)** | Chlorophyll core; enzyme activation | Interveinal chlorosis | Dolomitic lime, Epsom salt (foliar), compost |
| **Sulfur (S)** | Amino acids, glucosinolates, alliin | Uniform yellowing of young leaves | Elemental sulfur, compost |

**Micronutrients:** Iron, manganese, zinc, copper, boron, molybdenum, chlorine — required in
trace amounts but essential. Almost always adequate in living organic soil. Usually deficient
only when pH is wrong or soil is depleted/heavily tilled.

### Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC)

CEC is the soil's "battery capacity" — its ability to hold and release positively charged
nutrients (cations: Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺, H⁺).

- Sandy soils: CEC 1-10
- Clay soils: CEC 20-50
- Peat / OM: CEC 100-300
- Biochar: CEC up to 400

**Building CEC = building organic matter and clay content.** This is the long game of soil
improvement — and it takes years, but the investment compounds.

## 3 · The Soil Microbiome

### Bacteria

Soil bacteria perform the most critical biogeochemical functions:

**Nitrogen fixers:**
- *Rhizobium*, *Bradyrhizobium* — symbiotic with legume roots; fix atmospheric N₂ into NH₄⁺
- *Azotobacter*, *Azospirillum* — free-living N fixers; found around grass roots
- *Frankia* — symbiotic with non-legume trees (alder, casuarina, sea buckthorn)

**Phosphorus solubilisers:** *Bacillus*, *Pseudomonas* — secrete organic acids that dissolve
phosphate minerals; essential because most soil P is locked in insoluble forms

**Nitrifiers:** *Nitrosomonas*, *Nitrobacter* — convert NH₄⁺ → NO₃⁻ (plant-available nitrate)

**Disease suppressors:** *Bacillus subtilis*, *Streptomyces* — secrete antibiotics that suppress
fungal pathogens. These are the origin of most modern antibiotics.

**Building the bacterial community:** Compost, compost tea, undisturbed soil, cover crops,
diverse organic matter inputs. Minimise synthetic nitrogen (bypasses N-fixing bacteria,
creating dependency).

### Fungi — The Underground Network

**Mycorrhizal fungi** form the most important plant-fungus partnership on Earth. Over 90%
of vascular plant species form mycorrhizal associations.

**Arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) fungi** (*Glomus*, *Rhizophagus*):
- Form inside root cells (arbuscules — tiny tree-like structures for nutrient exchange)
- Extend hyphae 10-100× beyond root reach
- Exchange: plant gives sugar (up to 30% of photosynthate) → fungi gives water, phosphorus, zinc, copper
- Present in most food crops (except brassicas and chenopods)

**Ectomycorrhizal fungi** (boletes, truffles, chanterelles, fly agaric):
- Sheath around roots; form Hartig net between root cells
- Primarily in trees: oak, pine, birch, beech
- Many are edible and medicinal mushrooms (chanterelle, porcini, truffle)

**The Wood Wide Web:**
Mycorrhizal networks connect trees across a forest. Older "mother trees" transfer carbon,
water, nutrients, and even chemical signals to younger trees and stressed neighbours via the
fungal network. This was first proven by Suzanne Simard (1997) and confirms the plant kingdom
as a genuinely networked intelligence.

**Decomposer fungi** (saprotrophic: *Trichoderma*, *Penicillium*, oyster mushroom, lion's mane):
- Break down cellulose, lignin, and recalcitrant organic matter
- Release locked minerals; create humus
- Many are medicinal (oyster, lion's mane, turkey tail, reishi)

**Supporting fungi:**
- Avoid fungicides (obvious)
- Avoid tilling (rips hyphal networks)
- Maintain living roots (fungi need sugar supply)
- Add wood chips (carbon source for fungal decomposers)
- Inoculate transplants with mycorrhizal inoculant

### Protozoa and Nematodes

Protozoa (amoebae, flagellates, ciliates) graze on bacteria. This "microbial loop" releases
nitrogen in plant-available form near roots — a process called the **mineralisation pump**.

Nematodes occupy multiple roles:
- **Bacterial feeders** — release nitrogen (beneficial)
- **Fungal feeders** — essential in decomposition
- **Plant feeders** — the pest nematodes (harmful in excess)
- **Predatory nematodes** — control pest nematodes and insects

Healthy, biologically active soil maintains a balance of nematode types through competition.

## 4 · Biogeochemical Cycles

### The Nitrogen Cycle

```
Atmospheric N₂ (78% of air)
        │ (nitrogen fixation by Rhizobium, Azotobacter, lightning)
        ▼
Ammonium (NH₄⁺) — available to plants
        │ (nitrification: Nitrosomonas)
        ▼
Nitrite (NO₂⁻)
        │ (nitrification: Nitrobacter)
        ▼
Nitrate (NO₃⁻) — highly soluble; leaches; most used by plants
        │ (plant uptake) or (denitrification in waterlogged soils → lost as N₂)
        ▼
Organic N in plants/animals/microbes
        │ (decomposition)
        ▼
Back to ammonium
```

**Growing legumes is the single most cost-effective soil improvement strategy.**
A good legume stand can fix 50-200 kg N/ha/year — equivalent to a substantial chemical
nitrogen fertiliser application, at zero cost.

### The Carbon Cycle

Photosynthesis pulls CO₂ from air → organic carbon in plant tissue → enters soil as organic
matter. Soil contains ~3× more carbon than the atmosphere. Regenerative agriculture,
biochar addition, and reduced tillage are among the most scalable carbon sequestration
strategies available.

**Priming effect:** Adding fresh organic matter stimulates microbial activity, temporarily
increasing decomposition of existing soil carbon (priming). Avoid this by adding well-composted
material rather than fresh organic matter when soil carbon conservation is a goal.

### Mineral Weathering and Deep Cycling

Plants actively accelerate rock weathering through:
- Root exudates (organic acids, chelating compounds)
- Mycorrhizal acids that dissolve mineral structures
- Physical root expansion fracturing rock

**"Rock dust" (finely ground basalt or granite)** provides a slow-release source of all
minerals and silica. Research by Prof. David Beerling (Sheffield) shows enhanced weathering
of basalt can sequester significant CO₂ while improving crop yields.

## 5 · Compost — The Alchemist's Art

Composting is the controlled acceleration of natural decomposition.

### The biology of compost

**Active phase** (55-70°C): Thermophilic bacteria dominate; pathogens and weed seeds killed.
Requires: carbon:nitrogen ratio ~25-30:1; adequate moisture (wrung-out sponge); air.

**Curing phase** (ambient): Fungi, actinomycetes, earthworms process remaining material.
Humus formation occurs here. Minimum 3-6 months.

### Inputs and C:N ratios

| Material | C:N ratio | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Fresh grass clippings | 15:1 | Nitrogen source; add thinly |
| Kitchen vegetable scraps | 15-20:1 | Nitrogen; mix with browns |
| Comfrey leaves | 9:1 | Excellent activator |
| Straw | 80:1 | Carbon source |
| Wood chips | 400-500:1 | Carbon; best for fungal-dominant compost |
| Cardboard (plain) | 350:1 | Carbon; excellent with wood chips |
| Dried leaves | 60:1 | Carbon |
| Manure (horse, cow) | 20-30:1 | Good balanced input |

**Activators:** Comfrey leaves, seaweed, urine (diluted 10:1), nettles — all kick-start
nitrogen-rich microbial activity.

## 6 · Living Soil Management — The No-Dig Revolution

The **no-dig method** (developed by Charles Dowding and others) recognises that tilling
destroys soil structure, mycorrhizal networks, aggregate stability, and worm populations.

**Transition to no-dig:**
1. Sheet mulch: lay cardboard over grass/weeds (no digging); smother in compost/organic matter
2. Plant directly into compost layer
3. Annual top-dressing with 2-5cm of mature compost; never disturb soil below

**Results:** Higher yields, fewer weeds (weed seeds stay buried, not turned up), better
water retention, lower labour, healthier plants.

## 7 · Mineral Intelligence — Reading Your Plants

Plants display nutrient deficiencies visually:

| Symptom | Likely deficiency | Confirming sign |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| Yellowing from old leaves up | Nitrogen or sulfur | N: uniform yellow; S: younger leaves first |
| Purple/red leaves | Phosphorus | Cold temperatures also cause this |
| Brown leaf edges | Potassium | Especially on older leaves |
| Interveinal chlorosis (yellow between green veins) | Iron or manganese | Fe: young leaves; Mn: older leaves |
| Tip die-back | Calcium (or boron) | Blossom end rot in tomatoes |
| Poor flower set, cracked fruit | Boron | Common in alkaline soils |

**Before adding anything:** Test the pH first. Most deficiencies are caused by wrong pH,
not actual mineral absence.

## 8 · Earthworms — The Planetary Tillers

Charles Darwin spent 40 years studying earthworms and concluded they were among the most
important creatures in Earth's history. He was right.

**Lumbricus terrestris** (nightcrawler) — vertical burrower; pulls surface organic matter to depths; creates channels for air and water

**Eisenia fetida** (red wriggler) — surface-active decomposer; used in vermicomposting

Earthworm castings:
- Contain 5× more nitrogen, 7× more phosphorus, 11× more potassium than surrounding soil
- Enhanced plant-available mineral forms
- Microbiome enrichment — each casting contains hundreds of millions of bacteria
- Hormones and enzyme complexes that directly stimulate plant growth

**Creating earthworm habitat:** Mulch + organic matter + no tilling + no synthetic chemicals. Earthworms will come.
