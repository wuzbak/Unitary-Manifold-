## How to Use This Index

This guide is organised by body system, then by condition within each system. For each herb,
you will find: traditional system of use, key active compounds, primary actions, preparation
methods, safety notes, and growing information.

**Safety principle:** This index is educational. Chronic or serious conditions warrant
consultation with a qualified healthcare practitioner. The herbs listed are not a substitute
for emergency medical care.

**Abbreviation key:**  
TCM = Traditional Chinese Medicine · Ayur = Ayurveda · WH = Western Herbalism  
Folk = folk medicine · NA = North American Indigenous  
BBB = blood-brain barrier · HPA = hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis

## 3 · Digestive System

### Digestive Bitters and Liver Support

**Bitters** are the most important category of digestive herbs — they stimulate bitter taste
receptors (TAS2R) on the tongue and gut lining, triggering a whole cascade: HCl production,

### The Liver — Hepatoprotective Herbs

The liver processes every compound that enters the body. Supporting it is central to all other healing.

**Milk Thistle (Silymarin/Silybin):** The gold standard for liver protection.
- Inhibits toxin transport into hepatocytes (blocks bile acid transporters)
- Stimulates RNA polymerase I → increased ribosomal synthesis and hepatocyte regeneration
- Antioxidant in liver parenchyma
- Anti-fibrotic (reduces stellate cell activation)
- Evidence base: hepatitis, cirrhosis, mushroom poisoning (*Amanita phalloides* — silybin is an antidote)

**Schisandra** (*S. chinensis*): "Five Flavour Berry" — adaptogen and liver tonic
- Schisandrins induce Phase 1 and Phase 2 detoxification
- Anti-fibrotic, hepatoprotective against CCl4 toxicity
- Reduces AST/ALT in viral hepatitis

## 10 · The "Witch's Cabinet" — Plants with Sacred Histories

These plants appear across magical and spiritual traditions worldwide. Their chemistry
explains why.

| Plant | Tradition | Chemistry | Effect |
|-------|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Mugwort** (*Artemisia vulgaris*) | European, NA, Asian | Thujone, cineole, camphor | Dream enhancement; GABA modulation; uterine tonic |
| **Wormwood** (*Artemisia absinthium*) | European | Absinthin, thujone, artemisinin | Bitter tonic; anti-parasitic; the absinthe plant |
| **Rosemary** | Mediterranean | Rosmarinic acid, camphor | Memory (Shakespeare: "Rosemary for remembrance") — AChE inhibition |
| **Angelica** (*A. archangelica*) | Nordic, European | Furanocoumarins, phthalides | Anti-spasmodic, carminative; photosensitising |
| **Yarrow** | Universal | Achillin, borneol, azulene | Haemostatic; divination association; wound herb of Achilles |
| **Elder** (*Sambucus nigra*) | European folk | Anthocyanins, lectins, sambunigrin (COOK the flowers/berries) | Powerful antiviral; fever reduction |
| **Mandrake** (*Mandragora officinarum*) | Mediterranean, Medieval | Scopolamine, hyoscyamine | Narcotic, hallucinogenic at dose — used in "flying ointments" |
| **Henbane** (*Hyoscyamus niger*) | Ancient Egyptian, European | Hyoscyamine, scopolamine | Anticholinergic delirium — used by oracles (caution: toxic) |
| **Belladonna** (*Atropa belladonna*) | European | Atropine, scopolamine | Anticholinergic; pupil dilation; "beautiful lady"; toxic |
| **Cannabis** | Universal | THC, CBD, terpenes | Pain, appetite, anxiety, altered consciousness |
| **Fly agaric** (*Amanita muscaria*) | Siberian, Norse, Vedic | Ibotenic acid → muscimol | GABA-A agonist; shamanic visions; muscimol is sleep-enhancing |
| **Blue lotus** (*Nymphaea caerulea*) | Egyptian | Nuciferine, apomorphine | Mild narcotic; lucid dreaming; Nile shamanism |

**Note on the Nightshade family (Solanaceae):** Belladonna, henbane, and mandrake all contain
tropane alkaloids that block muscarinic acetylcholine receptors. These were historically used
in "flying ointments" applied to the skin. Transdermal absorption bypasses first-pass liver
metabolism — making skin application more potent than ingestion. These plants are genuinely
dangerous and are included here for educational/historical purposes only.

## Preparation Methods — Quick Reference

| Method | Best for | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| **Cold infusion** | Mucilaginous herbs (marshmallow, slippery elm), volatile aromatics | No heat to preserve mucilage or volatiles |
| **Hot infusion (tea)** | Leaves, flowers, aromatic herbs | 5-15 min steep; cover to retain volatiles |
| **Decoction** | Roots, bark, seeds, hard plant material | Simmer 20-45 min |
| **Tincture** | Most herbs | Alcohol extracts alkaloids, resins, most active compounds; preserved months-years |
| **Glycerite** | Alcohol-sensitive individuals; children | Vegetable glycerine; lower potency than alcohol |
| **Infused oil** | Fat-soluble compounds; topical use | Calendula, St John's Wort, rosemary in oil |
| **Poultice** | Topical wound/pain application | Fresh plant crushed and applied |
| **Syrup** | Respiratory, children, palatability | Decoction + honey/glycerine |
| **Standardised extract** | Precise therapeutic dosing | Use when specific compound levels matter |
| **Essential oil** | Aromatherapy; topical (diluted) | Never ingest without professional guidance |
