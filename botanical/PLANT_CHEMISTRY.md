# 🧪 Plant Chemistry — The Molecular Language of Plants

> *"The plant does not distinguish between food and medicine.
> Every molecule it makes is both a message to its environment and a gift to those who eat it."*

**Cross-ref:** [BRAIN_PLANTS.md](BRAIN_PLANTS.md) · [CELLULAR_HEALTH.md](CELLULAR_HEALTH.md) · [UNIFIED_THEORY.md](UNIFIED_THEORY.md)

## Overview

Plants produce **over 200,000 known secondary metabolites** — compounds not required for
basic growth and reproduction but serving roles in defence, signalling, attraction, and
stress response. These same molecules interact with human receptors, enzymes, and gene
expression in ways that modern pharmacology is only beginning to map.

The two tiers of plant chemistry:

| Tier | Name | Examples | Purpose in plant |
|------|------|---------|-----------------|
| **Primary metabolites** | Essential molecules | Sugars, amino acids, chlorophyll, ATP | Growth, energy, reproduction |
| **Secondary metabolites** | Specialised compounds | Alkaloids, terpenoids, phenolics | Defence, signalling, UV protection |

## 1 · Alkaloids

Alkaloids are nitrogen-containing compounds with potent biological activity. They typically
act on neurotransmitter systems in animals — which is why they feel so powerful.

### Major classes

| Alkaloid class | Example plants | Key compound | Primary action |
|----------------|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Purine alkaloids** | Coffee, tea, cacao | Caffeine, theobromine | Adenosine antagonist; CNS stimulant |
| **Indole alkaloids** | Periwinkle, ergot, mushrooms | Vincamine, ergotamine, psilocybin | Serotonin/dopamine modulation |
| **Tropane alkaloids** | Belladonna, henbane, coca | Atropine, scopolamine, cocaine | Acetylcholine, dopamine |
| **Isoquinoline alkaloids** | Opium poppy, turmeric, berberine plants | Morphine, berberine, sanguinarine | Opioid receptors, NF-κB inhibition |
| **Pyridine/piperidine** | Black pepper, tobacco, noni | Piperine, nicotine | AChR agonist; bioavailability enhancer |
| **Pyrrolizidine** | Comfrey, borage (caution) | Symphytine, lycopsamine | Anti-herbivory (hepatotoxic in excess) |
| **Quinoline alkaloids** | Cinchona, rue | Quinine | Anti-malarial; ion channel modulator |
| **Steroidal alkaloids** | Potato, tomato (leaves/unripe) | Solanine, tomatine | Anti-fungal defence |

### Herbalist's Note
Most alkaloids are dose-dependent: therapeutic at low dose, toxic at high dose. This is not
unique to alkaloids — it is a universal property of bioactive molecules. The Celtic and
Norse traditions called this "the gift and the goad." Modern pharmacology calls it the
dose-response curve.

## 2 · Terpenoids (Terpenes)

The largest and most structurally diverse class of plant compounds, built from repeating
five-carbon isoprene units. They are responsible for most plant aromas, resins, and essential oils.

### Classification by size

| Class | Carbon # | Examples | Key uses |
|-------|----------|---------|---------|
| **Monoterpenes** | C₁₀ | Limonene, linalool, menthol, thymol | Aromatherapy, antimicrobial, insect repellent |
| **Sesquiterpenes** | C₁₅ | β-Caryophyllene, artemisinin | CB2 receptor agonist, anti-malarial |
| **Diterpenes** | C₂₀ | Taxol, ginkgolides, stevioside | Anti-cancer, anti-platelet, sweetener |
| **Triterpenes** | C₃₀ | Ursolic acid, oleanolic acid, ginsenosides | Adaptogen, anti-inflammatory |
| **Tetraterpenes** | C₄₀ | β-Carotene, lycopene, lutein | Antioxidant, vision, skin |
| **Polyterpenes** | C₂₀₀₀+ | Rubber (latex) | Elasticity, wound sealing |

### Notable terpenoids

**Linalool** (lavender, coriander) — anxiolytic via GABA-A modulation; sedative at dose  
**β-Caryophyllene** (black pepper, cloves, cannabis) — endocannabinoid (CB2) agonist without psychoactivity; potent anti-inflammatory  
**Limonene** (citrus peel) — mood-elevating, anti-anxiety, anti-tumour signalling  
**Artemisinin** (sweet wormwood, *Artemisia annua*) — most effective anti-malarial compound known; reacts with haem in parasites  
**Taxol (paclitaxel)** (Pacific yew, *Taxus brevifolia*) — stabilises microtubules; first-line chemotherapy  
**Ginsenosides** (Panax ginseng) — adaptogenic, modulates hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis

## 3 · Phenolic Compounds

Phenolics are the most abundant class of plant antioxidants. They all share an aromatic ring
with one or more hydroxyl groups — a structure that makes them exceptional free-radical scavengers.

### The phenolic family tree

```
Phenolics
├── Simple phenols (catechol, guaiacol)
├── Phenolic acids
│   ├── Hydroxybenzoic acids (gallic, ellagic, vanillic)
│   └── Hydroxycinnamic acids (caffeic, ferulic, rosmarinic, chlorogenic)
├── Flavonoids (see Section 4)
├── Stilbenes (resveratrol, pterostilbene)
├── Lignans (secoisolariciresinol, matairesinol)
├── Tannins
│   ├── Condensed (proanthocyanidins — grape seeds, pine bark)
│   └── Hydrolysable (gallotannins, ellagitannins — pomegranate, oak gall)
└── Coumarins (umbelliferone, scopoletin, warfarin-precursors)
```

### Key individual phenolics

**Rosmarinic acid** (rosemary, basil, sage, lemon balm) — crosses blood-brain barrier; inhibits AChE; potent neuroprotectant  
**Chlorogenic acid** (coffee, artichoke, potatoes) — slows glucose absorption; anti-diabetic properties  
**Ferulic acid** (feathery grains, asparagus) — UV protectant; crosses BBB; protects dopaminergic neurons  
**Ellagic acid** (pomegranate, walnut, strawberry) — anti-proliferative; metabolised to urolithins by gut flora  
**Resveratrol** (grape skin, Japanese knotweed) — activates sirtuins (SIRT1); anti-ageing, cardioprotective  
**Curcumin** (turmeric) — NF-κB inhibitor; BDNF modulator; anti-amyloid; requires piperine or lipid carrier for bioavailability

## 4 · Flavonoids

Flavonoids are a major subclass of phenolics — over 6,000 identified. They are responsible for
most flower pigmentation (attracting pollinators) and are among the most studied bioactive plant
compounds.

### Flavonoid classes and signature compounds

| Class | Key members | Plant sources | Primary bioactivity |
|-------|-------------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Flavonols** | Quercetin, kaempferol, myricetin | Onions, apples, capers, kale | Anti-inflammatory, antiviral, zinc ionophore |
| **Flavones** | Apigenin, luteolin, chrysin | Chamomile, parsley, artichoke | GABA-A modulator (anxiolytic), anti-spasmodic |
| **Flavanones** | Hesperidin, naringenin | Citrus | Cardiovascular, BBB-penetrant neuroprotection |
| **Flavan-3-ols** | Catechin, EGCG, theaflavins | Green tea, cacao, red wine | Antioxidant, autophagy, metabolic |
| **Isoflavones** | Genistein, daidzein | Soy, red clover | Phytoestrogenic, bone density |
| **Anthocyanins** | Cyanidin, delphinidin, malvidin | Blueberry, elderberry, purple foods | Antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, vision support |
| **Chalcones** | Xanthohumol | Hops | Anti-proliferative, liver protective |

### Quercetin — The Universal Flavonoid
Quercetin deserves special mention. It is:
- A **zinc ionophore** — opens channels that carry zinc (antiviral) into cells
- An **anti-histamine** — stabilises mast cells (allergy and inflammation)
- A **SIRT1 activator** — anti-ageing via epigenetic pathways
- A **senolytic** (in high dose) — clears senescent cells that drive ageing
- A **neuroprotectant** — reduces amyloid aggregation, crosses BBB

**Best food sources:** capers (raw), red onion (outer skin layers), lovage leaves, dill, buckwheat

## 5 · Glucosinolates and Isothiocyanates

Found almost exclusively in the Brassica family (cabbage, broccoli, mustard, rocket/arugula,
wasabi, horseradish). Glucosinolates are stable precursors; when plant tissue is damaged (chewing,
chopping), the enzyme **myrosinase** converts them to the bioactive **isothiocyanates**.

| Compound | Source | Activity |
|---------|--------|---------|
| **Sulforaphane** | Broccoli sprouts (highest), broccoli, cabbage | Nrf2 pathway activator — master antioxidant switch; anti-cancer; neuroprotective; modulates gut microbiome |
| **PEITC** | Watercress, nasturtium | Phase-2 enzyme induction; anti-carcinogenic |
| **Allyl ITC** | Wasabi, mustard, horseradish | Antimicrobial; TRPA1 agonist (heat/pain pathway) |
| **Indole-3-carbinol** | All brassicas | Hormone metabolism; aryl hydrocarbon receptor modulation |

**Practical note:** Lightly steaming Brassicas (< 3 min) preserves myrosinase. Boiling
destroys the enzyme — add raw mustard seeds or radish to boiled brassica dishes to restore
conversion.

## 6 · Polysaccharides and Glycans

Not all plant medicine is small-molecule. Many plants' therapeutic actions come from complex
carbohydrates that modulate the immune system directly.

| Compound | Source | Primary action |
|---------|--------|----------------|
| **Beta-glucans** | Oats, barley, medicinal mushrooms | Immune modulation via Dectin-1; cholesterol reduction |
| **Arabinogalactans** | Larch, echinacea roots | NK cell and macrophage activation |
| **Pectin** | Apple skin, citrus pith | Prebiotic; binds heavy metals; anti-diarrhoeal |
| **Inulin / FOS** | Chicory, Jerusalem artichoke, dahlia, garlic | Bifidogenic prebiotic; blood glucose regulation |
| **Aloe vera polysaccharides** | Aloe | Wound healing, immune modulation, anti-inflammatory |

## 7 · Organosulfur Compounds

Garlic, onions, leeks, and other Alliums produce sulfur compounds that are responsible for
their characteristic smell and potent biological effects.

**Allicin** — forms when garlic is crushed (alliinase converts alliin → allicin); antimicrobial,
antifungal, anti-platelet, vasodilatory. Unstable — use raw, crushed garlic within 10 minutes.

**Ajoene** — formed from allicin; anti-thrombotic, anti-leishmanial, anti-fungal

**DAS / DADS / DATS** (diallyl sulfides) — liver Phase-2 enzyme inducers; anti-carcinogenic

**Sulforaphane** (see Glucosinolates) — also an organosulfur, though from a different pathway

## 8 · Cannabinoids and Endocannabinoid Mimetics

The endocannabinoid system (ECS) is present in all vertebrates. Plants produce compounds
that interact with it — not only cannabis but many other species.

| Compound | Source | Receptor | Effect |
|---------|--------|---------|--------|
| **THC** (Δ9-tetrahydrocannabinol) | *Cannabis sativa* | CB1, CB2 | Psychoactive; analgesic; anti-nausea; appetite |
| **CBD** (cannabidiol) | Cannabis | CB1 modulator, 5HT1A, TRPV1 | Anxiolytic; anti-inflammatory; anti-epileptic |
| **β-Caryophyllene** | Black pepper, cloves, hops, lavender | CB2 (full agonist) | Anti-inflammatory without psychoactivity |
| **Palmitoylethanolamide (PEA)** | Eggs, peanuts, soy | CB2, PPAR-α | Anti-inflammatory; neuroprotective |

## 9 · Adaptogens — A Special Functional Class

Adaptogens are a **functional category**, not a chemical class. A true adaptogen:
1. Is non-toxic at therapeutic doses
2. Produces a non-specific stress-protective effect
3. Has a normalising action regardless of direction of pathology

Chemically, adaptogens typically contain a mixture of terpenoids (ginsenosides, withanolides,
eleutherosides), phenolics, and polysaccharides that act synergistically on the HPA axis,
the sympatho-adrenal system, and mitochondrial function.

| Adaptogen | Key active compounds | Primary application |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Ashwagandha | Withanolides, withaferin A | Cortisol reduction, thyroid support, muscle recovery |
| Panax ginseng | Ginsenosides (Rb1, Rg1) | Energy, immune, cognitive performance |
| Siberian ginseng | Eleutherosides | Endurance, stress resilience |
| Rhodiola rosea | Rosavins, salidroside | Mental fatigue, altitude adaptation, mood |
| Schisandra | Schisandrins | Liver protection, cognitive enhancement |
| Holy basil (Tulsi) | Eugenol, ursolic acid, ocimumosides | Stress, anxiety, blood sugar |
| Reishi mushroom | Triterpenoids, polysaccharides | Immune modulation, anti-tumour, sleep |
| Maca | Glucosinolates, macamides | Hormonal balance, libido, bone density |

## 10 · Phytohormones and Plant Signalling Molecules

Plants produce hormone-like molecules that, in some cases, interact with animal endocrine systems.

| Phytohormone | Sources | Human interaction |
|-------------|---------|------------------|
| **Phytoestrogens** (isoflavones, lignans, coumestans) | Soy, flaxseed, red clover, alfalfa | Weak ER-α and ER-β binding; hormone-modulating |
| **Ecdysteroids** (ecdysterone/20E) | Spinach, quinoa, maral root | Anabolic via ERβ; muscle protein synthesis |
| **Melatonin** | Cherries, walnuts, rice, corn | Direct melatonin receptor agonist; sleep |
| **Plant sterols / stanols** | Nuts, seeds, vegetable oils | Block cholesterol absorption in intestine |
| **Auxin-like compounds** | Various | Weak IGF-1 pathway interactions (under study) |

## The Entourage Effect

Perhaps the most important principle in plant chemistry: **single compounds rarely equal
the whole plant**. The entourage (or matrix) effect describes how the many compounds in a
plant work synergistically — sometimes in ways no individual compound produces alone.

Classic examples:
- Piperine in black pepper increases curcumin bioavailability by 2000%
- Cannabis full-spectrum vs. isolate CBD: full-spectrum has broader anti-anxiety effects
- Whole-food quercetin (with its co-occurring isorhamnetin, kaempferol, rutin) outperforms pure quercetin supplements in many trials
- Lavender essential oil: linalool + linalyl acetate together produce greater sedation than either alone

**Implication for herbalists:** Whole-plant preparations (teas, tinctures, food-as-medicine)
often outperform isolated extracts. Use extracts for specific, high-dose therapeutic applications.

## Safety Principles

1. **Dose makes the poison** — every compound in this guide is dose-dependent
2. **Herb-drug interactions are real** — St John's Wort induces CYP3A4 (reduces effectiveness of many pharmaceuticals); grapefruit inhibits the same enzyme; always check
3. **Quality matters** — adulteration of herbal products is common; use reputable sources, seek standardised extracts for clinical use
4. **Pregnancy and nursing** — many herbs that are safe in food quantities are contraindicated in concentrated forms during pregnancy
5. **Kidney and liver conditions** — always assess these before high-dose botanical protocols
6. **Individual variation** — genetic polymorphisms in drug-metabolising enzymes (CYP450 family) mean responses to botanical medicines vary significantly between individuals
