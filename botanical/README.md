## The Five Principles of This Guide

### 1 · Plants Are Chemical Factories
A plant synthesises thousands of compounds — not by accident but as evolutionary solutions
to pressures of light, drought, herbivory, pollination, and soil chemistry. Every compound
has a function in the plant, and many of those functions mirror our own biology because we
co-evolved with plants across hundreds of millions of years.

### 2 · Soil Is the Foundation
A healthy plant begins underground. The rhizosphere — the zone around roots — is among the
most complex ecosystems on Earth. A teaspoon of living soil contains more organisms than
there are humans on the planet. Without fungal networks, bacterial symbionts, and mineral
availability, even the most powerful medicinal plant produces inferior chemistry.

### 3 · Complexity Is Resilience
Polycultures, companion planting, and genetic diversity mirror the FTUM fixed-point principle:
stable systems are attractors, and diversity is what makes them robust to perturbation —
drought, pests, climate shifts.

### 4 · Traditional Knowledge Is Data
Every herbalist tradition — Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine, Western botanical medicine,
African ethnobotany, Indigenous North American plant knowledge — represents thousands of years
of clinical observation. This guide takes it seriously as data and cross-references it with
modern phytochemistry wherever possible.

### 5 · The Brain–Gut–Root Axis
The gut microbiome, the root microbiome, and the neural networks of the brain are all
variations on the same pattern: distributed networks that process information through molecular
signals. Plants that support one often support all three.
