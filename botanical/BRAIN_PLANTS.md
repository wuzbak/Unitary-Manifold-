## The Brain-Plant Interface

Human neurotransmitter systems — serotonin, dopamine, acetylcholine, GABA, glutamate,
endocannabinoids, adenosine — all have plant-derived molecules that interact with them.
This is not coincidence. We evolved in a world full of plants, eating them, breathing their
volatiles, with skin and gut exposed to their chemistry. Our nervous systems and plant
chemistry co-evolved for hundreds of millions of years.

### Four domains of brain support

| Domain | Goal | Key plants |
|--------|------|-----------|
| **Neuroprotection** | Prevent damage and degeneration | Ginkgo, Bacopa, Lion's Mane, turmeric |
| **Neurogenesis** | Promote new neuron and synapse growth | Lion's Mane, Bacopa, Ashwagandha, Blueberry |
| **Cognitive enhancement** | Memory, focus, processing speed | Rosemary, Bacopa, Ginkgo, Gotu Kola, Panax ginseng |
| **Mood and stress regulation** | Anxiety, depression, emotional resilience | Ashwagandha, Rhodiola, Lavender, St John's Wort, Passionflower |

## Tier 1 — The Evidence-Based Core

These plants have robust human clinical trial data.

---

### 1 · Bacopa monnieri (Brahmi)

**Traditional system:** Ayurveda (medhya rasayana — brain rejuvenator)  
**Key compounds:** Bacosides A and B (dammarane-type triterpenoid saponins)

**Mechanism:**
- Increases ACh synthesis and release in the hippocampus
- Activates tryptophan hydroxylase (serotonin production)
- Reduces stress-induced dopamine and serotonin turnover
- Upregulates BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) — the primary neurotrophin for memory consolidation
- Inhibits acetylcholinesterase (same mechanism as Alzheimer's drugs donepezil/rivastigmine)
- Direct antioxidant protection of hippocampal neurons

**Clinical evidence:**
- Improves working memory, word recall, and cognitive processing speed in healthy adults (double-blind RCTs, 12-week minimum)
- Reduces cortisol levels comparable to low-dose anxiolytics
- Therapeutic in attention deficit presentations in children (multiple RCTs)

**Growing:** Aquatic/semiaquatic perennial, tropical and subtropical. Can be grown indoors
in standing water. Sun to partial shade. Low-maintenance once established.

**Preparation:** Traditionally taken with ghee (fat) to enhance bioavailability. Standardised
extract: 300-450 mg/day of 55% bacosides. Allow 8-12 weeks for cognitive effects.

---

### 2 · Ginkgo biloba (Maidenhair tree)

**Traditional system:** Traditional Chinese Medicine (for circulation and brain)  
**Key compounds:** Ginkgolides (terpene trilactones), bilobalide, quercetin, kaempferol, isorhamnetin

**Mechanism:**
- PAF (platelet-activating factor) antagonist via ginkgolides — reduces micro-clotting in cerebral circulation
- Increases cerebral blood flow (vasodilatation)
- Inhibits monoamine oxidase (MAO-A and MAO-B) mildly — mood and neuroprotection
- Antioxidant: scavenges superoxide, hydroxyl radicals, nitric oxide
- Modulates NMDA receptors and adenosine receptors
- Inhibits amyloid-β aggregation (relevance to Alzheimer's)

**Clinical evidence:**
- Significant improvement in cognitive symptoms in mild-to-moderate dementia (meta-analysis)
- Reduces tinnitus and vertigo (circulation to inner ear)
- Positive effects in age-related cognitive decline; mixed evidence in healthy young adults

**Caution:** Inhibits platelet aggregation — discontinue 2 weeks before surgery.
Interaction with anticoagulants (warfarin, aspirin). Ginkgo seeds are toxic — use leaf extracts.

**Growing:** Extraordinarily hardy (USDA zone 3-8), pest-resistant, pollution-tolerant.
Long-lived tree (1,000+ years). Male cultivars preferred (female fruit is malodorous).

---

### 3 · Lion's Mane Mushroom (*Hericium erinaceus*)

**Traditional system:** Traditional Chinese and Japanese medicine  
**Key compounds:** Hericenones (from fruiting body), erinacines (from mycelium), β-glucans

**Mechanism:**
- **Erinacines A, B, C** are the most potent known natural inducers of **Nerve Growth Factor (NGF)**
- NGF is essential for maintenance, survival, and regrowth of neurons — especially cholinergic neurons lost in Alzheimer's disease
- Erinacine A crosses the blood-brain barrier and induces NGF synthesis in the brain
- Stimulates **BDNF** (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) — promotes neuroplasticity
- Anti-inflammatory: suppresses NF-κB activation in microglia
- Remyelination: promotes myelin repair around nerve axons

**Clinical evidence:**
- RCT (2009, 2019): significant improvement in mild cognitive impairment scores
- Case reports and pilot studies: reduced anxiety and depression symptoms
- Animal studies: nerve regeneration after peripheral nerve injury

**Growing:** Grows on hardwood (oak, beech, walnut). Log inoculation or grow kit. Prefers
cool, humid conditions (15-20°C). Fruiting in autumn and spring. Can be grown indoors.

**Preparation:** Fresh mushroom sautéed in butter/oil. Dual extract (hot water + alcohol)
for both erinacines and β-glucans. Mycelium extract for highest erinacine content.

---

### 4 · Ashwagandha (*Withania somnifera*)

**Traditional system:** Ayurveda (rasayana — rejuvenating tonic)  
**Key compounds:** Withanolides (withanolide A, withaferin A, withasomnine), alkaloids, sitoindosides

**Mechanism:**
- **Cortisol reduction**: inhibits HPA axis over-activation — reduces serum cortisol by 14-32% in clinical trials
- **GABA-A modulation**: triethylene glycol (specific fraction) is the primary sleep-inducing component
- **Thyroid support**: mild T3 and T4 elevation
- Increases acetylcholine receptor density in cortex
- Promotes neurogenesis in the hippocampus via BDNF upregulation
- **Anti-amyloid**: withaferin A disaggregates amyloid-β and tau tangles in vitro
- **Anabolic**: increases testosterone in sub-fertile men and reduces exercise-induced cortisol spike

**Clinical evidence:**
- Robust RCT data on cortisol reduction and perceived stress
- Significant sleep improvement (KSM-66 and Sensoril extracts)
- Improved memory and cognitive function in adults with MCI (2017 RCT)
- Improved endurance and muscle recovery in athletes

**Growing:** Hardy perennial shrub in USDA zones 7-10. Drought-tolerant once established.
Full sun, well-drained soil. Roots harvested in winter (year 2-3 for best potency).

---

### 5 · Rosemary (*Salvia rosmarinus*)

**Traditional system:** Mediterranean folk medicine, Western herbalism  
**Key compounds:** Rosmarinic acid, carnosic acid, carnosol, ursolic acid, 1,8-cineole (borneol)

**Mechanism:**
- **1,8-Cineole** (inhaled) — inhibits AChE directly; increases cerebral blood flow. Even smelling rosemary improves working memory scores
- **Rosmarinic acid** — crosses BBB; inhibits AChE and MAO; potent antioxidant; anti-inflammatory via NF-κB inhibition
- **Carnosic acid** — activates Nrf2 pathway (master antioxidant switch); neuroprotective after ischaemia
- **Ursolic acid** — BDNF-mimetic; promotes neurogenesis

**Use:** Fresh or dried herb in food. Essential oil diffused (proven effective for memory in exam settings). Tincture or tea for therapeutic doses.

**Growing:** USDA zones 7-11, tender in colder climates (grow in pots to bring indoors).
Full sun, excellent drainage, drought-tolerant. One of the easiest aromatic herbs to grow.

---

### 6 · Gotu Kola (*Centella asiatica*)

**Traditional system:** Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine, Balinese medicine  
**Key compounds:** Asiaticoside, asiatic acid, madecassoside, madecassic acid (triterpenoid saponins)

**Mechanism:**
- Enhances GABA receptor sensitivity (anxiolytic without sedation)
- Upregulates BDNF and NGF synthesis
- Promotes **axonal and dendritic growth** — actual structural remodelling of neurons
- Improves cerebral circulation
- Collagen synthesis stimulation (relevant to nerve repair and skin)
- Anti-amyloid: inhibits β-secretase (BACE1)

**Clinical evidence:**
- Significant reduction in anxiety with lower acoustic startle response
- Improved memory in elderly adults (RCT)
- Positive wound healing and vein/circulation support (separate from CNS)

**Growing:** Tropical/subtropical ground cover. Prefers moist, partially shaded conditions.
Spreads by runners. Can be grown indoors in shallow trays with moist potting mix.

## Tier 2 — Traditional Nervines and Anxiolytics

These plants are deeply embedded in herbal tradition and have strong mechanistic data,
if less clinical trial data than Tier 1.

---

### 7 · Passionflower (*Passiflora incarnata*)

**Compounds:** Chrysin (flavone), vitexin, isovitexin, maltol  
**Mechanism:** Chrysin is a GABA-A positive allosteric modulator (benzodiazepine-like);
also inhibits MAO-A and MAO-B mildly  
**Use:** Anxiety, insomnia, nervous restlessness, opiate withdrawal support  
**Growing:** Climbing perennial vine, USDA zones 6-10, full sun, well-drained soil, vigorous grower

---

### 8 · Valerian (*Valeriana officinalis*)

**Compounds:** Valerenic acid, valerenol, GABA (paradoxically present in root), isovaleric acid  
**Mechanism:** Valerenic acid binds GABA-A receptor β-subunit; inhibits GABA transaminase  
**Use:** Insomnia (sleep onset and quality), anxiety, nervous tremor  
**Note:** Works better with consistent use; "paradoxical stimulation" in ~10% of users  
**Growing:** Hardy perennial, USDA zones 4-9, moist rich soil, full sun to partial shade

---

### 9 · Lemon Balm (*Melissa officinalis*)

**Compounds:** Rosmarinic acid, apigenin, luteolin, eugenol, geraniol  
**Mechanism:** Inhibits GABA transaminase (increases GABA levels); AChE inhibition;
antiviral (particularly herpes simplex)  
**Use:** Anxiety, mild depression, insomnia, cognitive function in Alzheimer's (positive trial)  
**Growing:** Very easy perennial, USDA zones 4-9, any soil, spreads readily — container recommended

---

### 10 · St John's Wort (*Hypericum perforatum*)

**Compounds:** Hypericin, pseudohypericin (naphthodianthrones), hyperforin, quercetin, rutin  
**Mechanism:** Hyperforin inhibits reuptake of serotonin, dopamine, noradrenaline, GABA, and
glutamate simultaneously (unique mechanism unlike SSRIs)  
**Clinical evidence:** Strong evidence for mild-to-moderate depression — comparable to SSRIs
in multiple large RCTs with fewer sexual side effects  
**Caution:** Potent inducer of CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein — reduces plasma levels of:
oral contraceptives, antivirals, warfarin, cyclosporine, digoxin. Do NOT combine without review.  
**Light sensitivity:** Hypericin is phototoxic at high doses — fair-skinned individuals beware  
**Growing:** USDA zones 5-9, full sun, well-drained soil, perennial, spreads by seed

---

### 11 · Lavender (*Lavandula angustifolia*)

**Compounds:** Linalool (25-38%), linalyl acetate (25-45%), camphor, 1,8-cineole  
**Mechanism:** Linalool modulates GABA-A receptors; inhibits glutamate NMDA receptors;
reduces cortisol via HPA axis modulation  
**Clinical evidence:** Silexan (oral lavender oil, 80mg) proven equal to lorazepam for
generalised anxiety disorder in multiple RCTs — without dependence or sedation at that dose  
**Aromatherapy note:** Inhaled linalool measurably reduces cortisol and heart rate
(effective dose requires 15+ min exposure)  
**Growing:** USDA zones 5-9, full sun, alkaline well-drained soil, drought-tolerant

## Tier 3 — Entheogenic and Visionary Plants

*Included for completeness and research relevance. These plants have powerful neurological
effects and require careful context, set, and setting. Many are legally restricted.*

---

### 12 · Psilocybin Mushrooms (*Psilocybe* spp.)

**Compounds:** Psilocybin → psilocin (active); also baeocystin, norbaeocystin  
**Mechanism:** Psilocin is a full agonist at 5-HT2A and 5-HT2C serotonin receptors;
also activates 5-HT1A (anxiolytic) and 5-HT2B (trophic/neuroplastic)  
**Effects:** Increased neuroplasticity; disruption and reset of default mode network (DMN)
— the "ego network." Single supervised dose produces sustained personality change
(increased openness) for 12+ months.  
**Clinical evidence:** Johns Hopkins, NYU, Imperial College London: psilocybin therapy produces
rapid, durable remission of treatment-resistant depression (60-80% response rates),
end-of-life anxiety, and tobacco/alcohol addiction  
**BDNF and neurogenesis:** Psilocybin upregulates BDNF and promotes dendritic spine growth
at sub-perceptual (microdose) levels  
**Safety:** Physiologically safe; no lethal dose established; not physically addictive.
Psychological risks (challenging experiences) require appropriate set/setting/support.

---

### 13 · Kava (*Piper methysticum*)

**Compounds:** Kavalactones (kavain, dihydrokavain, methysticin, dihydromethysticin, yangonin)  
**Mechanism:** GABA-A potentiation; noradrenaline reuptake inhibition; sodium/calcium channel
blockade; dopaminergic effects; monoamine oxidase inhibition  
**Use:** Anxiety, insomnia, social anxiety — without impairing cognition at moderate doses  
**Clinical evidence:** Significant anxiolytic effect compared to placebo (meta-analysis);
comparable to buspirone for GAD  
**Safety:** Noble kava varieties are safe when prepared traditionally (aqueous extract of root).
Hepatotoxicity risk with solvent-extracted products or aerial parts. Do not combine with alcohol.  
**Growing:** Tropical (warm, humid, partial shade). Not frost-tolerant.

## The BDNF Protocol — Growing a Bigger Brain

BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) is the primary protein that grows new neurons and
synaptic connections. Botanical ways to increase it:

1. **Bacopa monnieri** — increases BDNF mRNA and protein expression
2. **Lion's Mane mushroom** — NGF induction, also increases BDNF
3. **Blueberries (pterostilbene, anthocyanins)** — directly upregulate BDNF expression
4. **Turmeric (curcumin + piperine)** — increases BDNF, reduces amyloid
5. **Ashwagandha** — cortisol reduction indirectly rescues BDNF (high cortisol suppresses BDNF)
6. **Resveratrol** — activates SIRT1 which upregulates BDNF
7. **Green tea EGCG** — promotes hippocampal neurogenesis via BDNF
8. **Physical exercise** (bonus: not a plant) — strongest BDNF inducer known

## The Vagus Nerve and the Gut–Brain Connection

The vagus nerve carries 80% of its signals from gut to brain (not the other way around).
The enteric nervous system has more neurons than the spinal cord. Plants that support the gut
microbiome directly support brain function:

- **Prebiotic fibres** (inulin, FOS from chicory, garlic, Jerusalem artichoke) — feed SCFA-producing bacteria that upregulate BDNF and serotonin production
- **Fermented plant foods** (sauerkraut, kimchi, miso, tempeh) — probiotic sources; metabolites cross the BBB
- **Ginger** — 5-HT3 antagonist in the gut (reduces nausea), reduces neuroinflammation
- **Peppermint** — activates cold/menthol TRPM8 channels in gut vagal afferents; anti-spasmodic
- **Chamomile** — apigenin binds central benzodiazepine receptors; also modulates gut inflammation
