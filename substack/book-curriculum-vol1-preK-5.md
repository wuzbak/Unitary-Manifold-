This curriculum guide is for **everyone**: parents, teachers, school administrators, curriculum designers, tutors, home-schoolers, researchers, and policymakers. ## A Note on the Formal Vocabulary

A small number of terms used in this guide require brief definition:

**Phonemic awareness:** The ability to hear, identify, and manipulate individual sounds (phonemes) in spoken words. This is an auditory skill that precedes reading.

**Phonics:** The systematic correspondence between written letters or letter groups and spoken sounds. The foundation of decoding skill.

**Number sense:** Intuitive understanding of numbers — their size, their relationships, their behavior in operations — as distinct from memorized procedures.

**Executive function:** The set of cognitive skills — working memory, cognitive flexibility, inhibitory control — that allow a person to plan, focus, follow multi-step instructions, and regulate behavior. Develops substantially in the preschool and early elementary years.

**Formative assessment:** Assessment whose primary purpose is to inform instruction — to find out what students know and where they are confused, in order to adjust teaching. Distinguished from summative assessment, which evaluates performance at the end of a unit.

**Mastery learning:** An approach in which students are assessed repeatedly and corrected until they demonstrate mastery of each concept before moving to the next.

**Scaffolding:** Temporary instructional support — hints, worked examples, frameworks — that helps a student accomplish a task they cannot yet do independently, and that is gradually removed as competence grows.

## DEVELOPMENTAL FOUNDATION: WHAT EVERY EDUCATOR SHOULD KNOW

Before entering the grade-by-grade content, three foundational concepts from developmental science should orient every educator working with children ages 3-11.

### The Brain in the Early Years

Human brain development is more rapid and more experience-dependent in the first decade of life than at any subsequent period. The National Scientific Council on the Developing Child at Harvard University has synthesized decades of neuroscience in a series of working papers (available at developingchild.harvard.edu) documenting that:

- **Neural circuits are built bottom-up, and early circuits form the foundation for later ones.** Sensory processing pathways are established first; then motor systems; then cognitive and emotional regulation systems. Building higher-order skills on top of weak foundational circuits is less efficient and less effective than building the circuits in proper sequence.
- **Serve-and-return interactions are the active ingredient of early development.** When an infant or young child "serves" — babbles, reaches, looks — and an adult "returns" — responds contingently — the neural circuits for language, social cognition, and emotional regulation are activated and strengthened. Environments that are chaotic, unresponsive, or threatening disrupt this architecture.
- **Toxic stress has biological effects.** Chronic adversity that activates the stress response system repeatedly — without a buffering, regulated adult relationship — produces physiological changes (elevated cortisol, altered hippocampal development, reduced prefrontal engagement) that impair the cognitive and regulatory capacities required for learning.

Source: Center on the Developing Child, Harvard University (2010). *The Foundations of Lifelong Health Are Built in Early Childhood.*

### Executive Function: The Skill Behind the Skills

Executive function — the set of cognitive skills governing working memory, cognitive flexibility, and inhibitory control — is a stronger predictor of kindergarten readiness than IQ or early reading and mathematics scores (Blair & Razza, 2007, *Child Development*). Executive function development is most rapid between ages 3 and 7 and continues developing into early adulthood.

**Working memory:** Holding and manipulating information in mind over short periods — the capacity required to follow multi-step instructions, solve multi-step problems, and compose connected sentences.

**Cognitive flexibility:** Shifting attention and strategy in response to changing demands — the capacity required to move between different types of problems, consider different perspectives, and adapt to unexpected information.

**Inhibitory control:** Suppressing a dominant response in favor of a more appropriate one — the capacity required to raise a hand before speaking, to check an answer before submitting it, and to persist on a difficult task without seeking immediate gratification.

Executive function develops through play (especially fantasy play), physical activity, music instruction, and structured experiences that require planning and self-regulation (Diamond & Lee, 2011, *Science*). It is teachable — and the evidence for programs like Tools of the Mind, which explicitly trains executive function through play-based activities, is strong (Bodrova & Leong, 2007; Barnett et al. 2008, *Developmental Psychology*).

### The Role of Language in All Learning

Language is the medium through which virtually all formal learning occurs. A child's vocabulary at kindergarten entry is one of the strongest predictors of reading comprehension in subsequent grades (Cunningham & Stanovich, 1997, *American Educator*). Vocabulary does not develop primarily through direct instruction of word lists; it develops through exposure to rich, connected discourse — conversation, read-alouds, discussion, storytelling — in which words are encountered in meaningful context repeatedly over time.

**Implications for every subject area:** Science, mathematics, social studies, and arts instruction all contribute to vocabulary development — not as a side effect but as a central mechanism. A child who learns the words "evaporate," "condense," "precipitate" in a science unit on the water cycle is not just learning science; they are building the academic vocabulary register that reading comprehension requires. Subject-area vocabulary instruction is language instruction.

## CHAPTER 1: PRESCHOOL / PREKINDERGARTEN (Ages 3–5)

### 1.1 Developmental Overview

Children ages 3-5 are engaged in the most intensive period of brain development that will occur after birth. They are acquiring language at a rate of approximately 5-10 new words per day (Beck, McKeown & Kucan, 2013, *Bringing Words to Life*), developing theory of mind (the capacity to understand that other people have mental states different from their own — a milestone typically reached between ages 3 and 5), and building the foundational executive function skills through play.

**What children this age are ready to learn:**
- Language and vocabulary through rich conversation and story
- Number concepts through concrete manipulation of objects
- Foundational scientific inquiry through observation and questioning
- Artistic expression through drawing, singing, movement, and dramatic play
- Social skills through structured and unstructured play with peers
- Self-regulation through predictable routines and gentle guidance

**What children this age are not ready for:**
- Formal reading instruction that requires prolonged sitting and abstract letter-sound work disconnected from meaningful language (evidence from Elkind 2007, *The Power of Play*, and Golinkoff et al. 2017, *Becoming Brilliant*)
- Standardized assessment under time pressure
- Prolonged periods of passive listening

The most comprehensive guidance on PreK practice is the National Association for the Education of Young Children (NAEYC)'s position statement on **Developmentally Appropriate Practice (DAP)**, most recently updated in 2022 (NAEYC, *Developmentally Appropriate Practice in Early Childhood Programs Serving Children from Birth Through Age 8*, 4th ed., 2022). DAP frames effective practice as responsive to what children are developmentally ready for — neither under-challenging (letting children be idle) nor over-challenging (imposing developmentally inappropriate demands).

### 1.2 Mathematics: PreK

**Standards reference:** NAEYC & NCTM joint position statement (2010), *Early Childhood Mathematics: Promoting Good Beginnings*; Head Start Early Learning Outcomes Framework (2015) Mathematics domain.

**Core Concepts at PreK:**

**Counting and Number:**
- **Counting sequence:** Children learn the oral counting sequence to 20 and beyond. This begins as a recitation (like a song) and develops into understanding that each number word corresponds to one object (one-to-one correspondence) and that the last number said tells "how many" (cardinality).
- **Subitizing:** The ability to instantly recognize small quantities (1-4) without counting. Research by Clements and Sarama (2009, *Learning and Teaching Early Math*, Routledge) identifies subitizing as a foundational component of number sense, developed through pattern recognition games with dot cards.
- **Comparing quantities:** More, fewer, the same. Children at this age can compare sets by matching one-to-one before they can do so numerically.

**Shapes and Spatial Sense:**
- Naming and sorting basic 2D shapes (circle, square, triangle, rectangle) and 3D shapes (sphere, cube, cylinder)
- Recognizing shapes in the environment
- Using spatial language: above, below, inside, outside, next to, behind

**Patterns:**
- Creating and extending simple AB patterns (red-blue, red-blue) with physical objects, sounds, and movement
- Recognizing that patterns repeat

**Measurement (informal):**
- Comparing length, weight, and capacity directly: "Which block is longer?" "Which cup holds more?"
- Using non-standard units: "How many hands wide is the table?"

**Instructional Approach:** All mathematics at PreK should be embedded in concrete, hands-on activity and play. The Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract (CPA) progression (Bruner, 1966, *Toward a Theory of Instruction*) begins with the concrete stage: physical objects, not worksheets. Mathematical conversations — "How did you know there were three?" "What would happen if we added one more?" — develop mathematical language and reasoning simultaneously.

**Key Resource:** *Big Math for Little Kids* (Greenes et al., 2004, Pearson Learning Group); *Clements, D.H. & Sarama, J. (2009). Learning and Teaching Early Math: The Learning Trajectories Approach. Routledge.*

### 1.3 Language and Literacy: PreK

**Standards reference:** Common Core State Standards Kindergarten Foundations (for progression context); Head Start Early Learning Outcomes Framework (2015) Literacy domain; NAEYC Joint Statement with IRA on Learning to Read and Write (1998).

**Oral Language:**
- Listening comprehension: Following 2-3 step directions; understanding stories read aloud; answering questions about stories
- Expressive vocabulary: Using an expanding vocabulary to describe experiences, ask questions, and retell stories
- Conversation: Taking turns in dialogue; staying on topic; using complete sentences

**The daily read-aloud** is the single highest-leverage language activity in PreK. Reading aloud with discussion — pausing to explain vocabulary, ask predictive questions, connect to children's experience — develops listening comprehension, vocabulary, and narrative understanding simultaneously. Beck and McKeown (2001, "Text Talk: Capturing the Benefits of Read-Aloud Experiences for Young Children," *The Reading Teacher*) developed the "Text Talk" approach, which uses structured discussion during read-alouds to develop Tier 2 vocabulary (words like "enormous," "reluctant," "transform" that are important across academic contexts).

**Print Awareness:**
- Understanding that print carries meaning; that books are read left-to-right, top-to-bottom in English; that letters are different from numbers
- Recognizing their own name in print
- Distinguishing letters from words from sentences

**Phonological Awareness** (auditory — no letters required):
- Rhyme recognition and production: "Cat rhymes with hat. What else rhymes with cat?"
- Syllable segmentation: Clapping syllables in words (ta-ble = 2 claps)
- Onset-rime: Identifying the beginning sound of a word ("Bus starts with /b/")
- Phoneme isolation: beginning in late PreK, identifying the first sound in simple words

**Note on phonics:** Explicit letter-sound correspondence (phonics) is appropriately introduced in Kindergarten and Grade 1, not in PreK, for most children. PreK's contribution to reading development is phonological awareness, oral vocabulary, and print awareness — the prerequisites for phonics instruction to be productive.

**Writing (emergent):**
- Drawing to represent ideas and experiences
- Dictating sentences to an adult scribe
- Experimenting with marks and letter-like forms; beginning to write their name

### 1.4 Science: PreK

**Standards reference:** NGSS Appendix G (Crosscutting Concepts — patterns, cause and effect, structure and function begin here); *A Framework for K-12 Science Education* (NRC, 2012), Chapter 10 (PreK guidance).

PreK science is **observation, questioning, and wonder.** It is not the transmission of scientific facts but the cultivation of scientific habits of mind: noticing, asking "why?", making predictions, testing ideas, and sharing observations.

**Life Science:**
- Observing plants and animals in the local environment
- Understanding that living things need food, water, and air; that they grow and change
- Comparing and sorting living and non-living things
- Simple life cycles: caterpillar → chrysalis → butterfly; seed → seedling → plant

**Earth Science:**
- Weather observation: tracking weather patterns over days and weeks; understanding that weather changes
- Seasons: what changes across seasons in the local environment
- Rocks, soil, and water as earth materials

**Physical Science:**
- Properties of materials: sorting objects by color, texture, weight, hardness
- Simple machines in everyday life: ramps, levers, wheels
- Light and shadow: exploring how shadows form and change

**Instructional Approach:** Science at PreK is primarily **inquiry-based exploration** guided by the teacher's questions. The 5E model (Engage, Explore, Explain, Elaborate, Evaluate) developed by Roger Bybee (1997, *Achieving Scientific Literacy*) provides a useful framework even at PreK. Teachers act as co-investigators, modeling the scientific habit of making observations before making claims.

### 1.5 Social Studies: PreK

**Standards reference:** NCSS (National Council for the Social Studies) C3 Framework for Social Studies (2013); Head Start Early Learning Outcomes Framework Social Studies domain.

**Self, Family, and Community:**
- Identity: Who am I? What makes me unique? What is my family like?
- Family structures: Families look different — different compositions, traditions, languages, configurations. All are valid.
- Rules: Why do we have rules? How do rules help us live together?
- Helpers in the community: roles of different community workers

**Geography:**
- Location: Here, there, near, far; my school, my neighborhood, my town
- Maps as representations: simple maps of the classroom and school
- Land and water: the world has both; basic globe awareness

**History:**
- Personal timeline: past, present, future in their own life; "I used to be a baby; now I am 4; when I am 5 I will go to kindergarten"
- Family history: stories about the past from family members; photos as historical documents

### 1.6 Arts: PreK

**Standards reference:** National Core Arts Standards (NCAS, 2014); *Eisner, E.W. (2002). The Arts and the Creation of Mind. Yale University Press.*

The arts in PreK are not enrichment extras — they are primary cognitive and developmental activities. Drawing, painting, singing, dancing, and dramatic play develop visual-spatial reasoning, fine motor skills, phonological awareness (through song), narrative understanding (through dramatic play), emotional regulation (through creative expression), and mathematical thinking (through pattern, rhythm, and spatial arrangement).

**Visual Arts:**
- Drawing as thinking: children's drawings are their theories about the world made visible
- Exploring materials: paint, clay, collage, found objects — developing fine motor control and aesthetic judgment
- Looking at art: discussing colors, shapes, feelings, stories in works of art (museum visits, art books, classroom prints)
- Key: Process over product. The goal is exploration and developing the habit of looking carefully and making intentional marks.

**Music:**
- Singing: daily singing develops phonological awareness, memory, community, and the physical experience of rhythm and melody. Research by Anvari et al. (2002, *Brain and Cognition*) documents a relationship between music skills and phonological awareness.
- Rhythm: clapping, drumming, moving to a beat — the physical experience of pattern in time
- Listening: distinguishing loud/soft, fast/slow, high/low — building the perceptual vocabulary for musical engagement
- Simple instruments: drums, shakers, xylophones — developing control and listening simultaneously

**Drama and Movement:**
- Dramatic play: children play house, restaurant, hospital, school — developing narrative understanding, perspective-taking, and language
- Structured movement games: Simon Says, freeze dance, Hokey Pokey — developing body awareness, following directions, and inhibitory control
- Simple puppetry and storytelling with props

**Dance:**
- Moving expressively to music; developing body coordination, spatial awareness, and the understanding that the body is an expressive instrument

### 1.7 Physical Education and Health: PreK

**Standards reference:** SHAPE America National Standards and Grade-Level Outcomes for K-12 Physical Education (2014); CDC Healthy Schools guidance.

Physical development in PreK includes both **gross motor** (running, jumping, climbing, throwing, catching) and **fine motor** (drawing, cutting, building with small blocks) skills. Physical activity in early childhood is not separate from cognitive development; aerobic exercise increases BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), which supports hippocampal growth and memory consolidation (Ratey & Hagerman, 2008, *Spark*).

**Daily physical activity target:** At least 60 minutes of total physical activity per day, including structured and unstructured activity (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2011).

**Health concepts:**
- Washing hands: why and how
- Healthy foods vs. "sometimes foods" — building healthy eating habits without food anxiety
- Sleep: children ages 3-5 need 10-13 hours per day (AAP, 2016)
- Bodies: understanding the basic functions of their own body; appropriate touch vs. inappropriate touch (safety education)

### 1.8 Social-Emotional Learning: PreK

**Standards reference:** CASEL (Collaborative for Academic, Social, and Emotional Learning) Framework (2020); *Denham, S.A. (1998). Emotional Development in Young Children. Guilford Press.*

SEL at PreK focuses on building the foundational capacities for healthy social and emotional development:

- **Self-awareness:** Naming emotions; understanding that feelings have causes; "I feel angry because..."
- **Self-regulation:** Calming strategies (deep breathing, the "turtle technique," seeking a trusted adult); the concept of impulse control
- **Social awareness:** Recognizing feelings in others; simple perspective-taking; understanding that others' experiences differ from their own
- **Relationship skills:** Taking turns; sharing; asking for what you need with words; listening when others speak
- **Responsible decision-making:** Simple problem-solving: "What can I do when I am angry? What are the consequences of hitting vs. using my words?"

**Evidence-based PreK SEL curricula:**
- **Tools of the Mind** (Bodrova & Leong): play-based executive function and self-regulation development; strong RCT evidence (Barnett et al., 2008)
- **Preschool PATHS** (Promoting Alternative Thinking Strategies): emotion recognition and problem-solving; evaluated in multiple RCTs (Domitrovich et al., 2007, *Early Education and Development*)
- **Second Step Early Learning**: emotion skills, attention, and friendship skills; widely used

### 1.9 Assessment at PreK

**What not to do:** Standardized pencil-and-paper tests with time limits. These measure test-taking skills, not learning. They produce anxiety and negative school associations in 3-5-year-olds with no compensating benefit.

**What to do:**
- **Observation and documentation:** Teachers systematically observe and record children's behavior, language, and work over time. The **Work Sampling System** (Meisels et al., 2001) and **Teaching Strategies GOLD** are evidence-based observational assessment systems widely used in PreK.
- **Portfolio assessment:** Collecting samples of children's drawing, writing, and verbal performance over time to document growth
- **Developmental screening:** The ASQ-3 (Ages & Stages Questionnaires, 3rd ed., Squires & Bricker, 2009) is a widely validated screening tool for identifying children who may need further developmental evaluation

## CHAPTER 2: KINDERGARTEN (Age 5–6)

### 2.1 Developmental Overview

Kindergarten children are in the midst of a critical transition: from primarily play-based, egocentric learning to increasingly structured, collaborative, and symbol-using learning. The brain's prefrontal cortex — the seat of executive function — is developing rapidly; the development of inhibitory control (the capacity to suppress an impulse) is visible in classic tasks like the marshmallow test (Mischel, Shoda & Rodriguez, 1989, *Science*) and strongly predictive of later academic and life outcomes.

**Key developmental characteristics at age 5-6:**
- Reading readiness: many children are ready to begin systematic phonics instruction; some are not yet developmentally ready and will benefit from continued phonological awareness work
- Number sense developing rapidly: understanding of the relationship between quantity and numerals; beginning addition and subtraction with small numbers
- Play remains essential: 60-minute play periods remain developmentally appropriate and cognitively valuable
- Social development: increasingly able to see others' perspectives; beginning to develop genuine friendships; gender self-concept crystallizing

**Reference:** Dombro, A.L., Jablon, J. & Stetson, C. (2011). *Powerful Interactions: How to Connect with Children to Extend Their Learning*. NAEYC.

---

### 2.2 Mathematics: Kindergarten

**Standards reference:** Common Core State Standards — Mathematics, Kindergarten (CCSSM, 2010); NCTM *Principles to Actions* (2014).

**Counting and Cardinality:**
- Count to 100 by ones and by tens
- Count forward from a given number
- Recognize, write, and represent numbers 0-20 with objects, drawings, and numerals
- Understand that the number of objects is the same regardless of arrangement or counting order
- Compare groups of objects: greater than, less than, equal to

**Operations and Algebraic Thinking:**
- Represent addition as "putting together" and "adding to" with objects, fingers, mental images, and equations
- Represent subtraction as "taking apart" and "taking from"
- Decompose numbers less than or equal to 10 into pairs in more than one way (7 = 3 + 4 = 2 + 5 = 1 + 6)
- For any number from 1-9, find the complement to 10 ("What goes with 3 to make 10?")

**Number and Operations in Base Ten:**
- Compose and decompose numbers 11-19 as "ten ones and some ones" — the foundational concept of place value

**Measurement and Data:**
- Directly compare two objects with a measurable attribute (length, weight)
- Classify objects into categories and count the numbers in each category

**Geometry:**
- Describe, analyze, compare, and sort 2D and 3D shapes
- Compose simple shapes to form larger shapes

**Research Base:** The National Research Council's *Adding It Up* (Kilpatrick, Swafford & Findell, 2001, National Academy Press) identifies five strands of mathematical proficiency — conceptual understanding, procedural fluency, strategic competence, adaptive reasoning, and productive disposition — as intertwined goals, not a sequence. Kindergarten instruction should develop all five simultaneously rather than treating procedural fluency as the only goal.

### 2.3 English Language Arts: Kindergarten

**Standards reference:** CCSS ELA Standards — Kindergarten (2010); National Reading Panel (2000); Scarborough's Reading Rope model (Scarborough, 2001, in *Handbook of Early Literacy Research*, Guilford Press).

**Phonological Awareness:**
- Recognize and produce rhyming words
- Count, pronounce, and blend syllables in spoken words
- Blend and segment onsets and rimes in spoken words
- Add or substitute individual phonemes (sounds) in simple words

**Phonics and Word Recognition:**
- Demonstrate understanding of one-to-one letter-sound correspondence
- Associate each consonant with its most common sound
- Distinguish long and short vowel sounds in simple words
- Read high-frequency words by sight ("the," "I," "a," "and," "is," "in")
- Decode simple CVC words (cat, sit, map)

**Fluency:**
- Read emergent-reader texts with purpose and understanding (at this stage, mostly supported by pictures and memorized text)

**Comprehension:**
- Ask and answer questions about key details in texts read aloud
- Identify the main topic and key details of informational texts
- Retell stories, including key details

**Writing:**
- Use a combination of drawing, dictating, and writing to compose simple texts
- Write letters for most consonant and short-vowel sounds
- Write own name and simple words

**Language:**
- Use frequently occurring nouns, verbs, adjectives, and prepositions correctly
- Form regular plural nouns correctly (cat → cats)
- Capitalize the first word in a sentence and the pronoun "I"

**Note on the Science of Reading:** The strongest consensus in reading research supports systematic, explicit phonics instruction beginning in Kindergarten (National Reading Panel, 2000; Kilpatrick, 2015, *Essentials of Assessing, Preventing, and Overcoming Reading Difficulties*, Wiley). This does not mean that phonics is all that matters: vocabulary, background knowledge, listening comprehension, and motivation are equally important for the full development of skilled reading. The "Reading Rope" model (Scarborough, 2001) presents word recognition (phonics/decoding) and language comprehension as two intertwined strands that together constitute reading comprehension.

### 2.4 Science: Kindergarten

**Standards reference:** Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS, 2013) — Kindergarten Performance Expectations; *A Framework for K-12 Science Education* (NRC, 2012).

**NGSS Kindergarten Topics:**
- **Forces and Motion (Physical Science):** Pushes and pulls — describing the strength and direction of forces; how pushes and pulls change the speed and direction of objects
- **Weather and Climate (Earth Science):** Observing, recording, and predicting local weather patterns across seasons; types of weather (sunny, cloudy, rainy, snowy) and how weather affects daily life
- **Living Things and Their Needs (Life Science):** All plants and animals need food, water, and air; plants need sunlight; living things and their needs in different habitats

**Science and Engineering Practices (introduced):**
- Asking questions and defining problems
- Planning and carrying out investigations
- Analyzing and interpreting data
- Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information

**Crosscutting Concepts (introduced):**
- Patterns: patterns in nature, weather, and the behavior of objects
- Cause and Effect: events have causes; relationships between cause and effect can be observed

### 2.5 Social Studies: Kindergarten

**Standards reference:** NCSS C3 Framework for Social Studies Standards (2013); *National Geography Standards* (National Geographic Society, 2012).

- **Civics:** Rules and laws; why we have rules; how rules are made in the classroom, school, and community; democratic processes (class voting)
- **Economics:** Needs and wants; goods and services; the concept of scarcity; jobs and what people do to earn money
- **Geography:** Mapping the classroom and school; cardinal directions (north, south, east, west); land and water on a globe; the community and its features
- **History:** Using calendars; understanding past and present; learning about holidays and their origins; family traditions and cultural heritage

### 2.6 Arts: Kindergarten

**Music:**
- Singing songs with expanding range and complexity; call-and-response songs; multicultural songs
- Keeping a steady beat; identifying beat vs. rhythm
- High/low pitch; loud/soft dynamics; fast/slow tempo
- Introduction to musical notation: connecting sound to symbol (whole notes, half notes — visually)
- Listening and movement: moving to music to express its character

**Visual Arts:**
- Drawing and painting with increasing intentionality and control
- Introduction to the elements of art: line, shape, color, texture
- Creating art in response to stories, experiences, and observations
- Beginning to discuss and respond to art: "What do you see? What does it make you feel? What do you think the artist was trying to show?"

**Drama and Movement:**
- Dramatizing familiar stories; creating simple characters
- Movement exploring general space, personal space, levels, pathways
- Simple improvisation: "Show me how a bear walks" — developing body control and imaginative expression

---

### 2.7 Physical Education and Health: Kindergarten

- **Locomotor skills:** Walking, running, jumping, hopping, galloping, sliding, skipping — developing the full range of fundamental movement patterns
- **Non-locomotor skills:** Balancing, bending, twisting, stretching
- **Object control:** Throwing, catching, kicking, striking — beginning with large objects and slow speeds
- **Health:** Germs and how they spread; hand washing; dental hygiene; food groups; the importance of sleep and exercise
- **Safety:** Pedestrian safety; bus safety; what to do in an emergency (fire, stranger danger); internet safety introduction

---

### 2.8 Social-Emotional Learning: Kindergarten

- Identifying and naming a wider range of emotions: excited, nervous, proud, disappointed, grateful, frustrated
- Using words to express needs and feelings
- Understanding classroom rules and contributing to rule-making
- Taking turns and sharing in structured contexts
- Beginning to understand consequences: "If I hit, someone gets hurt; if I ask, maybe we can share"
- Developing a growth mindset introduction: "I can't do this yet" (Dweck, 2006, *Mindset*)

---

### 2.9 Assessment at Kindergarten

- **DIBELS (Dynamic Indicators of Basic Early Literacy Skills):** Widely used, validated measure of early reading skills including phoneme segmentation fluency and nonsense word fluency. Used for progress monitoring, not summative evaluation.
- **Formative observation:** Teacher observation during center time, guided reading groups, and whole-class activities
- **Kindergarten readiness assessments** (e.g., Kindergarten Assessment Tool — varies by state): Used at entry to identify children who need additional support; should inform instruction, not determine placement
- **Portfolio:** Collections of work samples over time

**Note:** Kindergarten retention based on readiness testing is not supported by research evidence. Studies consistently show that retained students do not outperform comparable peers by middle school (RAND, 2002; National Association of School Psychologists position statement, 2011).

**Framework Seed (Optional):** The "ten frame" is a kindergarten mathematics tool that makes visible the structure of base-ten: ten squares arranged in two rows of five. Children who can decompose any number 1-10 relative to ten have internalized the base of our number system. This is not just arithmetic — it is pattern recognition in a positional system. The same pattern-seeking underlying base-10 understanding generalizes, much later, to understanding why certain numbers appear repeatedly in physics (the braided winding number 5, for instance, emerges from the geometry of the compact dimension for reasons that are mathematical — not mystical).

## CHAPTER 3: GRADE 1 (Age 6–7)

### 3.1 Developmental Overview

First graders are in the midst of a sensitive period for reading acquisition. The foundational phonological awareness skills developed in PreK and Kindergarten are now being mapped onto systematic letter-sound correspondences. Children who receive systematic, explicit phonics instruction in Grade 1 make substantially larger reading gains than those who do not (Ehri et al., 2001, meta-analysis in *Elementary School Journal*). This is the grade at which the reading science most clearly supports a departure from incidental or whole-language approaches.

Cognitively, first graders are increasingly able to hold multiple pieces of information in mind, follow multi-step instructions, and engage with symbolic representation (numbers and letters) with growing fluency. They are motivated by mastery — by the experience of being able to do something they could not do before — and require frequent success experiences to sustain effort on difficult tasks.

---

### 3.2 Mathematics: Grade 1

**Standards reference:** CCSSM Grade 1; Clements & Sarama Learning Trajectories.

**Operations and Algebraic Thinking:**
- Add and subtract within 20, demonstrating fluency for addition and subtraction within 10
- Understand the meaning of the equal sign (= means "the same value as," not "the answer is")
- Determine the unknown whole number in an addition or subtraction equation
- Apply properties: commutativity of addition (3 + 5 = 5 + 3); associativity

**Number and Operations in Base Ten:**
- Count to 120, starting at any number
- Understand place value: a two-digit number represents amounts of tens and ones (34 = 3 tens + 4 ones)
- Compare two two-digit numbers using <, =, >
- Add within 100 using place value understanding

**Measurement:**
- Order three objects by length
- Measure lengths using non-standard units
- Tell and write time in hours and half-hours

**Geometry:**
- Distinguish between defining and non-defining attributes of shapes
- Compose and decompose 2D and 3D shapes
- Partition circles and rectangles into halves and fourths

**Critical practice:** **Fact fluency** (knowing addition and subtraction facts within 20 with automaticity) is a Grade 1 and Grade 2 goal. Automaticity matters because it frees working memory capacity for more complex reasoning. The most effective routes to fluency are strategic — building from known facts using derived fact strategies (doubles, near-doubles, make-ten) rather than rote memorization alone (Baroody, 2006, *Developmental Psychology*).

---

### 3.3 English Language Arts: Grade 1

**Phonics and Word Recognition:**
- Decode regularly spelled one-syllable words
- Distinguish long from short vowels in written words
- Know spelling-sound correspondences for common vowel teams (ai, ay, ee, ea, oa, ow)
- Recognize and read grade-appropriate irregularly spelled words
- Read words with inflectional endings (-s, -ing, -ed)

**Reading Fluency:**
- Read Grade 1 texts with sufficient accuracy and fluency to support comprehension (CCSS target: 60-90 words per minute with accuracy)

**Reading Comprehension:**
- Retell stories including key details and demonstrate understanding of the central message
- Describe characters, settings, and major events using key details
- Compare and contrast adventures or experiences of characters
- Read informational texts: identify main topic, key details, vocabulary

**Writing:**
- Write opinion pieces: "My favorite animal is a dog because..."
- Write informative/explanatory texts about a topic
- Write narratives with two or more sequenced events, details, and a sense of closure
- Participate in shared research and writing projects

**Handwriting:** While typing skills are increasingly important, handwriting fluency in Grade 1-2 is associated with better writing quality and composition (James & Engelhardt, 2012, *Trends in Neuroscience and Education*). Students should practice letter formation with correct grip and posture.

---

### 3.4 Science: Grade 1

**NGSS Grade 1 Topics:**
- **Sound and Light (Physical Science):** How vibrations create sound; how objects can be seen only when illuminated; materials that allow light to pass through vs. block light vs. bend light
- **Plants and Animals (Life Science):** Animals use information from their environment to survive; plants depend on water, light, and animals (pollination, seed dispersal); parents and offspring
- **Space Systems (Earth Science):** Patterns of motion of the Sun, Moon, and stars as observed from Earth; seasonal changes

**Disciplinary Core Ideas emphasized:** Structure and function; cause and effect; patterns in natural systems.

---

### 3.5 Social Studies: Grade 1

- **Civics:** Rules vs. laws; the concept of government; community helpers and civic roles; rights and responsibilities
- **Economics:** Producers and consumers; the exchange of goods and services; the concept of opportunity cost (choosing one thing means giving up another)
- **Geography:** Physical features of communities; maps with compass rose and legend; how people adapt to their geographic environment
- **History:** Important historical figures who contributed to community and country (first encounters with biographies as historical narratives)
- **Cultural Awareness:** Similarities and differences among cultures; holidays and traditions from multiple cultures

---

### 3.6 Arts: Grade 1

**Music:**
- Reading simple musical notation: quarter notes, eighth notes, whole notes
- Singing in tune with increasing range; part songs; rounds
- Rhythmic performance: using body percussion, simple instruments
- Introduction to instruments of the orchestra: families (strings, woodwinds, brass, percussion)
- Multicultural music: songs and instruments from multiple traditions

**Visual Arts:**
- Elements of art: line, shape, color, value, texture, form, space — introduced as vocabulary and applied in creating work
- Principles of design: balance, pattern, emphasis — noticed in works of art and applied
- Mixing colors: primary, secondary colors; warm and cool colors
- Looking and discussing: What does this painting tell us? What decisions did the artist make?

**Drama:**
- Acting out stories with clear character motivation
- Voice, movement, and characterization
- Audience and performer roles

---

## CHAPTER 4: GRADE 2 (Age 7–8)

### 4.1 Developmental Overview

Second graders are consolidating the foundational skills acquired in Kindergarten and Grade 1. Reading fluency is developing rapidly; children who are reading fluently by the end of Grade 2 can direct their cognitive resources toward comprehension rather than decoding. The "reading to learn" transition begins meaningfully in Grade 3, making Grade 2 the critical year for ensuring that all students reach decoding fluency.

Socially, second graders are increasingly capable of sustained cooperative work; friendship becomes more stable and important; the peer group is an increasingly significant influence on behavior and motivation.

---

### 4.2 Mathematics: Grade 2

**Standards reference:** CCSSM Grade 2.

**Operations and Algebraic Thinking:**
- Fluently add and subtract within 20 using mental strategies
- Know from memory all sums of two one-digit numbers
- Solve one- and two-step word problems using addition and subtraction within 100
- Determine whether a group of objects (up to 20) has an odd or even number of members

**Number and Operations in Base Ten:**
- Understand that 100 can be thought of as a bundle of ten tens (hundreds)
- Count within 1000; skip-count by 5s, 10s, and 100s
- Read and write numbers to 1000 using base-ten numerals, number names, and expanded form
- Add and subtract within 1000 using place value understanding

**Measurement and Data:**
- Measure and estimate lengths using standard units (inches, feet, centimeters, meters)
- Solve word problems involving lengths
- Work with time and money: tell and write time to the nearest five minutes; solve word problems with dollar bills and coins
- Generate measurement data and represent on a line plot
- Draw a picture graph and a bar graph to represent data with up to four categories

**Geometry:**
- Recognize and draw shapes with specified attributes
- Partition rectangles into rows and columns of same-size squares (foundation for area)
- Partition circles and rectangles into halves, thirds, and fourths

---

### 4.3 English Language Arts: Grade 2

**Reading:**
- Apply phonics and structural analysis skills to decode multisyllabic words
- Read grade-level text accurately and fluently with expression (target: 90-110 wpm)
- Comprehend literary and informational texts: summarize, identify main idea and supporting details, explain cause and effect, compare and contrast
- Ask and answer questions (who, what, where, when, why, how) to demonstrate understanding

**Writing:**
- Introduce the topic and supply some facts about it (informational writing)
- Use temporal words (first, next, then, finally) in narrative writing
- Strengthen writing through editing (correcting sentence errors, punctuation)
- Produce informational text with illustrations when useful

**Vocabulary and Language:**
- Determine meaning of unknown words using context clues, prefixes, and suffixes
- Understand shades of meaning among closely related verbs (e.g., look, peek, stare, glare)
- Correctly use common irregular verbs (sit/sat, go/went)

---

### 4.4 Science: Grade 2

**NGSS Grade 2 Topics:**
- **Matter and Its Interactions (Physical Science):** Properties of materials; changing states of matter; heating and cooling; natural vs. designed objects
- **Interdependent Relationships in Ecosystems (Life Science):** How plants and animals depend on their environment and on each other; what happens when an environment changes
- **Earth's Systems (Earth Science):** Wind and water shape Earth's surface; maps show where water is on Earth; how humans affect the land

**Practices Emphasized:** Planning and carrying out investigations; analyzing and interpreting data; constructing explanations.

---

### 4.5 Social Studies: Grade 2

- **Community:** Urban, suburban, and rural communities — differences and similarities; how communities change over time
- **Economics:** Scarcity, supply and demand in age-appropriate context; simple understanding of interdependence — communities depend on each other
- **Geography:** Continents and oceans; physical and political maps; climate zones; how geography shapes how people live
- **History:** Timelines — placing events in chronological order; introduction to primary sources as historical evidence

---

### 4.6 Arts: Grade 2

**Music:**
- Reading musical notation in treble clef: notes on lines and spaces
- Singing and playing pentatonic melodies (5-note scales — accessible and harmonically forgiving for beginning musicians)
- Recognizing form in music: verse-chorus, ABA (ternary form)
- World music: introduction to music from different cultural traditions — African drumming, Andean flute, Gamelan — as musical systems with their own internal logic

**Visual Arts:**
- Creating work in multiple media: drawing, painting, printmaking, sculpture
- Observational drawing: drawing what you actually see, not what you think something looks like
- Art history: looking at and discussing works from different cultures and time periods (age-appropriate: Romare Bearden, Frida Kahlo, Hokusai, Aboriginal art)
- The art critique process: Describe → Analyze → Interpret → Evaluate (Feldman's method, adapted)

## CHAPTER 5: GRADE 3 (Age 8–9)

### 5.1 Developmental Overview

Grade 3 marks the transition from "learning to read" to "reading to learn." Children who have not achieved decoding fluency by the end of Grade 3 face significantly elevated risk of persistent academic difficulty — the research on this threshold is consistent across multiple large studies (Hernandez, 2011, "Double Jeopardy," Annie E. Casey Foundation; NAEP longitudinal data). **Identifying and intensively supporting students who are not yet fluent readers at the beginning of Grade 3 is the single most important early intervention opportunity available.**

Cognitively, Grade 3 children are increasingly able to think logically about concrete situations (Piaget's "concrete operational" stage, approximately 7-11). They can classify objects hierarchically, understand reversibility (addition can be "undone" by subtraction), and reason about conservation across multiple dimensions simultaneously.

---

### 5.2 Mathematics: Grade 3

**Standards reference:** CCSSM Grade 3.

**Operations and Algebraic Thinking:**
- Interpret products as total number of objects in equal groups (multiplication as repeated addition and as an array structure)
- Interpret quotients as number of objects in each share or number of equal shares (division)
- Determine unknown in multiplication and division equations
- Apply properties of multiplication: commutative, associative, distributive
- **Fluency goal:** Multiply and divide within 100 with automaticity (multiplication facts through 10×10)
- Identify and explain arithmetic patterns

**Number and Operations — Fractions:**
- Understand a fraction as a number on the number line; represent on a number line
- Understand two fractions as equivalent if they are the same size (1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6)
- Compare fractions with the same numerator or the same denominator

**Measurement and Data:**
- Tell and write time to the nearest minute; solve elapsed time problems
- Measure and estimate liquid volumes and masses
- Represent and interpret data (scaled picture graphs and bar graphs)
- Understand concepts of area; measure areas by counting unit squares
- Recognize perimeter as an attribute of plane figures

**Geometry:**
- Understand that shapes in different categories share attributes
- Partition shapes into parts with equal areas

**Critical note on multiplication:** The multiplication table is not merely a memorization task — it is the gateway to all subsequent arithmetic and algebra. Students who do not achieve automaticity with multiplication facts in Grade 3 carry a cognitive load cost into every subsequent mathematics lesson that requires multiplication. Fact fluency should be developed through conceptual grounding (understanding what multiplication means) followed by strategic derived-fact practice and then fluency games/retrieval practice, not through timed drills used as assessment before conceptual understanding is established.

---

### 5.3 English Language Arts: Grade 3

**Reading:**
- Ask and answer questions to demonstrate understanding of a text; refer explicitly to the text
- Determine the main idea of an informational text; explain how key details support it
- Describe characters and explain how their actions contribute to the plot
- Determine the meaning of words and phrases in text; distinguish literal from nonliteral language
- Compare and contrast key points from two texts on the same topic

**Writing:**
- Write opinion pieces supporting a point with reasons
- Write informative pieces that examine a topic and convey ideas clearly
- Write narratives: establish a situation, introduce a narrator or characters, use dialogue and description
- Conduct short research projects; gather information from print and digital sources; take brief notes

**Speaking and Listening:**
- Engage in collaborative discussions; come prepared; build on others' comments
- Report on a topic in an organized manner; speak clearly

**Language:**
- Explain the function of nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs in general and their functions in sentences
- Form and use regular and irregular plural nouns and verbs
- Use spelling patterns and generalizations; use a dictionary

---

### 5.4 Science: Grade 3

**NGSS Grade 3 Topics:**
- **Forces and Interactions (Physical Science):** Balanced and unbalanced forces; effects of friction; magnetic attraction and repulsion
- **Heredity (Life Science):** Reproduction; how organisms pass traits to offspring; variation within species; fossils as evidence of past life
- **Weather and Climate (Earth Science):** Climate as typical weather patterns; climate variability; climate affects organisms' traits
- **Engineering Design:** Define problems; generate solutions; optimize solutions

**Emphasis:** The disciplinary core idea of **inheritance and variation** introduces students to one of the most important unifying concepts in biology — that living organisms transmit instructions from generation to generation, with variation. This is the foundation on which all of genetics, evolution, and molecular biology is built.

---

### 5.5 Social Studies: Grade 3

- **US Geography:** Regions of the United States; physical geography and cultural characteristics; relationship between geography and economic activity
- **Native American Peoples:** Diverse Indigenous cultures of different regions; traditional ways of life; impact of European colonization (age-appropriate honesty, not mythologized)
- **Early Exploration and Colonization:** Why Europeans came to the Americas; the Columbian Exchange and its consequences for all parties
- **Economics:** Markets; supply and demand; entrepreneurship; interdependence

---

### 5.6 Arts: Grade 3

**Music:**
- Playing an instrument (recorder, simple melodic instrument, or classroom percussion set) — developing reading notation through performance
- Part-singing: singing in two parts; understanding harmony
- Composing short melodic and rhythmic phrases
- Music of different cultures: structural analysis — "What is the same? What is different?"

**Visual Arts:**
- Perspective introduction: horizon line, foreground/middle ground/background in landscapes
- Color theory: color wheel, complementary colors, color temperature; creating mood through color
- Art history: examining works from different cultures alongside their historical context
- Sketchbook practice: regular observational drawing as a habit

**Drama:**
- Improvisation with imaginary objects and scenarios; developing specificity
- Script reading and performance: short plays adapted from literature
- Puppetry and storytelling

---

## CHAPTER 6: GRADE 4 (Age 9–10)

### 6.1 Developmental Overview

Grade 4 children are increasingly able to engage in sustained independent reading, complex writing, and multi-step mathematical reasoning. They are entering a period of expanding social comparison — increasingly aware of how they perform relative to peers — which makes this a critical time for establishing growth mindset (the belief that ability can develop through effort and strategy), without which the experience of difficulty produces helplessness rather than persistence.

Academically, this is the year when the consequences of incomplete foundational knowledge become most visible: students who have not consolidated basic multiplication, fraction concepts, or reading comprehension strategies in Grades 2-3 face compounding difficulty in Grade 4's more demanding content.

---

### 6.2 Mathematics: Grade 4

**Standards reference:** CCSSM Grade 4.

**Operations and Algebraic Thinking:**
- Interpret multiplication equations as comparisons (35 = 5 × 7 can mean "35 is 5 times as many as 7")
- Solve multi-step word problems using all four operations
- Assess the reasonableness of answers using estimation and mental computation
- Find factor pairs and multiples; determine if a whole number is prime or composite

**Number and Operations in Base Ten:**
- Generalize place value understanding to multi-digit whole numbers through 1,000,000
- Read and write multi-digit numbers in expanded form and word form
- Compare multi-digit numbers; round to any place
- Fluently add and subtract multi-digit numbers using the standard algorithm
- Multiply a whole number of up to four digits by a one-digit whole number; multiply two two-digit numbers

**Number and Operations — Fractions:**
- Extend understanding of fraction equivalence to denominators up to 100
- Add and subtract fractions with like denominators
- Multiply a fraction by a whole number
- Understand decimal notation for fractions; compare decimal fractions

**Measurement and Data:**
- Know relative sizes of measurement units within systems (km, m, cm; kg, g; lb, oz; l, ml; hr, min, sec)
- Apply area and perimeter formulas for rectangles
- Understand angles as geometric shapes; measure and draw angles with a protractor

**Geometry:**
- Draw and identify points, lines, line segments, rays, angles, and perpendicular and parallel lines
- Classify two-dimensional figures by properties of their lines and angles
- Recognize a line of symmetry for a two-dimensional figure

---

### 6.3 English Language Arts: Grade 4

**Reading Literature:**
- Determine theme from details; summarize text
- Describe a character in depth (traits, motivations, feelings) drawing on specific text details
- Compare and contrast points of view of two characters; narrator and character perspective

**Reading Informational Text:**
- Determine main idea; explain how it is supported by key details; summarize
- Explain events, procedures, ideas, or concepts in a text including what happened and why
- Interpret information from charts, graphs, and diagrams; integrate with text

**Writing:**
- Write opinion essays: introduce the topic, state a clear opinion, support with reasons and evidence, provide a concluding statement
- Write informative/explanatory texts: introduce a topic clearly, use facts, definitions, concrete details, quotations, and examples
- Write narratives: orient the reader; use dialogue, description, and pacing; use transitional words; provide a conclusion
- Conduct research projects using multiple sources; avoid plagiarism; provide basic citation

**Speaking and Listening:**
- Paraphrase portions of a text read aloud
- Identify the reasons and evidence a speaker provides
- Present clearly on a topic, using appropriate facts and details, speaking audibly and clearly

---

### 6.4 Science: Grade 4

**NGSS Grade 4 Topics:**
- **Energy (Physical Science):** Energy can be transferred; collisions; sound and light; electric and magnetic forces at a distance; information transfer
- **Structure, Function, and Information Processing (Life Science):** The internal and external structures of plants and animals; the brain's role in processing sensory information; patterns in the fossil record
- **Earth's Systems (Earth Science):** Processes that shape Earth: weathering, erosion, rock cycle; maps of Earth's features; natural hazards

**Emphasis:** This is the year when students connect observable phenomena to the underlying mechanisms that produce them — understanding that earthquakes and volcanoes are not random but reflect the movement of tectonic plates; that fossils record the history of life; that the brain is the organ through which all sensory experience is processed.

---

### 6.5 Social Studies: Grade 4

- **United States History:** Colonial period through American Revolution; the founding documents (Declaration of Independence, Constitution, Bill of Rights) in age-appropriate depth; the meaning of democracy as a system of governance
- **State-Level Geography and History:** Most states have Grade 4 standards focused on state history and geography — a connection between the local and the national
- **Economics:** Colonial economy; trade; taxation without representation as an economic and political concept
- **Civics:** Structure of government: three branches; checks and balances; the role of citizens

---

### 6.6 Arts: Grade 4

**Music:**
- Instrumental instruction begins in many schools at Grade 4 or 5: band (wind and percussion) or orchestra (string instruments)
- Reading music in treble and bass clef; note values and time signatures; key signatures (C, G, F major)
- Music theory: scales, chords, intervals — beginning the vocabulary for musical analysis
- Music history: the Baroque, Classical, and Romantic periods — not as memorized dates but as musical styles connected to their historical context
- World music: music of Latin America, Sub-Saharan Africa, East Asia as systems with their own theories

**Visual Arts:**
- Drawing: one-point perspective; still life; portrait (proportion of the human face)
- Painting: watercolor and tempera techniques; color mixing for specific effects
- Printmaking: monoprinting, relief printing (linoleum or foam)
- Sculpture: wire sculpture, papier-mâché, clay coil building
- Art criticism: formal analysis of works from the Western canon and global traditions

---

### 6.7 Social-Emotional Learning: Grade 4

- **Growth mindset deepened:** Understanding the neuroscience of learning in age-appropriate terms — "Your brain grows stronger when you practice"
- **Conflict resolution:** Structured protocols for resolving conflicts with peers; understanding compromise
- **Digital citizenship:** Responsible use of technology; cyberbullying; online privacy; evaluating sources
- **Empathy and perspective-taking:** Using literature (fiction with complex characters) as an empathy lab
- **Self-advocacy:** Identifying your own learning needs; asking for help; understanding your own strengths

## CHAPTER 7: GRADE 5 (Age 10–11)

### 7.1 Developmental Overview

Grade 5 students are approaching the transition to middle school, and many are beginning early adolescence — hormonal changes, shifting social priorities, increasing importance of peer relationships, and emerging capacity for more abstract reasoning. This developmental transition can disrupt academic engagement if the instructional environment does not adapt to it: students who felt confident in elementary school can suddenly become self-conscious about answering questions in class, asking for help, or making mistakes publicly.

Cognitively, most Grade 5 students are at the late concrete-operational stage and beginning to develop formal operational thinking — the capacity to reason about abstract, hypothetical situations. This is the stage at which pre-algebraic thinking becomes possible: the ability to use a variable as a placeholder for an unknown quantity rather than as a specific mystery number.

---

### 7.2 Mathematics: Grade 5

**Standards reference:** CCSSM Grade 5.

**Operations and Algebraic Thinking:**
- Write and interpret simple numerical expressions (using parentheses) without evaluating them — beginning of algebraic notation
- Generate two numerical patterns using two different rules; identify relationships

**Number and Operations in Base Ten:**
- Understand the place value system: that in a multi-digit number, a digit in one place is 10 times as large as in the place to its right
- Read, write, and compare decimals to thousandths
- Multiply multi-digit whole numbers using the standard algorithm
- Find whole-number quotients with up to four-digit dividends and two-digit divisors
- Add, subtract, multiply, and divide decimals to hundredths

**Number and Operations — Fractions:**
- Add and subtract fractions with unlike denominators
- Interpret multiplication of fractions (4/5 × 7 as 4/5 of a set of 7 objects; 4/5 × 3/4 as "scaling" — this is a major conceptual shift)
- Divide unit fractions by whole numbers and whole numbers by unit fractions

**Measurement and Data:**
- Convert measurement units within a system; solve multi-step problems
- Understand volume: measure using unit cubes; apply the formula V = l × w × h; relate volume to multiplication and addition

**Geometry:**
- Understand and use a coordinate system (two-dimensional coordinate plane — plot points in the first quadrant)
- Classify two-dimensional figures in a hierarchy based on properties

**Note on fractions:** The multiplication and division of fractions is among the most conceptually demanding topics in the elementary mathematics curriculum and one of the most poorly understood by students and, in some cases, teachers. The standard algorithm (multiply numerators, multiply denominators; invert and multiply for division) works correctly but explains nothing. Conceptual instruction using area models, number lines, and the scaling interpretation of multiplication should precede and accompany algorithm instruction.

---

### 7.3 English Language Arts: Grade 5

**Reading Literature:**
- Compare and contrast two or more characters, settings, or events in a story, drawing on specific text evidence
- Determine theme and explain how it develops over the course of a text
- Describe how a narrator's or speaker's point of view influences how events are described
- Analyze how visual and multimedia elements contribute to the meaning of a text

**Reading Informational Text:**
- Determine main idea and explain how it is supported by key details; summarize
- Explain the relationships between two or more concepts in a text
- Analyze multiple accounts of the same event or topic; note similarities and differences in the point of view they represent
- Integrate information from several texts on the same topic in writing or speaking

**Writing:**
- Write sustained opinion essays with multiple reasons, supported by facts and evidence from sources
- Write researched informational essays with introduction, body, and conclusion; cite sources
- Write narratives with character development, dialogue, and narrative techniques
- Use technology to produce and publish writing

**Research:**
- Conduct short research projects investigating a question using multiple sources
- Recall relevant information; summarize and paraphrase
- Provide a list of sources (introduction to bibliographic format)

---

### 7.4 Science: Grade 5

**NGSS Grade 5 Topics:**
- **Matter and Its Interactions (Physical Science):** All matter is made of particles; properties of substances are unchanged when combined (conservation of mass); solutions vs. mixtures; chemical reactions (formation of new substances with different properties)
- **Earth's Systems (Earth Science):** Earth's geosphere, hydrosphere, atmosphere, and biosphere — interactions and interdependencies; the water cycle; Earth's history from rocks and fossils
- **Ecosystems (Life Science):** Matter and energy in organisms and ecosystems; food webs; decomposition; the roles of organisms in ecosystems

**Major conceptual achievement:** Conservation of mass in chemical reactions is a profound idea that connects everyday observation (iron rusting, wood burning) to an underlying law. Students who genuinely understand conservation of mass have grasped one of the most powerful and universal concepts in all of science. This concept should be established experimentally before it is stated abstractly.

### 7.5 Social Studies: Grade 5

- **American History — Colonial through Civil War Era:** Detailed study of the founding period; Constitution and Bill of Rights; westward expansion; slavery and the Civil War; connecting primary sources to historical narrative
- **Civics deepened:** How laws are made; the federal system; branches of government in depth; rights and responsibilities of citizenship
- **Economics:** Free enterprise; market economy; role of government in economic regulation
- **Global Connections:** How the Americas are connected to the rest of the world historically and currently

### 7.6 Arts: Grade 5

**Music:**
- If instrumental instruction has begun: scales, simple repertoire pieces, ensemble playing
- Music theory: major and minor keys; chord progressions (I-IV-V); beginning music composition
- Listening and analysis: identifying instrumentation, form, and style in recorded music
- Music and culture: jazz, blues, and gospel as distinctly American art forms and their historical context (the civil rights movement and music)

**Visual Arts:**
- Drawing: two-point perspective; figure drawing (proportion, gesture)
- Design principles: unity, variety, rhythm, movement, balance, contrast, emphasis
- Digital art: introduction to image editing, digital drawing — art tools of the contemporary world
- Exhibition preparation: selecting work for display; writing artist statements

**Drama:**
- Reading and performing scenes from published plays
- Writing original short plays
- Technical theater introduction: understanding staging, lighting, costumes, set design as artistic choices

### 7.7 Physical Education and Health: Grade 5

**Physical Education:**
- Sport-specific skills: dribbling, passing, throwing patterns across multiple sports
- Fitness concepts: cardiovascular endurance, muscular strength, flexibility, body composition — understanding the components of fitness and how to improve each
- FITT Principle: Frequency, Intensity, Time, and Type — students plan their own exercise regimen
- Team sports: developing cooperative play, communication, and sportsmanship

**Health:**
- Puberty education: age-appropriate introduction to adolescent development (physical, emotional, social changes)
- Nutrition and healthy eating patterns; reading nutrition labels
- Substance prevention: introduction to the effects of tobacco, alcohol, and other substances
- Mental health: stress management; understanding anxiety and depression as real, treatable conditions — reducing stigma

### 7.8 Social-Emotional Learning: Grade 5

- **Leadership:** Developing leadership skills in group projects; understanding that leadership is service
- **Ethical decision-making:** Complex scenarios with competing values; practicing principled reasoning
- **Academic identity:** Helping students develop a positive relationship with learning and difficulty; the science of learning (spaced practice, retrieval practice) taught explicitly
- **Transition preparation:** Preparing for middle school emotionally and academically — what to expect, what skills to build

### 7.9 Assessment at Grades 3-5

**Formative assessment practices that work:**
- **Exit tickets:** Brief written responses at the end of class that reveal what students understood and what remains confused
- **Retrieval practice quizzes:** Low-stakes, frequent (weekly or biweekly) quizzes on previously taught material — the most evidence-supported formative strategy
- **Think-pair-share:** A structured discussion format that reveals students' thinking before whole-class instruction
- **Hinge questions:** Single questions that reveal whether the class is ready to proceed to the next concept; designed so that different wrong answers reveal different specific misconceptions

**Summative assessment considerations:**
- End-of-unit tests should include some problems requiring transfer to unfamiliar contexts, not only repetition of practiced problem types
- Reading assessments should include texts that students have not previously read (not texts used for instruction)
- Writing assessments should include at least some that allow sufficient time for revision, reflecting the actual writing process

## APPENDIX B: LEARNING PROGRESSIONS BY SUBJECT

### Mathematics Learning Progression (PreK–5)

```
PreK:    Number → Counting → Comparing → Patterns → Shape
K:       Cardinality → Decomposition → Place Value (10) → Shape
Gr. 1:   Add/Subtract to 20 → Place Value (100) → Measurement → Geometry
Gr. 2:   Fluency ±20 → Place Value (1000) → Standard Measurement → Data
Gr. 3:   × and ÷ → Fluency ×÷100 → Fractions on number line → Area
Gr. 4:   Multi-digit × → Fraction + - × → Angles → Multi-step problems
Gr. 5:   Decimal operations → Fraction ×÷ → Volume → Coordinate Plane
```

### Reading Development Progression (PreK–5)

```
PreK:    Phonological awareness → Print awareness → Vocabulary (oral)
K:       Phoneme segmentation → Phonics (CVC) → Sight words → Fluency begins
Gr. 1:   Phonics (blends, digraphs, long vowels) → Fluency → Comprehension
Gr. 2:   Multisyllabic decoding → Fluency (90-110 wpm) → Comprehension strategies
Gr. 3:   Fluency consolidation → Comprehension depth → Text structure
Gr. 4:   Reading to learn → Informational text → Multiple-source synthesis
Gr. 5:   Complex text → Argumentative reading → Research → Literary analysis
```

## CLOSING NOTE ON VOLUME 1

This curriculum guide covers the foundational years of formal education — PreKindergarten through Grade 5 — in which the structures that determine all subsequent learning are established. The evidence is clear and consistent: what happens in these years matters enormously and durably. The reading skills established by the end of Grade 3, the mathematical fluency and conceptual understanding established by the end of Grade 5, the executive function skills and growth mindset cultivated through the early elementary years — these are not the prelude to the real education; they are the foundation on which the real education rests.
