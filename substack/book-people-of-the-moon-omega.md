## A Note on Voice and Approach

This book was synthesized by AxiomZero with GitHub Copilot. It draws on published ethnographic, historical, linguistic, and legal sources. It does not claim access to oral traditions held privately by the Snoqualmie people, and it does not pretend that a written text can substitute for living cultural transmission.

The Snoqualmie Indian Tribe maintains its own cultural programs, its own language revitalization efforts, and its own methods of teaching tribal history. This book is offered as a companion — a way of introducing the broader public to a people whose story has been largely invisible in mainstream American historical narratives. It is not the authoritative voice on Snoqualmie culture. That voice belongs to the Snoqualmie people themselves.

Where this book errs, it errs in good faith. Corrections from tribal members and scholars are welcomed.

## Preface: A Note on This Book

There is a waterfall in the foothills of the Cascade Mountains, forty miles east of Seattle, that drops two hundred and sixty-eight feet into a bowl of mist and thunder. It is taller than Niagara. The spray rises in great white columns visible from miles away, and on certain days the mist hangs in the air like a living thing, drifting upward through stands of old-growth cedar and Douglas-fir, catching the light in ways that make you understand, at a cellular level, why human beings have always believed some places are sacred.

The Snoqualmie people have known this for at least ten thousand years.

They called the falls *snoqʷəlʔ* — and they understood it as something more than a geological feature. It was the house of the mist-beings. It was the place where Moon, the great Transformer of the world, had shaped the landscape into its present form. It was the portal between the human world and the spirit world, the spot where the boundary between realms grows thin. It was the center of a cosmology, an ecology, a way of being human on this particular stretch of the Pacific Northwest coast.

In 1898 — forty-three years after the Snoqualmie signed a treaty with the United States government that promised them a reservation — a power company built a hydroelectric plant at the falls. The sacred center of the Snoqualmie world became a source of electricity for the growing city of Seattle.

The Snoqualmie people, who had never received the reservation they were promised, watched this happen from positions of enforced powerlessness. They had no land base, no formal federal recognition, no legal standing to stop the desecration of their most sacred site. They were, in the bureaucratic language of the United States government, essentially invisible — present in the historical record of the 1855 treaty signing, but absent from the administrative machinery of Indian affairs that might have protected their rights.

They would not receive federal recognition until 1999. One hundred and forty-four years after the treaty.

This is a book about the sdukʷalbixʷ — the People of the Moon, the Snoqualmie. It attempts to tell their story from the deep past to the present: the thousand-year settlement of a valley, the cosmology that grew from a particular landscape, the catastrophe of colonialism, the long decades of dispossession and cultural suppression, and the remarkable resilience that preserved community, ceremony, and identity across a century and a half of marginalization.

**How to read this book:** It is organized chronologically and thematically. Part I establishes the land — the geography and ecology of the Snoqualmie Valley, without which nothing else makes sense. Parts II through VI cover the deep history and cultural life of the people before sustained European contact. Parts VII through X trace the colonial period, the treaty, the dispossession, and the long struggle for recognition. Parts XI and XII address cultural survival and the contemporary Snoqualmie Tribe. The appendices provide reference material: vocabulary, village sites, timeline, neighboring peoples, and a note on sources.

**What this book cannot do:** It cannot transmit the experience of standing at Snoqualmie Falls and feeling the mist on your face while the thunder of falling water fills your chest. It cannot convey the sound of a Lushootseed elder telling a story in the language that has encoded this landscape for millennia. It cannot replace the knowledge held by Snoqualmie families in their own memories, their own ceremonies, their own practices of cultural transmission. Some knowledge is not meant to be written down, and this book respects that boundary.

**What this book acknowledges:** The history it describes includes genocide — deliberate and incidental — forced cultural erasure, broken legal promises, and the long machinery of a settler-colonial state working to eliminate Indigenous peoples as a distinct cultural presence. These things are called by their names here. Euphemism is a form of complicity.

The Snoqualmie people survived. They are here. Their Falls is still roaring. This book is offered in recognition of that endurance.

## A Note on Language

The Snoqualmie people speak — or historically spoke, and are actively working to speak again — a language called Lushootseed (also written Lkʷungən or dxʷləšucid in various orthographies). Lushootseed belongs to the Coast Salish branch of the Salishan language family, which extends across much of the Pacific Northwest and into British Columbia. Within Lushootseed itself, there are two main dialects: Northern Lushootseed (spoken by peoples around the northern Puget Sound, including the Snohomish, Stillaguamish, and Sauk-Suiattle) and Southern Lushootseed (spoken by peoples around the southern Puget Sound and the lower Cascade foothills, including the Snoqualmie, Duwamish, Muckleshoot, and Puyallup).

Lushootseed is not a phonologically simple language for English speakers. It contains sounds that do not exist in English and that require some explanation before the words in this book can be approached even approximately.

### Pronunciation Guide

The following symbols appear in Lushootseed words throughout this text. They follow the Americanist phonetic notation as standardized for Lushootseed by linguists at the University of Washington, particularly Vi taqʷšəblu Hilbert and her collaborators.

**Vowels:**
- **a** — as in "father"
- **e** — as in "bed"
- **i** — as in "machine"
- **u** — as in "flute"
- **ə** (schwa) — the unstressed vowel in "about" or "the"; the most common vowel in Lushootseed
- **á, é, í, ú** (with accent) — stressed versions of the above vowels

**Special Consonants:**
- **xʷ** — a voiceless velar fricative with lip rounding; imagine the "wh" in an old-fashioned pronunciation of "what," but pushed much further back in the throat, with rounded lips. It is the sound in *xʷəlč* (orca).
- **x̌** — a voiceless uvular fricative; further back than **xʷ**, produced at the very back of the throat near the uvula. English has no equivalent. Think of the final sound in Scottish "loch" but moved even further back.
- **q** — a voiceless uvular stop (like a "k" but produced at the uvula, the dangling thing at the back of your throat)
- **qʷ** — the same sound with lip rounding
- **č** — like "ch" in "church"
- **š** — like "sh" in "shore"
- **ƛ̕** — a lateral affricate with a glottal catch; produced at the alveolar ridge (where you make "l") but released explosively. English has no equivalent whatsoever. The closest approximation is a sharp "tl" sound, but that is very approximate.
- **ʔ** — the glottal stop; the catch in the throat between the two syllables of "uh-oh." In Lushootseed it can appear at the beginning of words, in the middle, and at the end, and it changes meaning.
- **ʼ** (apostrophe after a consonant) — ejective consonant; the consonant is produced with a simultaneous closure of the glottis and a burst of air from the larynx. Ejective consonants are common in Northwest Coast languages and many other languages worldwide, but absent from English. They sound slightly more forceful than their non-ejective counterparts.
- **L** (capital L) — a lateral resonant, distinguished from the lowercase **l** in some transcription systems; varies by dialect and context
- **w̓** — a labial glide with a glottal catch

**A note on the name "Snoqualmie":** The modern English spelling and pronunciation "Snoqualmie" (approximately snoh-KWAHL-mee) is an anglicization of *sdukʷalbixʷ*, the people's name for themselves. The word *sdukʷalbixʷ* is more literally translated as "the people of the moon" or "moon people." The English corruption "Snoqualmie" preserves only the roughest approximation of the original sounds and has shed the meaning entirely.

**Why the language matters:** Language is not merely a communication system. It is a way of structuring reality — of dividing the world into categories, of encoding relationships, of carrying cultural memory. The Lushootseed language encodes a particular understanding of the Pacific Northwest landscape: its plants, animals, weather, water, and spiritual geography. Many Lushootseed concepts do not translate cleanly into English. *Sxʷəl* — roughly "spirit" or "power" — refers to something that permeates living things and certain places, something between what English speakers might call a soul and what we might call a vital force. There is no English word that fully captures it.

When the boarding schools forbade Snoqualmie children to speak Lushootseed — when teachers hit children for uttering words in their own language — they were not simply suppressing a communication habit. They were attempting to sever a people from their cosmology, their ecological knowledge, and their ways of being in relationship with the world they inhabited. The revival of Lushootseed is not nostalgia. It is the recovery of a complete way of understanding life.

Words in Lushootseed appear throughout this book in italics, followed by approximate English translations. A vocabulary appendix at the back provides forty key terms with pronunciation guidance. Readers are encouraged to attempt the sounds — imperfectly — rather than skipping over them. The attempt is itself a kind of respect.

## Part I — The Land: River, Valley, and the Roaring Water

### The Geography of Everything

To understand the Snoqualmie people, you must first stand in the valley.

The Snoqualmie River is formed by the convergence of three forks. The North Fork rises in the high Cascades near Stevens Pass and flows south through the town of Index, picking up the waters of tributary creeks as it descends. The Middle Fork — the wildest and least developed of the three — threads through a narrow valley of old-growth timber and glacial rubble, rushing down from the alpine country near Snoqualmie Pass. The South Fork descends from the pass itself, from the ancient route through the mountains that human beings have been using for at least ten thousand years. These three rivers meet near the town of North Bend, and the combined Snoqualmie River flows northwest across a broad, flat valley floor — a valley carved by glaciers and periodically rearranged by floods — through what is now the town of Snoqualmie, toward the edge of the Cascade foothills.

And there, at the edge of the plateau, the river simply ends.

Not in a delta, not in a quiet confluence with another river, but in an abrupt, thunderous plunge. Snoqualmie Falls drops two hundred and sixty-eight feet into the gorge below — higher than Niagara Falls by more than one hundred feet, though narrower and less widely known. The spray that rises from the base creates a perpetual mist that hangs over the gorge in all seasons, catching sunlight in rainbows and filling the air with a constant, low thunder that you feel in your sternum before you hear it with your ears.

Below the falls, the river continues northwest through the Snoqualmie Valley — a broad, fertile bottomland that remains one of the most productive agricultural valleys in Washington State — and eventually joins the Skykomish River near Monroe to form the Snohomish River, which empties into Puget Sound at Everett.

This geography is not incidental. It shapes everything.

### The Ecology of the Valley

The Snoqualmie Valley sits at the intersection of two major bioregions. To the west, the lowland forests of western Washington: towering Douglas-fir, western red cedar, and western hemlock, with an understory of sword fern, salal, and vine maple. To the east, the Cascade Mountains rise sharply, with subalpine meadows giving way above treeline to rock, ice, and the permanent snowfields of peaks like Mount Si, which looms nearly four thousand feet above North Bend and is visible from the valley floor as a constant presence — watchful, enormous, unambiguous.

The river system itself creates a series of distinct ecological zones. The river bottom supports cottonwood and black cottonwood groves, red alder thickets, and the dense, tangly vegetation of Pacific Northwest riparian zones. The valley floor — regularly flooded by the Snoqualmie, which is a river of considerable seasonal violence — produces the rich bottomland soils that grow camas, wapato, and countless other food plants. The upland forests produce cedar, hemlock, and fir of enormous size; before logging, trees in this valley regularly reached diameters of ten to fifteen feet.

And through all of it, salmon. The Snoqualmie River system supports all five Pacific salmon species: chinook (king), coho (silver), chum (dog), pink (humpback), and sockeye (red), as well as steelhead trout, cutthroat trout, and bull trout. The salmon were not merely food. They were the economic foundation, the protein engine, the nutritional basis of everything. A good salmon run meant abundance; a failed run meant hunger. The relationship between the Snoqualmie people and the salmon they depended on was not a relationship of predator and prey but something more like a covenant — a mutual obligation maintained through ceremony, careful harvest, and the return of bones to the water.

### Snoqualmie Falls: The Sacred Center

*Snoqʷəlʔ* — Snoqualmie Falls — was the axis mundi of the Snoqualmie world.

In Snoqualmie cosmology, the falls was not merely a spectacular geological feature. It was the place where Moon, the great Transformer, had shaped the landscape into its current form. It was a house for the mist-beings who inhabited the spray and the gorge. It was a portal between the human world and the world of spirits, a place where the membrane between realms grew thin enough that communication between them became possible. Prayers offered at the falls were heard. Ceremonies conducted there carried particular power.

The salmon came upriver through the falls — or more precisely, the salmon who ascended the Snoqualmie River came from below the falls, making their way upstream from Puget Sound, through the lower valley, through the gorge below the falls, and up into the upper river and its tributaries to spawn. The falls itself was a barrier to salmon passage above a certain point, concentrating the run in the lower river and creating exceptional fishing conditions. But in the Snoqualmie story, the falls was not where salmon were blocked — it was where they arrived. The first salmon ceremony was conducted at the falls. The covenant with the salmon people was renewed there, year after year, generation after generation.

The mist that rises perpetually from the gorge was understood as the breath of the spirit world — a visible sign of what is normally invisible, the ongoing presence of powers that shape the world but are not fully of it. Standing at the rim of the falls and feeling that mist on your face was not a sensory experience separate from spiritual experience. It was the same experience. The landscape was the theology.

This is a concept that Western thinking has enormous difficulty grasping, because it separates the natural from the sacred, the geological from the spiritual, the material from the meaningful. In Snoqualmie cosmology — as in most Indigenous cosmologies of the Pacific Northwest — these are not separate categories. A waterfall is a waterfall and it is sacred. A mountain is a mountain and it is a person. A salmon is a salmon and it is a being with obligations and rights. The world is not a backdrop against which human spiritual life is lived; the world is the spiritual life.

Snoqualmie Falls, in this understanding, is not a place where the Snoqualmie happen to feel religious feelings. It is the center of a living cosmos. Its power does not depend on human recognition of that power. It was there before the sdukʷalbixʷ arrived. It will be there after they are gone. The relationship between the people and the falls is a matter of reciprocal acknowledgment — the people recognize what the falls is, and in recognizing it, participate in the larger order of the world.

This is what was sold in 1889.

This is what was dammed in 1898.

This is what the Snoqualmie people have been fighting to reclaim ever since.

### The Mountains as Presence

The Cascades are not background scenery in the Snoqualmie world. Every significant peak, every notable rock formation, every unusual bend in the river has a story — a story in which that feature was created by, or is the transformed body of, a being from the time before the world took its present form. Mount Si (*sxʷiləq*), which dominates the skyline above North Bend, is named in Lushootseed tradition and figures in stories. The three forks of the Snoqualmie each have their own characters, their own spiritual associations, their own histories in the oral tradition.

The landscape, in other words, is a text. Those who know how to read it — who have been taught the stories that explain each feature, who know the names and their meanings — live inside a narrative that makes every journey through the valley a kind of reading. Every cedar tree, every salmon pool, every mountain meadow is simultaneously a practical resource and a site of meaning.

When the logging companies stripped the old-growth forests of the Snoqualmie Valley — beginning in the 1880s and continuing through the twentieth century — they were not merely removing timber. They were erasing text. When the developers built houses on the camas meadows and the wapato flats, they were not merely converting land use. They were paving over sentences. The cumulative effect was the destruction of a library — not of books, but of landscape — that had been assembled and annotated over ten thousand years.

Some of that library survives. The falls is still there. Mount Si is still there. The river still runs. And the sdukʷalbixʷ still know how to read what remains.

## Part II — Deep Time: Origins and Archaeology

### The First People

Human beings have been living in the Snoqualmie Valley for at least ten thousand years. This is not a metaphor or an approximation. Archaeological evidence from sites throughout the greater Puget Sound region — including Marymoor Park in Redmond (where excavations revealed stratified occupation layers reaching back into the early Holocene), sites along the Tolt River (a tributary of the Snoqualmie), and numerous locations along the lower Snoqualmie and Skykomish rivers — confirms a continuous human presence in this landscape since the retreat of the Vashon Glacier, which covered the entire region under several thousand feet of ice during the last glacial maximum.

The people who became the Snoqualmie are part of the larger Coast Salish world — a cultural and linguistic grouping of peoples who occupied the Pacific Northwest coast from the mouth of the Columbia River north into British Columbia, sharing a broadly related set of languages (the Salishan family), material culture, ceremonial practices, and cosmological frameworks. Within Coast Salish, the Snoqualmie belong specifically to the Lushootseed-speaking peoples: the group of related tribes who occupied the Puget Sound drainage, speaking dialects of dxʷləšucid (Lushootseed) — a language that all evidence suggests has been spoken in this region for at least three to four thousand years, and possibly much longer.

Linguistic and archaeological evidence together paint a picture of remarkable cultural continuity. The core elements of Snoqualmie culture — the salmon-based economy, the cedar longhouse, the potlatch, the guardian spirit quest, the First Salmon Ceremony — are not recent innovations. They are ancient adaptations to a particular environment, refined over millennia into a sophisticated and sustainable system of life.

### Archaeology in the Valley

The archaeological record of the Snoqualmie Valley is incomplete, partly because Pacific Northwest conditions — acidic soils, heavy rainfall, dense vegetation — are not kind to organic materials, and partly because archaeological investigation of the region has been limited and sporadic. Much of what exists has been found incidentally, during construction projects that disturbed buried deposits.

What has been found tells a coherent story. The earliest occupation of the Puget Sound lowlands, following glacial retreat approximately fourteen thousand years ago, was by peoples hunting the large mammals — mastodons, giant ground sloths, ancient bison — that grazed the newly deglaciated landscape. By ten thousand years ago, the climate had warmed, the megafauna had largely disappeared (a process almost certainly accelerated by human hunting), and the dense conifer forests that would characterize western Washington had begun to establish themselves. The human populations of the region adapted: the economy shifted from big-game hunting to the mixed strategy of salmon fishing, shellfish gathering, hunting of deer and elk, and intensive plant harvesting that would define Northwest Coast cultures for the following millennia.

The Marymoor site, at the southern end of Lake Sammamish — adjacent to the Snoqualmie watershed — has produced evidence of occupation from multiple cultural periods, including remains of fire pits, lithic tools, and food processing debris. The Tolt River sites have yielded similar evidence. Throughout the lower Snoqualmie Valley, agricultural development and construction have periodically uncovered burials and occupation layers, most of which have not been systematically excavated.

What the archaeology cannot recover — and what must be understood as a limit of the discipline — is the cosmology, the oral tradition, the social organization, and the ceremonial life of the people who made those fire pits and fashioned those stone tools. For that, we must turn to the oral tradition itself, mediated through anthropological records, linguists' transcriptions, and — most importantly — the community knowledge that survived the colonial period.

### The 1700 Cascadia Subduction Zone Earthquake

On the night of January 26, 1700 — the date established with remarkable precision through Japanese written records of the resulting tsunami, the dating of "ghost forests" of drowned trees along the Pacific Northwest coast, and the correlation of these records with Cascadia fault models — the Cascadia Subduction Zone ruptured along approximately a thousand miles of its length. The resulting earthquake, estimated at magnitude 9.0, was one of the largest in the history of North America. The tsunami it generated traveled across the Pacific Ocean and damaged villages in Japan.

For the peoples of the Pacific Northwest coast, it was an apocalyptic event. The ground shook for several minutes. Landslides buried river valleys. The coastline subsided in some places by several feet, drowning lowland forests. Tsunamis swept through the inlets and bays of Puget Sound, though the relative shallowness of the Sound and its distance from the open ocean somewhat limited their destructive force compared to the outer coast. Oral traditions throughout the region describe a catastrophic event involving shaking earth, rising waters, and the rearrangement of the landscape.

The significance of this event for understanding Snoqualmie culture is twofold. First, it demonstrates the deep geological instability of the region — the Snoqualmie Valley is seismically active, and the people who lived there for ten thousand years were not passive observers of a static landscape but active inhabitants of a dynamic, sometimes violent one. Second, it provides a remarkable example of how oral tradition encodes geological memory. Linguist Ruth Ludwin and her collaborators, working with Indigenous oral traditions from throughout the Pacific Northwest, have documented stories that describe events consistent with major subduction zone earthquakes — oral records extending back centuries before European contact that accurately encode the behavior of a Cascadia rupture. The Snoqualmie and their neighbors were not merely telling metaphorical stories about the earth shaking. They were transmitting geological data across generations through the medium of narrative.

This is not primitive. This is sophisticated. It is information management of a different kind than written archives, no less accurate over long timescales and arguably more resilient — a written library can burn, but an oral tradition embedded in cultural practice and ceremony can survive as long as there are people to carry it.

### The Stability of a Culture

What archaeology and historical linguistics together suggest is this: the culture that Europeans encountered in the Puget Sound region in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries was not a recent development. The Snoqualmie and their neighbors had been living in essentially the same way — with ongoing refinements and adaptations, of course — for thousands of years. Their salmon economy was ancient. Their cedar technology was ancient. Their cosmology, with its emphasis on the reciprocal relationships between humans and the natural world, was ancient.

This matters because a common trope in American historical writing is to describe pre-contact Indigenous cultures as "Stone Age" or "primitive" — as if they represented an early, undeveloped stage of a universal human progress that was eventually superseded by European civilization. This framing is not merely condescending. It is factually wrong. A culture that has successfully sustained a complex society in a particular environment for ten thousand years is not undeveloped. It is perfected. It has been tested by every disaster, every climate shift, every disease and famine and earthquake that its landscape could produce, and it has survived. The accumulated knowledge embedded in that culture — the ecological knowledge, the social technologies, the ceremonial practices that maintain community cohesion under stress — represents a form of intelligence that took millennia to develop and cannot be reconstructed quickly once it has been destroyed.

The colonial project that began in the Snoqualmie Valley in the 1840s was not the introduction of progress into a static backwater. It was the violent collision of a ten-thousand-year-old civilization with a newcomer that did not know what it was destroying.

## Part III — Cosmology and Belief

### Moon, the Transformer

In the beginning — or rather, in the time before the world became what it is now — there was a different world. The beings who inhabited that world were not fully differentiated: they were neither fully human nor fully animal, neither fully material nor fully spiritual. The world was malleable, not yet fixed in its present forms.

Then Moon came.

In Snoqualmie cosmology, Moon (*čələ́kʷu* in Lushootseed) is not the distant celestial body of European astronomy, not a rock orbiting the Earth, not a symbol. Moon is a person — a Transformer — a being of enormous power who traveled through the world and shaped it into its present form. Where Moon passed, things became what they are now. The features of the landscape — the waterfalls, the mountain peaks, the river bends, the unusual rock formations — are records of Moon's passage, stories solidified into geography.

Snoqualmie Falls is the most important of these records. According to the oral tradition, Moon created the falls as a special place: a house for the spirit people who inhabit the mist, a site of concentrated spiritual power, a place where the membrane between the human world and the spirit world was made deliberately thin so that communication between them would be possible. The falls was not an accident of geology. It was a deliberate act of creation, made by a being with intention and purpose.

This cosmological framework — the Transformer as active agent who shapes the world — is common throughout the Pacific Northwest. Among the Coast Salish peoples, the Transformer figure takes various forms: sometimes Raven, sometimes Coyote, sometimes a being specific to a particular cultural tradition. What these figures share is the quality of active engagement with the world: they do not create the world ex nihilo and then stand back; they move through it, encountering problems, making decisions, transforming beings and landscapes as they go. The result is a world that is everywhere marked by intention — everywhere, if you know how to read it, a record of meaningful action.

The implications of this cosmology for how the Snoqualmie people relate to their landscape are profound. If every significant feature of the landscape was created by a powerful being for a purpose, then every significant feature of the landscape is meaningful — carries a story, embodies an intention, participates in a continuing relationship with the human beings who inhabit that landscape. You do not simply use a landscape. You live within a story that the landscape is telling.

### The Spirit World: sxʷəl

The Lushootseed concept of *sxʷəl* is difficult to translate. It is sometimes rendered as "spirit" or "power" or "spiritual force," but none of these English words fully captures it. *Sxʷəl* is not a supernatural category separate from the natural world; it is a quality that permeates the natural world. Living things have *sxʷəl*. Places of particular power have concentrated *sxʷəl*. The salmon have *sxʷəl*. The cedar tree has *sxʷəl*. Snoqualmie Falls has *sxʷəl* in such abundance that it radiates outward and can be felt by those who are attuned to it.

The spirit world — the realm of beings whose primary existence is *sxʷəl* rather than physical matter — interpenetrates the human world rather than existing in a separate place. The beings of the spirit world are present in the human world; they are simply not always visible to ordinary human perception. A person with developed spiritual sensitivity — through the discipline of the guardian spirit quest, or through natural gift, or through the training of a shaman — can perceive and interact with these beings in ways that others cannot.

This is not fantasy or superstition. Within the Snoqualmie framework, it is a sophisticated account of the structure of reality — one that emphasizes the relational nature of existence (everything is connected to everything else through the medium of *sxʷəl*), the porousness of categories that Western thinking tends to keep separate (natural/supernatural, human/animal, living/dead), and the importance of maintaining right relationship with the nonhuman world.

### Guardian Spirits: sqʷəlalitut

Every person has the potential to acquire a guardian spirit — a *sqʷəlalitut* (roughly, "one's guardian power" or "one's spiritual helper"). The guardian spirit is not chosen by the person but by the spirit itself, which recognizes something in the seeker and chooses to enter into a relationship of mutual support and obligation.

The acquisition of a guardian spirit is the central spiritual achievement of an individual's life. It confers abilities, protection, and a particular kind of power — the specific power associated with the nature of the spirit. A person whose guardian spirit is a hawk might have exceptional visual acuity or the power of swift decisive action. A person whose spirit is a cedar tree might have unusual strength and endurance. A person whose spirit is associated with water might become an exceptional fisherman or a healer of water-related illnesses.

The guardian spirit is acquired through the *séwaLəb* — the spirit quest — in which a young person (traditionally in late childhood or early adolescence, but sometimes later) goes alone into the wilderness for an extended period, fasting, bathing in cold water, and remaining open to encounter. The spirit, if it chooses to come, arrives in the form of a vision or encounter — something experienced as absolutely real, not as a metaphor or a dream. The encounter establishes a relationship that lasts for life.

The guardian spirit relationship is not merely personal. It connects the individual to the larger spiritual geography of the world — to the beings who inhabit that geography, to the *sxʷəl* that flows through it. A person with a strong guardian spirit relationship is, in a meaningful sense, more deeply embedded in the world than one without.

The guardian spirit must be honored. It must be called upon appropriately. If neglected, it may withdraw — leaving the person vulnerable, less able, spiritually adrift. The winter spirit dances (discussed in Part VI) are partly a mechanism for maintaining the guardian spirit relationship — for renewing it annually, for allowing the spirit's energy to flow through the person in a social and ceremonial context.

### Sacred Geography

In the Snoqualmie world, sacred geography is not a special category of exceptional sites set apart from ordinary landscape. Every significant feature of the landscape has spiritual significance. But some places are more concentrated with power, more explicitly associated with spiritual beings, more important in the ceremonial and cosmological life of the community.

Snoqualmie Falls is the supreme example — the most powerful site in the Snoqualmie world, the center around which the rest of the spiritual geography orients itself. But there are others: particular river pools where spirits are known to reside; mountain passes where travelers must show respect and make offerings; old-growth cedar trees of unusual size or form that embody particular *sxʷəl*; the grave sites of powerful ancestors who continue to exert influence from the spirit world.

This sacred geography is encoded in the oral tradition — in the stories that explain why this rock formation exists here, why this bend in the river has a particular name, why this mountain is approached with particular caution. The stories are not decorative additions to a landscape that exists independently of them. They are the landscape's meaning, its instruction manual, its record of the relationships that constitute a living world.

When the Snoqualmie people were dispossessed of their land, they were not merely losing real estate. They were being separated from the texts that made the world intelligible — from the places where their cosmology was embedded, where their obligations to the nonhuman world were located, where the ongoing relationships that sustained life were maintained. No reservation could have restored everything that was lost. But the absence of any reservation at all — the refusal to set aside even a small piece of the original landscape — was a particularly complete form of spiritual violence.

## Part IV — Village Life and Social Structure

### A Village-Based Society

The sdukʷalbixʷ were not a nomadic people, despite the common misconception that all pre-contact Indigenous peoples of North America were hunters constantly in motion. They were a village-based society with permanent winter settlements and seasonal movement to follow food resources — a pattern sometimes called "tethered nomadism" but more accurately described as a sophisticated system of seasonal resource use centered on fixed home bases.

The Snoqualmie River valley supported a series of interconnected village communities. These were not isolated settlements; they were nodes in a network of kinship, trade, and ceremonial relationship that extended across the Puget Sound lowlands and over the Cascade passes to the peoples of the Columbia Plateau. People moved between villages regularly — for marriage, for ceremony, for trade, for seasonal resource harvesting. The village was the basic unit of social organization, but identity extended beyond the village to a broader sense of being sdukʷalbixʷ — of belonging to the people of this particular river valley and the particular sacred geography it contained.

### The Village Sites

The principal permanent settlement of the Snoqualmie was near the falls itself — the falls village, situated on the river near *snoqʷəlʔ*, was the spiritual and political center of sdukʷalbixʷ territory. Its proximity to the most sacred site in the Snoqualmie world made it a place of special significance; major ceremonies were conducted here, important visitors received, and the annual First Salmon Ceremony performed at the falls.

Further down the river, near the confluence of the Snoqualmie and Tolt rivers (the Tolt, known in Lushootseed as *si'aL*), was another major village. The Tolt River confluence was an important fishing location, a meeting point for different branches of the Snoqualmie people, and — in the period of treaty negotiations — a place associated with Chief Patkanim. The modern town of Carnation sits roughly where this village community was located.

Additional villages were distributed along the lower Snoqualmie Valley, near other fishing sites, camas meadows, and resource-gathering locations. The people were riverine people: their settlements followed the river and its tributaries, oriented toward the salmon runs and the rich ecology of the riparian zone.

### The Longhouse

The physical center of village life was the longhouse — in Lushootseed, *ʔiišəd*, though the architectural tradition varied somewhat by location and period. These were large cedar plank structures, typically rectangular, sometimes extending to lengths of one hundred feet or more, housing multiple family groups under one roof.

The construction of a longhouse was a major undertaking. Western red cedar (*xpáyəq* in Lushootseed) was the preferred building material — its straight grain allowed it to be split into planks with minimal tools, its natural oils made it extraordinarily resistant to rot, and its weight and stability made it structurally sound. The great cedar trees of the Snoqualmie Valley — some of them eight, ten, twelve feet in diameter — provided materials of a quality and scale unavailable anywhere else in the world. A single large cedar could yield enough planks for a significant portion of a longhouse.

The longhouse served multiple functions simultaneously. It was a residence — the extended family groups who occupied it slept and ate within its walls. It was a workshop — weaving, tool-making, food processing, and the hundred other activities of daily life took place here. It was a social center — the space where community decisions were made, disputes resolved, and relationships negotiated. And it was a ceremonial space — the winter spirit dances and other ceremonies were conducted inside the longhouse, the central fire creating the combination of heat, light, and smoke that was part of the ceremonial atmosphere.

Inside the longhouse, space was allocated by family group and by social status. High-born families — the *si'ab*, the hereditary elite — occupied the places of honor near the entrance and the central fire. Lower-status families occupied the ends. The spatial organization of the longhouse embodied the social organization of the community.

### Social Organization: si'ab and Kinship

Snoqualmie society was organized along lines of hereditary status. The *si'ab* — the high-born, the people of rank — occupied a position analogous to an aristocracy, though the comparison is imperfect. Their status derived from birth and from the accumulation and display of wealth through the potlatch. Below them were commoners (*stab*), and below commoners were slaves — captured from other peoples in raids, or occasionally received in trade.

The *si'ab* were expected to demonstrate their status through generosity — through the ability to host elaborate potlatches at which they gave away rather than merely accumulated. A *si'ab* family that hoarded its wealth without redistributing it lost respect; the social logic of the potlatch was that the accumulation of wealth was only legitimate if it was periodically dispersed. This created a redistributive mechanism that moved resources through the community and prevented the kind of extreme wealth concentration that characterizes modern capitalist societies.

Kinship was bilateral — descent was traced through both the mother's and father's lines — with a tendency toward the father's line for the inheritance of high-status names and ceremonial privileges. Marriage alliances were crucial mechanisms for maintaining and extending social networks. A high-status Snoqualmie family might arrange marriages with Duwamish families to the west, Skykomish families to the north, and — through the Snoqualmie Pass trade route — even with Yakama families to the east. These marriage connections created webs of obligation and reciprocity that facilitated trade, provided allies in times of conflict, and spread biological and cultural diversity across a wide area.

### The Potlatch

The potlatch (*paʔtəd* in various Coast Salish languages) was the central institution of Northwest Coast social and economic life — and it was so profoundly alien to European capitalist assumptions that American and Canadian authorities spent decades trying to suppress it.

A potlatch was a feast and ceremonial gathering hosted by a person or family of status, at which the host gave away large quantities of wealth — food, blankets, canoes, copper objects, and other valuables — to the assembled guests. The more that was given away, the greater the honor accruing to the host. Guests at a potlatch were simultaneously witnesses to the host's claims of status, validators of those claims (by their presence and acceptance of gifts), and recipients of generosity. A guest who received at one potlatch was obligated to give in return when they hosted, creating a circulation of wealth and obligation that knit communities together.

Potlatches marked important social transitions: the formal claiming of an inherited name or ceremonial privilege, the mourning of a death, the marriage of a high-status child, the installation of a new leader. They were, in effect, the public record system of a society without writing — the occasion at which important social facts were publicly witnessed and therefore made real.

The redistribution function of the potlatch was economically sophisticated. In a subsistence economy subject to significant year-to-year variation in resources — some salmon runs were exceptional, some were poor; some camas harvests were abundant, some were thin — the obligation to give away surplus wealth in good years and to receive from others in bad years created an insurance system of considerable effectiveness. No family went hungry as long as there were families among their network who had had good years and were therefore obligated to be generous.

### Slaves and the Limits of Idealization

The presence of slavery in Northwest Coast societies must be acknowledged honestly. The sdukʷalbixʷ, like other peoples of the region, took slaves in raids on neighboring peoples, and the keeping of slaves was part of the social fabric of high-status households. Slaves had no rights, no status, and could be killed at the whim of their owners — most notoriously, as part of mortuary ceremonies for high-status people.

This practice does not make the Snoqualmie exceptional among the world's peoples — slavery has been nearly universal across human cultures and historical periods. But it complicates any effort to idealize pre-contact Snoqualmie society as purely harmonious and egalitarian. It was not. It was a complex society with hierarchy, exploitation, and violence alongside its sophisticated ecological knowledge, its rich ceremonial life, and its redistributive social institutions.

Acknowledging this is not a concession to those who would use Indigenous slavery to minimize the crimes of colonialism — "they had slaves too" is not an argument for colonialism, and two wrongs do not make a right. It is simply a commitment to honesty, which serves the Snoqualmie people better than sanitized idealization.

## Part V — Material Culture: Cedar, Salmon, and the Seasonal Round

### The Tree of Life

Western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) — *xpáyəq* in Lushootseed — was the foundation of material life for the Snoqualmie and their neighbors. If you had to identify a single species whose presence or absence determined the shape of Northwest Coast civilization, it would be cedar. The tree is extraordinary: it lives for over a thousand years, reaches heights of two hundred feet and diameters of fifteen feet or more, its wood is light and strong, its grain is perfectly straight and easily split, and its natural oils make it almost immune to rot. In the dense, wet forests of western Washington, where most other wood species rot within a few decades, cedar endures.

From cedar, the Snoqualmie made virtually everything.

**Canoes** were the primary transportation technology — without roads, the rivers and Puget Sound itself were the highways, and a well-made cedar canoe was a fast, maneuverable, and seaworthy vessel. The ocean-going canoes of the outer coast could be sixty feet long, capable of carrying a ton of cargo and crossing open water in conditions that would challenge a modern small craft. The river canoes of the Snoqualmie were smaller and more agile, optimized for the particular conditions of their waterways.

A dugout canoe was made from a single cedar log, selected with great care, felled with fire and adze, shaped over months of painstaking work, steamed to spread the hull and give it its characteristic flared profile. A good canoe represented months of skilled labor. It was not merely a tool; it was a work of art and a source of pride.

**Baskets** of cedar bark and cedar root were the primary containers — for food storage, for cooking (by dropping hot stones into water-filled baskets), for berry picking, for carrying loads. The basketry tradition of the Snoqualmie and their neighbors was technically sophisticated: twined and coiled baskets of remarkable tightness and elegance, some waterproof enough for cooking, decorated with geometric designs that encoded cultural meanings and family identities. A master basket-maker spent years developing her skill (basketry was primarily women's work, though not exclusively) and produced objects of beauty and precision that contemporary craft traditions struggle to equal.

**Clothing** was made from shredded cedar bark — softened by pounding and then woven or wrapped into garments. In warmer weather, cedar bark clothing was often supplemented or replaced by garments of animal skin. Rain capes of woven cedar bark were functional waterproofing. Cedar bark hats, woven in conical shapes, shed rain and provided shade. The technology was elegant — using the most locally abundant material to address the most pressing weather challenge (the Pacific Northwest's extraordinary rainfall) with minimal processing.

**The longhouse** has been described in the previous chapter. Add to this the bentwood boxes — steamed and bent cedar planks joined at the corners without nails to create waterproof, vermin-resistant containers for food storage and the keeping of valuables — and the burial boxes in which the dead were placed. Cedar was present at every stage of Snoqualmie life, from cradle to grave.

**Spiritual uses of cedar** were extensive. Cedar bark was burned as incense and cleansing smoke. Cedar boughs were used in purification ceremonies. The great cedar trees themselves were understood as beings of power — *si'ab* of the plant world — whose *sxʷəl* could be called upon for healing and protection.

### Salmon: The Economic and Spiritual Foundation

The Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.) is one of the most remarkable animals on Earth. Born in freshwater, it migrates to the ocean where it spends most of its adult life, then returns unerringly to the specific stream of its birth to spawn and die — enriching the freshwater ecosystem with marine nutrients and feeding not just the human beings who harvest it but the eagles, bears, wolves, and forest trees that depend on the annual salmon cycle.

For the Snoqualmie, salmon was everything. The five species — chinook, coho, chum, pink, and sockeye — ran in different seasons, providing a near-year-round supply of protein when their runs overlapped. A good salmon year meant abundant food, the ability to accumulate surplus for winter, the wherewithal to host potlatches and maintain social obligations. The salmon were not merely food. They were, in the Snoqualmie understanding, persons — the Salmon People — who chose to offer their bodies to human beings in an act of gift that required reciprocation through ceremony, through careful treatment of the remains (bones returned to the water so the Salmon People could reconstitute themselves and return), and through the maintenance of the river systems that supported the salmon life cycle.

Salmon fishing techniques were sophisticated and varied. **Weirs** — barrier structures built across the river that directed salmon into traps — were constructed at key points in the river, particularly at riffles where the water was shallow enough and the bottom rocky enough to anchor the structure. Weirs required collective labor to build and maintain; they were community infrastructure, not individual property. **Dip nets** — large nets on frames that were dipped into the current to catch passing salmon — were used at falls and rapids where salmon concentrated. **Spearing** from platforms or from canoes was practiced for larger fish. **Traps** of woven willow or cedar stakes directed fish into enclosures from which they could not escape.

The management of salmon resources was not simply a matter of taking what the river offered. The Snoqualmie, like other Pacific Northwest peoples, understood — through thousands of years of observation — that the salmon runs could be depleted by overfishing and that restraint in one year was an investment in future abundance. The First Salmon Ceremony was not merely a religious observance; it was a management mechanism. By requiring that the first salmon of the run be treated with particular reverence, with its bones returned intact to the water, and by establishing a period of ritualized restraint before full-scale harvesting began, the ceremony encoded sustainable harvest practices in ceremonial form — ensuring that at least some salmon completed their spawning run before the harvest reached maximum intensity.

### The Camas Meadows and the Plant World

Salmon was the foundation, but the Snoqualmie diet rested on a diverse base of plant foods as well. The most important was camas (*Camassia quamash*) — a bulb plant that grows in wet meadows and produces a showy blue flower in spring. Camas bulbs were harvested in late spring and summer (when the flowers made identification of the edible species unmistakable — avoiding deadly camas, which is similar in appearance), and either eaten fresh or slow-cooked in pit ovens for preservation. Properly cooked and dried, camas could be stored for months and traded widely.

The camas meadows of the Snoqualmie Valley were not accidental features of the landscape. They were, at least in part, managed — through burning, through selective harvesting that left seed bulbs in place, through the maintenance of the wet, open meadow conditions that camas requires. The Snoqualmie Valley's bottomlands, today largely converted to pasture and farmland, were once dotted with camas meadows that represented centuries of careful cultivation without the European model of enclosed fields and single-crop agriculture.

**Wapato** (*Sagittaria latifolia*) — arrowleaf, or Indian potato — grew in the shallow margins of lakes and slow-moving water. Its starchy tubers were dug with the toes in winter, when other foods were scarce, and were an important supplement to the autumn salmon surplus. **Bracken fern** rhizomes were roasted and eaten. **Salal berries**, **huckleberries**, **salmonberries**, **blackberries**, **elderberries**, and **Oregon grape** provided fruit in summer and early fall, some preserved by drying for winter use. **Stinging nettle** was harvested in spring for both food and fiber — its stem fibers, when processed, could be spun into strong cord.

### The Seasonal Round

The Snoqualmie year was not a monotonous cycle of the same activities repeated. It was a precisely calibrated rotation through different resource zones and different ecological opportunities — a schedule built from thousands of years of observation of the valley's rhythms.

**Winter** (November through February): The community was concentrated in the longhouse villages. Winter was the ceremonial season — the time of spirit dances, of storytelling, of the transmission of oral tradition, of the most intense social and spiritual life. Food from the autumn salmon harvest, preserved by smoking and drying, sustained the community. The winter season was not leisure; the longhouse was a workshop as well as a ceremony house, and the production of baskets, tools, clothing, and other necessities continued. But the pace was different from the active seasons of harvest.

**Spring** (March through May): The first stirrings of the food year. Nettles and other early greens were harvested. Camas began to bloom, signaling the approach of the harvest season. Waterfowl arrived in abundance on the wetlands. The first runs of steelhead and salmon began in some streams. The community began to extend outward from the winter villages, moving to fishing camps, gathering locations, and trading visits to neighboring peoples.

**Summer** (June through August): The peak of activity. Multiple salmon runs overlapped. Camas and other bulbs were harvested in the meadows. Berries ripened in the uplands. Families dispersed across the resource landscape, each working its traditional gathering areas, fishing its hereditary weir sites, picking its family's berry grounds. The high mountain meadows became accessible, and hunting parties ranged into the Cascades for elk and deer.

**Autumn** (September through October): The great salmon runs reached their peak — particularly the chinook and coho runs that were the foundation of the winter food supply. Smoking and drying facilities worked at maximum capacity. The community's surplus for the year was determined by the intensity and quality of the autumn salmon harvest. A good autumn meant a comfortable winter and the ability to host potlatches; a poor one meant a hungry spring.

This was not a primitive economy. It was a sophisticated system of diversified resource management, adapted to a specific landscape over thousands of years, producing reliable abundance without destroying the resource base. The contrast with industrial salmon fishing — which depleted Pacific salmon runs to a fraction of their historical abundance within a century of full-scale commercial operation — is instructive.

## Part VI — Ceremony, Ritual, and Spiritual Practice

### The First Salmon Ceremony

When the first salmon of the spring run appeared in the river, it was not treated as food. It was treated as a guest.

The First Salmon Ceremony was conducted with the solemnity appropriate to receiving an honored visitor. The first salmon caught was brought ashore and placed on a clean mat facing upstream. It was addressed with words of welcome and gratitude. It was offered cedar smoke. The community gathered to receive it.

Then the fish was prepared ceremonially and shared among the community. Every person present ate a small portion. The act of communal eating was sacramental: a renewal of the covenant between the Salmon People and the human people, a confirmation that the relationship of mutual obligation was intact, that the salmon would continue to give themselves to the people as long as the people continued to honor that gift.

After the meal, the bones of the first salmon — every bone, carefully preserved — were returned to the river. In the Snoqualmie understanding, the Salmon People could reconstitute themselves from their returned bones and rejoin the sea, eventually returning again in subsequent years. The destruction of the bones, or their careless disposal, would break the cycle. This was not superstition. It was an encoded understanding, expressed in ceremonial terms, that the salmon runs required careful management and that disrespect for the fish would have consequences.

The First Salmon Ceremony was conducted at Snoqualmie Falls. The falls was where the salmon entered the river's spiritual domain, where the covenant was annually renewed, where the sacred and the practical were most completely united. The power of the falls amplified the ceremony. The mist-beings who inhabited the gorge were witnesses.

### The Spirit Quest

The guardian spirit quest was the individual's most important spiritual undertaking — the experience that would shape the rest of their life, determining their special abilities, their relationship to the spirit world, and in many cases their vocation.

Traditionally, the quest was undertaken in late childhood or early adolescence, when the young person was old enough to survive several days alone in the wilderness but young enough for the spirit encounter to be formative. The preparation was extended and serious: bathing in cold water repeatedly at specific power spots on the river, fasting, physical hardship, and spiritual instruction from elders. The goal was to put the body and mind in a state of receptive openness.

The seeker went alone into the wilderness and remained alone for days or weeks. They fasted. They bathed in cold streams. They kept vigil. They waited.

The encounter, when it came, was absolutely real — not a waking dream or a metaphor, but an actual meeting with a being from the spirit world. The spirit communicated its nature, its powers, and its requirements. It entered into a relationship of mutual obligation with the seeker. The seeker returned transformed — with new capabilities, new responsibilities, and a lifelong companion in the spirit world.

The guardian spirit relationship required ongoing maintenance. The spirit's song — learned during the encounter — had to be sung at appropriate times. The spirit's preferences had to be respected. Failure to maintain the relationship could result in the spirit's withdrawal, leaving the person spiritually unprotected.

In the colonial period, when children were sent to boarding schools and forbidden to participate in traditional practices, the opportunity to undertake the guardian spirit quest was systematically denied to generations of young Snoqualmie people. This was not a minor cultural disruption. It was the amputation of the mechanism by which individuals were connected to the spiritual dimension of their world — a wound that cascaded through families and communities for generations.

### The Winter Spirit Dances

The most powerful ceremony of the Snoqualmie year — and arguably the most powerful still practiced today — was the winter spirit dance, sometimes called the smokehouse dance or winter dance. This ceremony is alive. It is practiced. It is not an artifact of the past.

Because the winter spirit dance is a living ceremony, there are aspects of it that are not appropriate to describe in a public text. What can be said is this:

The winter spirit dance season runs from approximately November to March — the dark, cold months when the community is gathered in the longhouse. The ceremony involves the emergence and expression of guardian spirit power through the medium of song and dance. A person who has been called to dance must give the spirit's power a channel of expression in the human world.

The dances are communal: the community gathers around the dancer, witnessing, supporting, singing. The ceremonial fire, the drumming, the heat and smoke of the longhouse — all create conditions in which the boundary between the human world and the spirit world grows thin. The dances last for days. The physical demands are extreme.

In the colonial period, American authorities used social and religious pressure to suppress the spirit dances, identifying them correctly as the most resistant core of traditional spiritual practice. Despite decades of sustained pressure, the spirit dances were never entirely extinguished. They were practiced in reduced form, in secret when necessary. The knowledge was preserved in families, passed from elders to the young people who came seeking it.

The revival of the winter spirit dances in the second half of the twentieth century — as part of the broader Indigenous cultural revival across the Pacific Northwest — was not a reconstruction of a dead tradition. It was the return of a practice that had never fully died.

### Slahal: The Bone Game

Slahal (also spelled lahal, or called the "bone game" in English) is a guessing game played with small bones — one marked, one unmarked — hidden in the hands of the opposing team. Players hide the bones, sing power songs, and use spiritual concentration to confuse their opponents. The opposing team tries to guess which hand holds the unmarked bone.

This description makes it sound like a parlor game. It is not.

Slahal is a form of spiritual competition. The power songs are real medicine — they invoke the player's guardian spirit to confuse and misdirect the opponent's perception. Skilled slahal players are people with strong spiritual power, and a team that wins is understood to have demonstrated genuine spiritual superiority, not just luck. High-stakes games between competing villages involved not just the players but the entire community, with large amounts of wealth wagered and the spiritual reputation of the community on the line.

Slahal is another ceremony that was never extinguished. It is played throughout the Pacific Northwest today, at gatherings and celebrations, with the same singing and the same spiritual intensity that have always accompanied it. It is one of the most visible continuities between pre-contact Snoqualmie culture and the present.

### Shamans and Healing

The shaman occupied a particular role in Snoqualmie society: the person whose guardian spirit relationship was so developed and whose spiritual sensitivity was so acute that they could work in the spirit world on behalf of others. The shaman diagnosed illness, conducted healing ceremonies, retrieved lost souls from the spirit world, and managed relations with the more dangerous spirit beings.

Illness, in the Snoqualmie framework, was rarely purely physical. It was typically the loss of the soul, the intrusion of a harmful spirit, or the consequence of broken relationship — with a spirit, with the community, with the natural world. The shaman's role was diagnostic and therapeutic in this broader framework: identifying the spiritual cause of illness and addressing it through ceremony and the focused application of spiritual power.

Shamans were not always comfortable figures. Their power was double-edged: those who could heal could also harm, and shamans were sometimes feared as well as respected. The tension between the healer's power and its potential for misuse was part of the social dynamic surrounding them.

### What Remains Private

Not everything in Snoqualmie ceremonial life belongs in a public text. Some ceremonies, some songs, some ritual objects, and some aspects of spiritual practice are held by specific families or ceremonial societies and are not intended for general circulation. The winter spirit dances include elements — specific songs, specific ritual processes — shared only within the ceremonial community.

This book respects those boundaries. The existence of private knowledge is acknowledged here to make an important point: the fact that some knowledge is not written down, is not publicly accessible, is transmitted only through living relationship — this is not a deficiency of the tradition. It is the tradition. The knowledge that matters most travels through human relationships, not through texts.

## Part VII — The Catastrophes: Contact, Smallpox, and the Great Dying

### First Contact

The first Europeans to enter the Snoqualmie world came by sea. Captain George Vancouver, commanding HMS Discovery, sailed through the Strait of Juan de Fuca and into Puget Sound in May 1792, charting the waterways with the systematic precision of a Royal Navy surveyor and naming every inlet and promontory after officers of the British crown and minor English geography. Lieutenant Peter Puget, for whom the Sound is named, led smaller boats into the southern reaches of the inland sea. Lieutenant William Broughton explored the Snohomish River delta area.

Vancouver's crew encountered the Coast Salish peoples of Puget Sound and described them as generally peaceable, curious about the strange ships and their contents, and willing to trade. The descriptions in Vancouver's journals are the first written records of the peoples of the Puget Sound lowlands — including, implicitly, the sdukʷalbixʷ, whose territory lay in the river valleys feeding the eastern shore of the Sound.

But the record does not begin with Vancouver. It begins with catastrophe.

### The Great Dying: Smallpox Before Contact

By the time Vancouver sailed into Puget Sound in 1792, the peoples of the Pacific Northwest had already been devastated by a disease they had never encountered and had no immunity to resist. Smallpox (*Variola major*) had reached the Northwest Coast before sustained European contact through the network of Indigenous trade routes that connected the Pacific Coast to the Great Plains and the Southwest, where Spanish colonizers had been spreading the disease for two centuries.

The first great smallpox pandemic to reach the Pacific Northwest occurred in the 1770s — approximately twenty years before Vancouver's voyage. It moved through the Indigenous population of the region with a speed and lethality that is almost impossible to comprehend. Smallpox mortality in populations with no prior exposure typically ran between 30% and 90% of those infected. In the dense longhouse villages of the Puget Sound region, where extended family groups lived in close quarters and physical care of the sick was a social obligation that brought caregivers into direct contact with patients, conditions were near-optimal for disease transmission.

Scholarly estimates of the depopulation of Pacific Northwest Indigenous peoples in the decades surrounding European contact vary, but most estimates suggest that between 50% and 90% of the pre-contact population died in the smallpox epidemics of the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. This is not a minor statistic. If the lower estimate is correct, at least half of every village died. If the higher estimate is closer to the truth, nine out of ten people died.

The physical and social effects of such catastrophic mortality are almost incomprehensible. Village after village was reduced to a handful of survivors. Social hierarchies were destroyed as elders and hereditary leaders died alongside commoners. Oral tradition was disrupted as the elders who carried specific knowledge died before they could transmit it. Ritual specialists, healers, and shamans — who were most exposed to the sick through their professional obligations — may have died at disproportionate rates. Children grew up in communities defined by absence — surrounded by the memory of people they had never known.

Vancouver noted in his journals that the peoples he encountered seemed to be living among the ruins of much larger communities — abandoned longhouses, unfilled graves, the physical evidence of recent massive population loss. He did not understand what he was seeing. He thought perhaps there had been a recent war. He was looking at the aftermath of a disease that had arrived before him and done more damage than any army.

### Subsequent Epidemics

Smallpox returned. An 1836-37 epidemic devastated the Columbia Plateau and the interior Pacific Northwest. An 1862 epidemic — when a smallpox-infected passenger arrived by ship in Victoria, British Columbia, and the colonial government expelled Indigenous peoples from the town rather than quarantining the infected — killed an estimated one-third of the Indigenous population of the British Columbia coast. By the mid-nineteenth century, the population of Puget Sound Indigenous peoples was a fraction — perhaps one-tenth — of what it had been a century earlier.

This context is essential for understanding the treaty period. When Isaac Stevens sat down with the leaders of the Puget Sound peoples in 1855, he was negotiating with the survivors of a century-long demographic catastrophe. The villages he visited were half-empty. The elders who might have provided experienced counsel on treaty negotiations had, in many cases, died in the epidemics. The social fabric that might have produced a more unified Indigenous response to American expansion had been shredded.

The Snoqualmie who signed the Point Elliott Treaty in 1855 were not the Snoqualmie of 1750. They were the survivors, and the children of survivors, of something very close to genocide — however that genocide was accomplished, whether by deliberate policy or by the terrible logic of infectious disease in an immunologically naive population.

### The Hudson's Bay Company and American Settlement

The Hudson's Bay Company established Fort Nisqually on the shores of Puget Sound in 1833, creating the first permanent European settlement in the Puget Sound basin. The Company's impact on the region's Indigenous peoples was complex: it created trade relationships that were, in some respects, mutually beneficial in the short term (Indigenous peoples exchanging furs for metal tools, blankets, and other manufactured goods), while simultaneously accelerating the transformation of the regional ecology, spreading new diseases, introducing alcohol, and establishing the infrastructure of economic dependence that would leave Indigenous peoples vulnerable to the political changes that followed.

American settlers began arriving in the Puget Sound region in significant numbers in the 1840s, following the opening of the Oregon Trail. The Donation Land Act of 1850 offered 320 acres of free land to any white male settler (and 640 acres to a married couple) who settled in the Oregon Territory — which included what would become Washington State. The land being offered was, of course, not unoccupied. It was the homeland of dozens of Indigenous peoples, all of whom had their own systems of land use, territorial boundaries, and political organization. The Donation Land Act simply did not recognize these facts. In the logic of the American state, Indigenous occupancy did not constitute ownership in the legal sense that the state recognized, and therefore the land was legally available for distribution to white settlers.

This legal sleight of hand — the refusal to recognize Indigenous land rights while simultaneously demanding that Indigenous peoples submit to American jurisdiction — set the stage for the treaty period.

### Christian Missionaries and the Assault on Ceremony

Christian missionaries — both Catholic and Protestant — preceded or accompanied American settlement in the Pacific Northwest. The Catholics, particularly the Oblate order and individual missionaries like Father Francis Blanchet, established missions among the Puget Sound peoples beginning in the 1840s. Protestant missionaries arrived in the wake of settlement.

The missionary enterprise was explicitly an assault on Indigenous culture. Conversion to Christianity was understood to require the abandonment of traditional spiritual practices: the guardian spirit quest was reframed as diabolism, the winter spirit dances as heathen revelry, the shamans as servants of the devil. The First Salmon Ceremony was an abomination before the Lord. The entire cosmological framework within which the Snoqualmie understood their world was to be replaced by a framework imported from the eastern Mediterranean two thousand years earlier and filtered through the particular cultural anxieties of nineteenth-century Anglo-American Protestantism and French Canadian Catholicism.

Some Snoqualmie adopted Christianity. The reasons were various: genuine conviction in some cases, social and economic pressure in others, the pragmatic recognition that visible Christianity might provide some protection from the worst treatment by settlers and government officials. But adoption of the outward forms of Christian observance did not necessarily mean the abandonment of traditional practice. Ceremonies continued, adapted to circumstances, went underground when necessary.

The missionaries themselves often recorded their frustration at the persistence of traditional practices among their converts — evidence that the people were not as thoroughly converted as the missionaries wished to believe. Colonialism rarely achieves total cultural victory. It achieves suppression, distortion, and damage — but rarely total victory.

## Part VIII — The 1855 Point Elliott Treaty and Its Betrayal

### Isaac Stevens and the Treaty Council

Isaac Ingalls Stevens was a man in a hurry. Appointed the first Governor of Washington Territory in 1853, at the age of thirty-five, he arrived in the Northwest with a mandate to clear the land for American settlement and the railroad that would follow. He was intelligent, energetic, ambitious, and entirely confident that American expansion was the will of Providence and that the Indigenous peoples of the region would benefit from the civilization he was bringing them. He was also, in retrospect, a man whose confidence in his own understanding vastly exceeded his actual comprehension of what he was doing.

In late 1854 and early 1855, Stevens organized a series of treaty councils with the Indigenous peoples of Washington Territory, moving through the region with remarkable speed and producing agreements at each stop. The Point Elliott Treaty, negotiated at Mukilteo on January 22, 1855, was one of these — a gathering of the Puget Sound peoples at which, in a single day, Stevens sought and obtained signatures (marks, mostly — few of the Indigenous signatories wrote English) from the representatives of dozens of peoples whose territorial interests, cultural identities, and political structures he only partially understood.

The council at Mukilteo brought together thousands of people. The United States government's representatives presented the treaty in English, with translation provided by intermediaries whose fluency in the full complexity of legal language was, to put it charitably, limited. The Indigenous leaders who spoke responded with eloquence and seriousness — the surviving transcripts of treaty councils from this period, imperfect as they are, reveal people grappling seriously with the implications of what was being proposed. They were not naive. They understood that something important was happening. They did not fully understand what they were agreeing to, not because they were incapable of understanding but because the full implications of American land law, the allotment system, the reservation system, and the long-term intentions of the American state were not visible from where they stood in 1855.

### What Stevens Promised

The Point Elliott Treaty made specific promises to the signatory peoples. In exchange for ceding their lands to the United States, the tribes were promised:

**Reservations:** The treaty provided for the establishment of reservations where the ceding peoples could continue to live. The Snoqualmie and their allies were to have a reservation — the treaty specifically contemplated this. The reservation would be their land, held in trust by the federal government, where their communities could maintain themselves and their way of life.

**Fishing rights:** Crucially, the treaty preserved the right of the signatory peoples to fish "at all usual and accustomed grounds and stations" — a provision that would, a century and a quarter later, be interpreted by the federal courts to mean that the tribes retained half the harvestable salmon in western Washington. This fishing rights provision, dormant for most of the treaty's history, would eventually be vindicated in the landmark 1974 Boldt Decision (United States v. Washington). In 1855, however, it was understood primarily as a reassurance that the people's subsistence fishing would not be prohibited.

**Annuities and services:** The treaty promised regular payments of goods and money, a physician, a school, and other services to the signatory peoples. These promises were inconsistently fulfilled at best.

**Protection:** The United States government promised to protect the treaty peoples from encroachment by settlers and to enforce the terms of the agreement.

### What the Snoqualmie Leaders Understood

The Snoqualmie and their allies understood the treaty as an agreement, not a surrender. They understood themselves as ceding the right to exclusive use of their territory — as agreeing to share the land with the settlers who were coming whether they agreed or not — in exchange for guaranteed rights to their most important resources (the salmon), a homeland in the form of a reservation, and the protections of American law.

Whether they fully understood that the reserved lands would be separated from the ceded lands — that the reservation would be a specific, bounded piece of territory, not the entire landscape — is less clear. The concept of property as a bounded, exclusive, alienable right was fundamentally foreign to the Snoqualmie framework of relationship to land. The translators at the treaty council were working across a conceptual gulf that no translation could fully bridge.

What is certain is that the Snoqualmie who signed the treaty did not understand themselves to be agreeing to disappear. They were agreeing to a new political relationship while retaining their identity, their community, and their connection to their homeland.

### Chief Patkanim: The Complicated Figure

The most prominent Snoqualmie leader at the treaty council was Patkanim — a chief of the Snoqualmie who had cultivated a relationship with American settlers and authorities since the early 1850s. Patkanim was a complicated figure: politically astute, strategically flexible, willing to use his relationship with American authorities as a source of power and protection for his people, and apparently willing to sign away considerably more than some of his followers believed was theirs to sign away.

In the years before the treaty, Patkanim had allied with American forces against other Indigenous groups — fighting alongside American soldiers in engagements during the early 1850s, and even presenting the scalps of enemies to American authorities in exchange for payment. He has been described by some historians as a collaborator, by others as a pragmatist who understood that the coming of American settlement was inevitable and sought to position his people as favorably as possible within the new order.

What is clear is that Patkanim signed the Point Elliott Treaty, that he is recorded in the treaty documents as a principal leader of the Snoqualmie and several allied peoples, and that the reservation promised to the Snoqualmie at treaty was never established. Whether Patkanim understood this would happen, whether he was deceived, or whether subsequent American policy simply ignored what the treaty said — the result was the same. His people got nothing of what was promised.

Patkanim died in 1858, three years after the treaty, before the full dimensions of the betrayal were clear.

### The Snoqualmie Reservation: A Promise Never Kept

The Point Elliott Treaty contemplated a Snoqualmie reservation. The treaty language was not precise about location or size, and the reservation's establishment was left to subsequent executive action. That action never came — or rather, it came for other peoples and not for the Snoqualmie.

The Tulalip Reservation was established on the shores of Puget Sound, consolidating several treaty peoples. Other reservations were created for the Muckleshoot, the Puyallup, the Lummi, and other signatories of the various treaties Stevens negotiated in 1855. The Snoqualmie, despite being signatories to Point Elliott, were never assigned a reservation of their own.

The reasons are complex and not entirely clear from the historical record. The Snoqualmie's upper-valley location, somewhat distant from the main Puget Sound settlements; the disruption of the Puget Sound War of 1855-56, which consumed administrative attention; Patkanim's relatively cooperative relationship with American authorities (which may have reduced the urgency of formalizing their status); and the general chaos and corruption of the territorial period — all of these factors probably contributed to the oversight that became a century-long injustice.

### The Puget Sound War of 1855-56

The promises of the treaties were barely dry before war broke out. In October 1855, Leschi of the Nisqually people — outraged by the terms of the Medicine Creek Treaty, which had ceded almost all Nisqually territory including the rich bottomlands the Nisqually depended on — led an armed resistance against American settlement. The war spread across the region as other aggrieved peoples joined the uprising.

The Snoqualmie response was divided. Chief Patkanim, consistent with his earlier alignment with American authorities, sided with the Americans — providing warriors and scouts who fought against the uprising. Other Snoqualmie were less certain. The war was brief but destructive: settler farms were burned, Indigenous villages shelled by American gunboats on the rivers, and Leschi eventually captured, tried for murder (in a trial of deeply questionable legitimacy), and hanged in 1858.

The aftermath of the Puget Sound War deepened the injustice for the peoples who had, like the Snoqualmie, supported the Americans. Having fought alongside American forces, having demonstrated their loyalty in the most concrete possible terms, the Snoqualmie might have expected their treaty promises to be honored. Instead, they received nothing. The reservation was not established. The annuities were inconsistently paid or not paid at all. The machinery of American Indian policy simply failed to follow through on what had been promised.

The lesson was clear, even if no one in authority was willing to acknowledge it: loyalty to the United States government did not guarantee that the United States government would honor its obligations.

## Part IX — Dispossession and Suppression

### A People Without a Land Base

In the decades following the Point Elliott Treaty, the Snoqualmie found themselves in an extraordinary and painful position: signatories to a treaty with the United States, bearers of treaty rights that existed on paper, but without the one thing the treaty had promised that would have given those rights practical effect — a land base. They lived in their ancestral territory as, increasingly, legal nonentities: present in the landscape but absent from the administrative categories that determined who had rights, who received services, and who mattered to the American state.

Other treaty peoples, settled on reservations, had land that was legally protected from settler encroachment. Their children had access to reservation schools (however destructive those schools would prove to be). Their leaders had a recognized relationship with the Bureau of Indian Affairs. Their political identity had a physical anchor. The Snoqualmie had none of these things. They lived in the cracks of the American system — in the river valleys and upland forests of their ancestral territory, increasingly displaced by settlers who held legal title to the land, with no reservation to fall back on and no administrative home within the federal Indian bureaucracy.

This condition of landless treaty people, maintained for 144 years, is the defining fact of modern Snoqualmie history.

### The General Allotment Act of 1887

The Dawes Act — the General Allotment Act of 1887 — was designed to destroy tribal land bases by dividing reservation lands into individual allotments and selling the "surplus" (the substantial portion that exceeded what each individual was allocated) to white settlers. Theoretically, it applied to reservation peoples; the Snoqualmie had no reservation to allot. But the Dawes Act's underlying philosophy — the belief that Indigenous communal land ownership was an obstacle to civilization and that individual private property ownership was the path to assimilation — shaped the entire era's policy toward Indigenous peoples, whether or not they had reservations.

The Dawes Act's effect on those tribes it did apply to was devastating. The total Indian land base in the United States declined from 138 million acres in 1887 to approximately 48 million acres in 1934 — a loss of about 90 million acres, more than two-thirds of what remained after the treaty period. The "surplus" lands were overwhelmingly the most productive agricultural and timber lands on the reservations. The Dawes Act transferred those lands to white ownership, impoverishing the tribal nations it affected.

For the Snoqualmie, already landless, the Dawes Act's most significant effect was as a philosophical statement of the era's approach to Indigenous peoples: you will become individual American farmers, you will abandon your communal ways, you will assimilate, and if you do not, the government will use economic and legal tools to compel you. The era's policy was explicitly assimilationist — the goal was not to find an accommodation between two ways of life but to eliminate one of them.

### Boarding Schools: The War on Children

The most explicitly violent tool of the assimilationist era was the Indian boarding school system. Beginning in the 1870s with the founding of the Carlisle Indian Industrial School in Pennsylvania — whose founder, Richard Henry Pratt, stated his philosophy as "Kill the Indian in him, and save the man" — the United States government established a network of off-reservation boarding schools where Indigenous children were sent, often forcibly, to be remade into Americans.

In the Pacific Northwest, the Tulalip Indian School, on the Tulalip Reservation near Marysville, and Chemawa Indian School, near Salem, Oregon (founded 1880, still operating), were the principal institutions. Snoqualmie children were sent to these schools — removed from their families, sometimes without parental consent, and placed in institutions whose explicit purpose was cultural erasure.

At the boarding schools:
- Children were forbidden to speak their languages. Children who spoke Lushootseed were punished — slapped, had their mouths washed with soap, were subjected to other physical punishments. The message was explicit and brutal: your language is wrong. Your language is shameful. Your language will be beaten out of you.
- Traditional clothing and hairstyles were forbidden. Children arrived and were immediately stripped of their clothes and had their hair cut — a profoundly disorienting and sometimes spiritually alarming act, since for many Northwest Coast peoples, specific hairstyles had ceremonial significance.
- Christian religious practice was mandatory. Traditional spiritual practices were forbidden and punished.
- Children were separated from their siblings and from any older children who might maintain cultural connections. The school's social environment was designed to replace family and tribal identity with an American, Christian, English-speaking identity.
- Physical punishment was common. Sexual abuse was documented at many institutions.

The psychological damage of the boarding school system was profound and multigenerational. Children who returned home after years at boarding school were often estranged from their families — unable to speak the language, unfamiliar with the ceremonies, shaped by an institutional environment that had systematically attacked everything their families valued. Some brought shame about their Indigenous identity that persisted for generations. Some brought the violence they had experienced and perpetuated it within their families. The phenomenon of intergenerational trauma — the transmission of the psychological wounds of colonialism from parents to children across multiple generations — is not a metaphor. It is a documented psychological and social reality, and the boarding school system was one of its primary engines.

The Lushootseed language, which encodes ten thousand years of ecological and cosmological knowledge, was among the casualties. The elders who died in the epidemics had taken some of that knowledge with them. The boarding schools attacked what remained. By the mid-twentieth century, Lushootseed was spoken fluently by a dwindling number of elderly people, and the prospect of its extinction was real.

### The Sale of Snoqualmie Falls: 1889

In 1889, a group of Seattle investors acquired the land surrounding Snoqualmie Falls. In 1898, they completed a hydroelectric power plant — the first underground power plant in North America — at the base of the falls. The turbines that drove the plant were fed by a tunnel drilled through the rock below the falls, diverting water from the sacred site to generate electricity for the growing city of Seattle.

The Snoqualmie people could not stop this. They had no reservation. They had no recognized political standing. They had no land rights at the site that American law recognized. The sacred center of their world — the house of the mist-beings, the site of the First Salmon Ceremony, the portal between worlds that Moon had created at the beginning of time — became a source of electrical power for streetcars and electric lights.

The power plant is still there. Puget Sound Energy (the current owner) generates approximately 54 megawatts of hydroelectric power from the falls complex. The falls itself remains spectacular — the diversion takes only a portion of the river's flow, and during high water, the falls roars with its full historic volume. But the infrastructure of industrial power generation surrounds the site, and for most of its post-1898 history, the Snoqualmie had no formal role in the management of their most sacred place.

### The Suppression of Ceremony

While the boarding schools attacked language and identity in the classroom, other mechanisms attacked ceremony. In Canada, the Indian Act of 1885 explicitly banned the potlatch and associated ceremonies, making them criminal offenses punishable by imprisonment. The American government did not pass an equivalent explicit prohibition, but the combination of missionary pressure, boarding school ideology, and the authority of Indian agents over reservation peoples created a de facto suppression.

The winter spirit dances went underground. The potlatch was conducted with caution, sometimes disguised as other kinds of gatherings. The guardian spirit quest continued in families that maintained it, quietly, away from the eyes of authorities and missionaries. The bone game — slahal — was harder to suppress than the more elaborate ceremonies, and it continued openly.

The suppression of ceremony was not merely a cultural loss. It was the disruption of the social mechanisms that maintained community cohesion, redistributed wealth, maintained the guardian spirit relationships that connected individuals to the spiritual geography of their world, and reproduced the cultural knowledge of the elders in the lives of the young. Without the potlatch, wealth redistribution faltered. Without the spirit dances, the community's mechanism for renewing its spiritual relationships was damaged. Without the guardian spirit quest, young people grew up without the spiritual foundation the tradition prescribed.

These were real losses with real consequences — social, psychological, and spiritual — that accumulated over generations.

### Administrative Invisibility

The most insidious form of dispossession the Snoqualmie experienced was what might be called administrative invisibility. Without a reservation, they fell outside the normal channels of federal Indian administration. The Bureau of Indian Affairs managed peoples through their reservation units; peoples without reservations were often simply not managed — meaning they received no services, no advocacy, no legal protection, and no formal relationship with the government that had made them treaty promises.

This invisibility was not neutral. It was a form of ongoing dispossession. Every year that the Snoqualmie spent outside the administrative system was a year in which their treaty rights were not enforced, their children did not have access to the limited protections of federal Indian law, and the legal clock was not running in their favor. The United States government had made specific promises in 1855. The government's subsequent failure to honor those promises was not merely passive negligence. It was, in its accumulated effect, active dispossession — the practical elimination of a people's legal standing while leaving intact the theoretical rights that no mechanism existed to enforce.

## Part X — Landless for 144 Years: Survival Without Recognition

### The Paradox of Presence

For 144 years — from the signing of the Point Elliott Treaty in 1855 to the granting of federal recognition in 1999 — the Snoqualmie Indian Tribe occupied a paradoxical position in American legal and political life. They were present in the historical record: their leaders' marks were on the treaty, their oral tradition preserved the memory of the negotiations, their descendants continued to live in the Snoqualmie Valley. They were not present in the administrative machinery of the American state: no reservation, no Bureau of Indian Affairs unit, no formal government-to-government relationship with the United States.

This paradox was not unique to the Snoqualmie — dozens of Indigenous peoples throughout the United States found themselves in similar positions, their treaty rights acknowledged in principle and denied in practice — but the Snoqualmie case has a particular poignancy. They were not peoples who had been missed by the treaty process. They had been at the table. Their leaders had spoken and signed. The promises had been made explicitly. And then, through a combination of administrative neglect, settler pressure, bureaucratic indifference, and what can only be called bad faith, those promises were simply not kept.

### How the Community Survived

Without a reservation, without a formal administrative structure, without the resources that federal recognition would have eventually brought, the Snoqualmie community survived through the only mechanisms available to it: family, memory, and the persistence of cultural practice.

Extended family networks maintained community cohesion across the dispersed landscape. Snoqualmie families who were landless in the formal legal sense continued to occupy the places they had always occupied — the river valleys, the fishing sites, the gathering grounds — as squatters, as tenants, as neighbors of the settlers who now held legal title to what had once been Snoqualmie territory. Some Snoqualmie individuals assimilated into the broader settler community, married non-Indigenous people, worked as laborers in the logging and agricultural industries that were transforming their homeland. Others maintained more distinctly Indigenous identities, participating in ceremony, maintaining the oral tradition in whatever form was possible, and passing on cultural knowledge to their children.

The winter spirit dances — the most powerful and most resistant of the ceremonies — continued, practiced by families that maintained the tradition through all the years of suppression. The bone game continued. The oral tradition was maintained in fragments, in the memories of elders who had received it from their own elders, an imperfect chain of transmission but a chain nonetheless.

What could not be maintained as fully was the language. Lushootseed required a community of speakers to sustain itself — children needed to hear the language spoken around them, needed to use it in daily life and ceremony, for it to persist as a living tongue. The conditions that would have sustained daily Lushootseed use — the longhouse community, the ceremonial calendar, the generations of elders teaching in their own language — were systematically disrupted by boarding schools, by displacement, by the dispersal of the community across a landscape increasingly dominated by English-speaking settlers. By the mid-twentieth century, the number of fluent Lushootseed speakers was small and declining.

### The Termination Era and Its Aftermath

In the 1950s, the United States government pursued a policy called "termination" — the elimination of the special political status of Indian tribes, the dissolution of reservations, and the ending of the government-to-government relationship between the United States and tribal nations. Between 1953 and 1964, more than 100 tribes were terminated — their reservation lands sold, their federal benefits ended, their political existence as tribes legally dissolved.

The Snoqualmie, having never been federally recognized in the first place, could not technically be terminated. But the termination era's philosophy — that the goal of federal Indian policy was the elimination of distinct Indigenous political and cultural existence — shaped the general atmosphere in which Snoqualmie people lived. The message from the federal government was clear: become American. Stop being Indian. Assimilate. The special relationship between Indigenous peoples and the United States government, such as it was, was to be ended.

The Indian Reorganization Act of 1934 had offered some counter-movement to this assimilationist pressure — it ended allotment, restored some tribal governance authority, and theoretically opened a path for landless tribes to obtain trust lands. For the Snoqualmie, it came too late to help: the tribe had no formal organization that could take advantage of the act's provisions, and the subsequent termination era reversed much of its potential progress.

### The Movement for Recognition: 1970s Onward

The modern Indigenous rights movement of the 1960s and 1970s — the American Indian Movement, the occupation of Alcatraz Island, the Trail of Broken Treaties, the Wounded Knee standoff of 1973 — transformed the political landscape for Indigenous peoples across the United States. The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 formally ended the termination policy and established the principle that tribes had the right to govern themselves and manage their own affairs. The American Indian Religious Freedom Act of 1978 provided some protection for traditional ceremonial practices.

For the Snoqualmie, the political opening created by this era provided the opportunity to pursue what they had never been given: formal federal recognition.

The federal acknowledgment process — administered by the Bureau of Indian Affairs through its Branch of Acknowledgment and Research — is one of the most demanding and expensive bureaucratic processes that any political entity in the United States can undertake. A tribe seeking recognition must produce a massive documentary record demonstrating continuous community identity, continuous political authority, and continuous tribal membership criteria from historical times to the present. The standards are demanding, the process is slow, the BIA staff handling petitions is limited, and the tribes seeking recognition typically lack the resources to assemble the required documentation.

The Snoqualmie began the formal petition process in the 1970s. Their petition accumulated documentation of community existence across the post-treaty period: census records, family genealogies, church records, oral history interviews, newspaper accounts of community events, BIA correspondence, and the testimony of elders who remembered and could describe the continuation of community identity. They documented the spirit dances, the bone game, the family networks that had maintained Snoqualmie identity across generations without a land base.

### The 1997 Denial and the Appeal

In 1997, the Bureau of Indian Affairs issued its initial determination on the Snoqualmie petition: denial. The BIA's finding focused in part on gaps in the documentary record of community continuity — the very gaps that resulted from the administrative invisibility that had been imposed on the Snoqualmie by the government's failure to establish the reservation promised in 1855. The tribe was, in effect, being penalized for the consequences of the injustice it was seeking to have remedied.

The Snoqualmie appealed. The appeal generated additional documentation, additional testimony, and the sustained attention of lawyers, historians, and tribal advocates. The case moved through the BIA's appeal process.

### Recognition: 1999

On October 7, 1999, 144 years after the signing of the Point Elliott Treaty, the Bureau of Indian Affairs granted federal recognition to the Snoqualmie Indian Tribe.

The recognition was not a restoration of everything that had been lost. It was a beginning. The Tribe gained formal political existence — the right to govern itself, to establish enrollment criteria, to enter into contracts with the federal government, to begin the process of reestablishing a land base. It gained the ability to apply for federal programs available to recognized tribes. It gained, finally, a government-to-government relationship with the United States that the treaty had contemplated but that had never, for 144 years, been made real.

What recognition did not provide, immediately, was land. The Snoqualmie were recognized as a people but remained, technically, without a reservation — without trust land, without a protected homeland. The process of land reacquisition, which would require the Tribe to purchase land and apply for it to be placed in federal trust, was just beginning.

The 144-year gap between promise and fulfillment stands as one of the more concrete measures of how thoroughly and how long the American government failed to honor its treaty obligations to the Snoqualmie people. It is not an abstraction. It is 144 years of families living in their ancestors' homeland as legal nonentities, 144 years of children who deserved and were owed education, health care, and cultural support not receiving it, 144 years of ceremonies conducted in the shadow of administrative invisibility, and 144 years of the Snoqualmie Falls powering the city of Seattle while the people who held it sacred had no legal mechanism to protect it.

That they survived — that the community endured, that the ceremonies continued, that the identity persisted — is a testament to something that American policy was entirely unable to extinguish.

## Part XI — Cultural Survival and Revival

### What Survived

The question of what survived 144 years of dispossession, boarding school trauma, language suppression, and administrative invisibility is both painful and astonishing. The answer is: more than anyone had a right to expect, given the systematic nature of the destruction.

**Family memory** survived most completely. The knowledge of who the Snoqualmie were — which families, which lineages, which connections to which places and which ceremonies — was preserved in the memories of elders and transmitted imperfectly but persistently through families. The genealogical records that supported the federal recognition petition were not invented; they were recovered from living memory, from church records, from the notebooks of earlier generations who had written down what they knew.

**The winter spirit dances** survived. They were suppressed, practiced in reduced form, occasionally conducted in secret, but never extinguished. The families that maintained the tradition carried it through the worst years of pressure and continued to practice it as conditions allowed. The winter dance tradition that exists today is a continuation, however imperfect, of the tradition that existed before contact.

**The bone game** survived openly, because it is not easily categorized as a religious practice and could be conducted without provoking the kind of official hostility that the spirit dances sometimes attracted. Slahal continued at gatherings throughout the Pacific Northwest, maintaining a social and spiritual connection between peoples even when more explicitly ceremonial practices were under pressure.

**Oral tradition** survived in fragments. The comprehensive oral library of the Snoqualmie — the full body of stories that explained the landscape, transmitted cosmological knowledge, and maintained the community's understanding of its place in the world — was substantially damaged by the disruptions of the colonial period. Specific stories were lost when the elders who carried them died without passing them on. But a significant body of tradition survived, preserved partly in the notebooks of early ethnographers like James Teit, partly in the recordings of linguists who worked with Lushootseed elders in the mid-twentieth century, and partly in the living memories of elders within the community.

**The salmon** survived, though diminished. The great runs that had sustained Snoqualmie civilization for millennia were drastically reduced by commercial overfishing, habitat destruction (logging, agriculture, dam construction), and the warming of Pacific Ocean waters. But salmon still run in the Snoqualmie River, and the spiritual and practical relationship between the Snoqualmie people and the salmon that their ceremonies have always maintained has not been severed.

### Vi taqʷšəblu Hilbert: A Light for the Language

The most important single figure in the modern Lushootseed language revival was Vi taqʷšəblu Hilbert, an Upper Skagit elder (1918-2008) who dedicated the later decades of her life to the recording, transcription, and revitalization of Lushootseed. Born into a family that maintained Lushootseed as a living language, Hilbert grew up fluent and spent years working with linguists at the University of Washington — particularly Thom Hess — to produce the foundational scholarly resources for Lushootseed study: grammars, dictionaries, and collections of oral literature.

Hilbert was not merely a passive informant for academic linguists. She was an active agent of cultural revitalization — teaching the language, insisting on its importance, and working to ensure that the knowledge she held would be transmitted to future generations. She produced recordings of Lushootseed stories told by the last generation of fully fluent speakers, creating an archive that has become foundational for subsequent language revitalization efforts. She taught at the university level, trained younger people in the language, and maintained until her death the commitment that Lushootseed would not die with its last native speakers.

The Lushootseed Language Program, housed at the University of Washington and supported by several tribal nations including the Snoqualmie, continues the work that Hilbert and Hess began. Language learning materials, recordings, dictionaries, and educational programs for tribal youth have made Lushootseed accessible to new learners in ways that would not have been possible a generation ago.

### The Fight for Snoqualmie Falls

The most visible and prolonged cultural and legal battle of the modern Snoqualmie Tribe has been the fight for meaningful access to and co-management of Snoqualmie Falls — the sacred center of the Snoqualmie world that has been operated as a commercial power generation facility since 1898.

The falls is owned by Puget Sound Energy (PSE), which holds a Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) license for the hydroelectric facility. Under federal law, FERC licenses for hydroelectric facilities on significant waters must be periodically renewed, and the renewal process requires consultation with affected tribes about cultural and environmental impacts.

The Snoqualmie Tribe used the 2003 FERC license relicensing process for the Snoqualmie Falls facility as an opportunity to assert its cultural interests in the site. The tribe participated extensively in the relicensing proceedings, presenting evidence of the falls' sacred significance, documenting the historical use of the site for ceremony, and seeking formal recognition of its rights as a treaty signatory to a voice in the management of the site.

The outcome of the relicensing process was a negotiated agreement that provided the Tribe with some increased access to the falls for ceremonial purposes and some voice in the management decisions affecting the site. It was not a complete vindication of tribal interests — the power plant remains, Puget Sound Energy retains operational control, and the transformation of the sacred center into an industrial facility has not been undone. But it was a formal acknowledgment, embedded in a federal regulatory process, that the Snoqualmie Tribe has rights at the falls that must be considered.

The fight is not over. The relationship between the Tribe and Puget Sound Energy continues to be negotiated. Full co-management of the site — the kind of meaningful, ongoing role in stewardship of the falls that the Tribe's cultural relationship with the site warrants — remains a goal rather than an achievement.

### NAGPRA and the Return of the Dead

The Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act of 1990 (NAGPRA) established a process by which Indigenous human remains and sacred objects held in museums and federal collections must be returned to the tribal nations from which they were taken. For the Snoqualmie and many other Pacific Northwest tribes, this process has involved the repatriation of ancestral remains that were collected — often without consent, sometimes from disturbed graves, sometimes from deliberate excavation — by museums, universities, and private collectors in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.

The repatriation process under NAGPRA has been slow, contested, and incomplete. Institutions have sometimes resisted returning items, claiming they cannot establish cultural affiliation with sufficient certainty. The documentation requirements are burdensome. The emotional and spiritual cost to tribal members of encountering their ancestors' remains in museum storage is not captured in the legal process's procedural language.

Nevertheless, NAGPRA has resulted in the return of significant quantities of ancestral remains and cultural objects to the Snoqualmie and other tribes. The reburial of returned remains — conducted according to traditional protocol, restoring the dead to the earth of their homeland — is understood by tribal members as an act of healing, both for the ancestors and for the living community.

### Cultural Camps and Youth Programs

The contemporary Snoqualmie Tribe invests in cultural programs for tribal youth — language learning, traditional arts, canoe culture, and the transmission of oral tradition and ceremonial knowledge. Cultural camps bring young tribal members into contact with elders who carry knowledge that is not available in any book or database: the particular songs, the specific practices, the embodied knowledge of how to do the things that the culture values.

These programs are not nostalgia and they are not the creation of artificial cultural practice for the benefit of tourists. They are the ongoing transmission of a living culture — imperfect, adapted to contemporary circumstances, drawing on fragmentary records and living memory in combination — to the next generation. The young Snoqualmie people who participate in these programs are not being taught to be pre-contact Indians in a twentieth-century world. They are being taught to be Snoqualmie in their own time: carrying an ancient identity into a contemporary existence, finding ways to maintain the threads of connection that link them to their ancestors and their landscape.

## Part XII — The Living People: The Snoqualmie Tribe Today

### Federal Recognition and Its Aftermath

When the Bureau of Indian Affairs granted federal recognition to the Snoqualmie Indian Tribe on October 7, 1999, the immediate practical effect was modest. The Tribe gained a formal government-to-government relationship with the United States, the right to apply for federal programs, and the legal standing to pursue its interests in administrative and judicial proceedings. What it did not gain immediately was land, money, or the infrastructure of tribal governance.

Building that infrastructure has been the work of the subsequent decades. The Tribe established a formal government: a Tribal Council, tribal departments covering education, health, housing, cultural programs, and natural resources management, and the legal and administrative capacity to engage with the federal and state governments on a government-to-government basis. Enrollment was established and managed — the Tribe currently enrolls approximately 800-900 members, with the enrollment criteria reflecting the community's own determination of who belongs to the sdukʷalbixʷ.

The early years after recognition were financially constrained. Federal programs for recognized tribes provided some resources, but the Tribe's lack of a land base limited what was possible. The path to economic sustainability would require a more substantial intervention.

### The Snoqualmie Casino: Economic Sovereignty in Practice

The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act of 1988 had established a framework under which federally recognized tribes could operate casinos on their trust lands. The Snoqualmie Tribe, gaining recognition in 1999, began the process of acquiring trust land and developing a gaming facility.

The Snoqualmie Casino opened in 2008 on trust land near Snoqualmie, Washington — in the ancestral territory of the sdukʷalbixʷ, within the Snoqualmie Valley. It has been economically transformative. The revenues from the casino have funded tribal services, housing programs, health care, cultural programs, elder services, youth programs, and the ongoing work of land reacquisition. The Tribe has become one of the significant employers in the Snoqualmie Valley — the very valley from which its people were dispossessed — and its operations have given it an economic base that its pre-recognition existence entirely lacked.

The politics of Indian gaming are complicated, and the Snoqualmie Casino has not been without controversy. Questions about its location, its size, its traffic impacts, and its relationship to the broader Snoqualmie Valley community have generated local debate. The relationship between the casino's commercial character and the Tribe's cultural identity is something tribal members themselves navigate with care — the casino is a means, not an end, and the Tribe has been deliberate about using its revenues to advance the cultural and social goals that the money serves.

What is clear is that the casino has provided what 144 years of promises had not: an economic foundation that makes genuine tribal self-determination possible. The Snoqualmie Tribe today funds its own health clinic, its own housing programs, its own cultural center, its own language revitalization efforts, and its own land reacquisition program — none of which would be possible without the economic base that gaming revenues provide.

### Land Reacquisition

The Tribe's land reacquisition program represents the long-term work of rebuilding the land base that the failure to establish a reservation took away. Using casino revenues, the Tribe purchases parcels of land in its ancestral territory and applies to have them placed in federal trust — a process that converts the land from ordinary private property, subject to state and local jurisdiction, to land held by the United States in trust for the Tribe, subject primarily to tribal and federal jurisdiction.

This process is slow, expensive, and contested — local governments sometimes oppose trust applications because trust land is not subject to local property taxes. But the Tribe has made progress, assembling parcels that add up to an emerging land base in the valley where the sdukʷalbixʷ have lived since before memory.

The goal is not simply to acquire land. It is to reestablish the spatial foundation of Snoqualmie identity — the physical connection to the places that the oral tradition, the ceremonies, and the ecological knowledge encode. A people whose sacred sites are accessible, whose salmon streams are managed, whose camas meadows can be maintained — such a people can transmit their culture to their children in ways that a landless people, however spiritually resilient, cannot fully replicate.

### Snoqualmie Falls: The Ongoing Negotiation

The relationship between the Snoqualmie Tribe and Snoqualmie Falls remains unresolved. The falls is still owned by Puget Sound Energy. The power plant is still operating. The negotiated agreements from the 2003 FERC relicensing process provide some ceremonial access and some voice in management decisions, but full co-management — the kind of meaningful, ongoing stewardship role that the Tribe's cultural relationship with the falls warrants — has not been achieved.

The Tribe's position is straightforward: Snoqualmie Falls is the center of their world, the most sacred site in their cosmology, the place where Moon created the mist-house and where the Salmon People are welcomed back each year. The fact that a private utility company holds title to this site and operates an industrial facility on it is an ongoing injustice — one that has its roots in the dispossession of 1889, which was itself only possible because the Tribe had never been given the reservation that would have established legal standing at the site.

Each renewal of the FERC license is an opportunity to renegotiate the terms of the relationship. The Tribe has been persistent and sophisticated in its advocacy, using the tools of federal administrative law to assert its interests in a process designed for power company regulation, not for the vindication of Indigenous cultural rights. The work continues.

### The Cultural Center and Its Programs

The Snoqualmie Tribe's cultural center serves as a hub for the community's cultural revitalization work. Language classes in Lushootseed are offered for tribal members of all ages. Elders are supported and honored as repositories of cultural knowledge. Traditional arts — basket weaving, canoe carving, regalia making — are taught and practiced. The oral tradition is recorded, preserved, and transmitted through formal programs and through the informal channels of family and ceremony.

The cultural center is also a point of engagement with the broader public — a place where the Snoqualmie story is told on Snoqualmie terms, where visitors can encounter the living culture rather than a museum diorama. The framing matters: the Snoqualmie are not an extinct people whose artifacts are displayed behind glass. They are a living people whose culture is practiced, whose language is being learned, whose ceremonies are conducted, and whose relationship to their landscape continues.

### The Work That Remains

Federal recognition has not resolved the injustices of 144 years of dispossession. It has created the legal and political tools with which to pursue resolution. The work that remains is substantial:

**Language revitalization** is urgent. The number of fully fluent Lushootseed speakers is small. Creating a new generation of fluent speakers — people who dream in Lushootseed, who think in its categories, who can tell the old stories in the language in which they were created — requires sustained investment in immersion programs, in the creation of Lushootseed-medium educational environments, and in the support of the teachers who carry the language.

**Full co-management of Snoqualmie Falls** remains a goal. The mist-house of Moon, the site of the First Salmon Ceremony, the portal between worlds — its management should reflect the Tribe's spiritual relationship to it, not just the economic interests of a utility company.

**Healing intergenerational trauma** is the work of generations. The damage done by the boarding school system, by the suppression of ceremony, by the decades of administrative invisibility — this does not disappear with the granting of federal recognition. It lives in families, in the patterns of relationship and self-regard that colonialism distorted. The Tribe's social services, health programs, and cultural revitalization efforts are all, in one dimension, about healing this damage. It is slow work.

**Land return** is the long-term project. The valley belongs to the Snoqualmie. Not in the sense that the settlers who built their farms and towns there must be displaced — that particular bell cannot be unrung — but in the sense that the Tribe's ability to exercise stewardship over its ancestral territory, to manage its salmon streams, to maintain its sacred sites, and to practice its culture in the landscape where that culture was born, is a fundamental requirement of justice.

### A Living People

The final and most important thing to say about the Snoqualmie Indian Tribe is simple: they are here.

After ten thousand years in the valley, after the catastrophic epidemics of the late eighteenth century, after the broken promises of 1855, after the boarding schools and the ceremony suppression and the language assault and the administrative invisibility of 144 years — the sdukʷalbixʷ are here. They are enrolled members with tribal ID cards and tribal healthcare plans and a tribal casino that funds their children's education. They are elders who know the old songs. They are young people learning Lushootseed. They are families who camp at the base of the falls on ceremonial occasions and feel the mist on their faces and know, in their bodies and in their histories, what that mist is.

They are not a memory. They are not a museum exhibit. They are not a footnote to the history of the Pacific Northwest.

They are the people of the Moon. They are the sdukʷalbixʷ.

They are home.

## Appendix B — Village Sites Gazetteer

The following is a partial list of known or documented sdukʷalbixʷ village sites and associated locations in the Snoqualmie Valley region. Lushootseed names are given where known from published sources. Modern location equivalents are approximate. Archaeological evidence for many of these sites is incomplete, and some identifications are based on oral tradition and early ethnographic records rather than systematic excavation.

---

**1. Falls Village (near snoqʷəlʔ)**
*Location:* Immediately adjacent to Snoqualmie Falls, near modern Snoqualmie, Washington
*Description:* The principal ceremonial village of the sdukʷalbixʷ, situated at the most sacred site in Snoqualmie territory. The proximity to the falls made this the center of ceremonial life — the site of the First Salmon Ceremony, the location of major winter dances, and the spiritual heart of the community. Archaeological evidence of occupation has been documented in the general vicinity, though much of the immediate falls area has been altered by the construction of the hydroelectric facility and tourist infrastructure.

**2. si'aL / Tolt River Confluence Village**
*Location:* Near the confluence of the Tolt (Snoqualmie) River and the Snoqualmie River; modern Carnation, Washington
*Description:* One of the major permanent village sites of the Snoqualmie people, associated in the nineteenth century with Chief Patkanim and other important leaders. The Tolt River confluence was an excellent fishing location and a natural gathering point. The village at si'aL was large enough to accommodate significant ceremonial gatherings and was an important node in the Snoqualmie's social network.

**3. Snoqualmie Prairie Village**
*Location:* The open prairies south of modern North Bend, in the upper Snoqualmie Valley
*Description:* Village communities associated with the camas meadows and hunting grounds of the prairie zone. The Snoqualmie Prairie was one of the largest natural prairie areas in the Cascade foothills, maintained in part through the burning practices of Indigenous inhabitants over centuries. Important for camas harvesting and for deer hunting.

**4. North Fork Village(s)**
*Location:* Along the North Fork of the Snoqualmie River, in the general vicinity of modern Duvall and Fall City
*Description:* The North Fork drainage supported several village communities, taking advantage of the salmon runs in the North Fork and its tributaries. These communities were somewhat more accessible to the peoples of the northern Puget Sound lowlands and facilitated connections with the Snohomish and related peoples.

**5. Middle Fork Village(s)**
*Location:* Along the Middle Fork of the Snoqualmie River
*Description:* The Middle Fork's wilder, narrower valley supported smaller communities, more oriented toward hunting and resource gathering in the deep forest than toward the large-scale fishing operations of the main river. Access to the high Cascades via the Middle Fork made these communities important for the hunting and gathering activities of the summer season.

**6. South Fork / Snoqualmie Pass Approach**
*Location:* Along the South Fork of the Snoqualmie River and the approaches to Snoqualmie Pass
*Description:* The South Fork drainage led directly to Snoqualmie Pass — the lowest and most accessible pass through the Cascade Range in this area of Washington — and was therefore the starting point of the major trade route connecting the Puget Sound lowlands with the peoples of the Columbia Plateau. Communities along the South Fork were important as waypoints for trade and as the people most directly involved in the trans-Cascade exchange networks.

**7. Cedar River / Confluence Area**
*Location:* Near the confluence of the Cedar River with other waterways; transitional zone between Snoqualmie and Duwamish territories
*Description:* The Cedar River drainage was a zone of interaction between the sdukʷalbixʷ and the Duwamish people, whose territory lay to the west along the lower river system. Marriage alliances between the two peoples made this transitional zone a space of regular social exchange rather than sharp territorial division.

**8. Lake Sammamish Area**
*Location:* Lake Sammamish (Lhəqtəbulqs or similar), modern Issaquah / Redmond, Washington
*Description:* The Lake Sammamish basin, at the western edge of sdukʷalbixʷ territory, was an important fishing and gathering area. The Marymoor site at the southern end of the lake has produced archaeological evidence of occupation spanning multiple millennia, making it one of the best-documented prehistoric sites in the greater Snoqualmie watershed. The lake supported populations of fish and waterfowl that supplemented the salmon-focused economy of the main river.

**9. Raging River Area**
*Location:* Raging River (tributary of the South Fork Snoqualmie), near modern Fall City
*Description:* The Raging River and its confluence zone was a secondary fishing and gathering location, providing additional salmon harvesting opportunities in a tributary system less heavily used than the main river.

**10. Skykomish / Snoqualmie Convergence**
*Location:* Near the convergence of the Snoqualmie and Skykomish rivers to form the Snohomish River; modern Monroe, Washington
*Description:* This convergence zone was the boundary area between the primary sdukʷalbixʷ territory and the territories of the Skykomish and Snohomish peoples. The lower Snoqualmie River was within the extended Snoqualmie cultural sphere, and the Monroe area communities maintained close relationships with both the upriver Snoqualmie communities and the coastal peoples of the lower Snohomish River.

---

*Note: The Snoqualmie Valley has been substantially transformed by Euro-American settlement, logging, agriculture, and suburban development since the mid-nineteenth century. Many village sites have been built over, flooded by changed water management, or disturbed by construction. The locations given above are approximate, and the actual extent and character of historical occupation was almost certainly considerably denser and more complex than the fragmentary record suggests.*

## Appendix D — Neighboring Peoples

The Snoqualmie people did not exist in isolation. They were embedded in a dense network of relationships with neighboring peoples — relationships of kinship through marriage, trade, ceremony, occasional conflict, and shared cosmological frameworks. The following brief entries describe the principal neighboring peoples and their relationships with the sdukʷalbixʷ.

---

### The Duwamish (dxʷdəwʔabš)

The Duwamish people occupied the drainage of the Duwamish River — which is formed by the Green River and the Black River — and the shores of Elliott Bay, where Seattle now stands. Their territory was immediately west of the lower Snoqualmie country, separated by the forested ridges that divide the river drainages. The Duwamish and the Snoqualmie shared Lushootseed speech (Southern dialect) and were closely connected through marriage alliances. Their most famous historical figure, Chief Si'ahl (Chief Seattle), was of mixed Duwamish and Suquamish heritage. Today the Duwamish Tribe remains without federal recognition — a situation with uncomfortable parallels to the Snoqualmie's own 144-year wait — though they maintain their political organization and are actively seeking recognition. The Duwamish connection to the Snoqualmie was one of the most important in the broader sdukʷalbixʷ social network.

---

### The Skykomish (sduhubš or skyqʷamish)

The Skykomish people occupied the drainage of the Skykomish River, which flows roughly parallel to the North Fork of the Snoqualmie and joins it near Monroe to form the Snohomish River. As immediate northern neighbors of the Snoqualmie, the Skykomish maintained close relationships through intermarriage, shared fishing sites, and joint participation in ceremonies. They spoke Northern Lushootseed, slightly different in phonology and vocabulary from the Southern Lushootseed of the Snoqualmie. The Skykomish did not maintain a separate tribal political organization through the reservation era; members merged into the Snohomish and Snoqualmie communities. The Stevens Pass route through the Cascades, in the Skykomish watershed, was a major trade artery connecting the Puget Sound peoples with the peoples of eastern Washington.

---

### The Sauk-Suiattle (sauk-suiattle)

The Sauk-Suiattle people occupy the Sauk River and Suiattle River drainages of the western Cascades, north of the Skykomish country. They share the Northern Lushootseed language and are related culturally and linguistically to both the Snoqualmie and the Skykomish. Their territory extends into the high country of what is now the North Cascades, and they were important partners in the trade networks that crossed the mountains via the more northern passes. The Sauk-Suiattle are today a federally recognized tribe with a small reservation on the Sauk River near Darrington, Washington. Their relationship with the Snoqualmie was that of neighbors and trading partners who shared cultural frameworks and occasionally intermarried.

---

### The Upper Skagit (sqʷuqʷe'iq')

The Upper Skagit people occupy the Skagit River valley above its confluence with the Sauk River, extending into the high Cascades. They are Northern Lushootseed speakers closely related to the Sauk-Suiattle. Vi taqʷšəblu Hilbert, the most important Lushootseed scholar of the modern era, was Upper Skagit. The Upper Skagit Tribe is today federally recognized with a small reservation near Concrete, Washington. Their relationship with the Snoqualmie was mediated through the Sauk-Suiattle and Skykomish connections — more distant geographically than the immediate neighbors, but part of the broader Lushootseed cultural world. The Upper Skagit maintained important trade connections through the North Cascades passes that linked the Puget Sound world with the Interior Salish peoples of British Columbia.

---

### The Muckleshoot (bəqəlšuhl)

The Muckleshoot are the immediate southern neighbors of the Snoqualmie, occupying the White River and Green River drainages south and east of Auburn, Washington. They are Southern Lushootseed speakers, closely related culturally and linguistically to the Snoqualmie. The Muckleshoot Reservation was established following the Medicine Creek and Point Elliott treaties, consolidating several peoples who had signed the two treaties. Today the Muckleshoot Indian Tribe is one of the more economically powerful tribal nations in Washington State, with a large casino and extensive tribal programs. Their relationship with the Snoqualmie has been consistently close — shared language, shared cultural practices, intermarriage, and the shared experience of the Puget Sound treaty system. The Muckleshoot's successful assertion of their fishing rights in and following the Boldt Decision has been an important model for other Puget Sound tribes.

---

### The Yakama (Yakama Nation)

The Yakama Nation occupies the Columbia Plateau east of the Cascades, in what is now south-central Washington State. Though much further from the Snoqualmie than the immediate Puget Sound neighbors, the Yakama were important trade partners through the Snoqualmie Pass route. The Columbia Plateau peoples produced buffalo robes (after the introduction of horses made plains hunting accessible), dried roots (particularly camas from the eastern prairies), obsidian tools, and other goods not available on the wet western side of the mountains. The Puget Sound peoples brought dried salmon, whale oil, dentalium shells, and other coastal products. This trans-Cascade trade had been operating for thousands of years before European contact. The Yakama people speak a Sahaptin language, entirely distinct from Lushootseed, and their cultural framework differs substantially from Coast Salish patterns — they were more oriented toward the bison-hunting economy of the Plateau and the horse culture that transformed that economy after the seventeenth century. But the trade relationship with the Snoqualmie and other Puget Sound peoples was longstanding and important, maintained through the annual passage across Snoqualmie Pass.

## Appendix E — A Note on Sources

### The Challenge of Writing About Indigenous Peoples

This book was written by an artificial intelligence drawing on published scholarly sources. That limitation must be stated plainly, not apologized for, but clearly acknowledged.

The history of Indigenous peoples has been written, for most of the history of that writing, by outsiders — by anthropologists, by government agents, by missionaries, by linguists, by historians trained in European academic traditions. These writings contain invaluable information. They also contain systematic biases, ethnocentric assumptions, and — in some cases — outright errors that resulted from observers who did not fully understand what they were seeing. The classic ethnographic literature on Pacific Northwest Coast peoples, for all its value, was written in the context of a scholarly tradition that often treated Indigenous peoples as objects of study rather than subjects with their own intellectual agency, and that frequently adopted an implicit "salvage" framework — the assumption that the cultures being recorded were dying and needed to be captured for posterity before they disappeared.

The cultures did not disappear. The anthropologists' predictions of extinction were wrong. And their texts, written without the full participation of the communities they described, require careful critical reading.

### The Scholarly Literature

Several bodies of scholarly work have been foundational for this book:

**Linguistic scholarship** — The work of Vi taqʷšəblu Hilbert and Thom Hess in documenting and codifying Lushootseed is irreplaceable. The Lushootseed Dictionary (Dawn Bates, Thom Hess, and Vi Hilbert, University of Washington Press, 1994) and the accompanying Lushootseed Grammar are the primary reference works for the language. Hilbert's collections of oral literature — particularly Haboo: Native American Stories from Puget Sound (University of Washington Press, 1980) — provide access to the oral tradition in a form mediated through a scholar who was also a community member, which gives those texts a different authority than purely external ethnographies.

**Ethnographic literature** — The early ethnographic work of Marian Smith (The Puyallup-Nisqually, Columbia University Press, 1940), Albert Reagan, and others, while requiring critical reading for the reasons described above, contains documentation of material culture, social organization, and ceremonial life that was recorded closer to the period of intact tradition and is therefore irreplaceable. The Handbook of North American Indians, Volume 7: Northwest Coast (edited by Wayne Suttles, Smithsonian Institution, 1990) is the most comprehensive scholarly reference work on the Coast Salish peoples and includes specific entries on the Snoqualmie.

**Historical and legal scholarship** — Alexandra Harmon's Indians in the Making: Ethnic Relations and Indian Identities around Puget Sound (University of California Press, 1998) is an important treatment of the complex, shifting, and contested nature of Indian identity in the Puget Sound region from the treaty period through the twentieth century. The legal history of the treaty and its aftermath is documented in the extensive literature on the Boldt Decision and tribal fishing rights; a useful starting point is American Indians, Time, and the Law by Charles Wilkinson (Yale University Press, 1987).

**Oral tradition and tribal sources** — The Snoqualmie Indian Tribe maintains its own cultural documentation and educational materials, which should be consulted as the authoritative voice on Snoqualmie culture and history from a tribal perspective. The Tribe's website (www.snoqualmietribe.us) and its cultural center programs represent the most current expression of how the Tribe understands and presents its own history.

### The Problem of Sacred Knowledge

Every attempt to write a comprehensive account of an Indigenous people's culture runs into a fundamental ethical problem: some of what would be most interesting and significant to write is knowledge that the community holds as private, sacred, or restricted — not because it is secret for arbitrary reasons, but because its transmission through unrestricted public texts would damage or destroy its cultural function.

The guardian spirit quest, the winter spirit dances, healing practices, specific ceremonial songs and procedures — these are not simply religious practices that could be described neutrally. Their power, in the tradition that holds them, depends in part on the conditions of their transmission: the relationship between teacher and student, the context of ceremony and community, the requirement of personal commitment and spiritual preparation. A book that attempted to describe them fully — that treated sacred ceremony as simply another category of ethnographic data — would not be neutral. It would be a form of desecration.

This book has attempted to describe what can be described in a public text — the existence and general character of ceremonies, the cultural context that gives them meaning, the reasons why they matter — while respecting the boundaries of what belongs in restricted transmission. Where those boundaries lie is not always clear, and this book may err in one direction or another. Tribal members who feel those boundaries have been crossed are invited to say so.
