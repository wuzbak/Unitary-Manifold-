## Dark matter as the B_μ irreversibility field

Dark matter is the greatest unsolved problem in observational cosmology. Roughly
27% of the universe's energy budget is in a form that does not emit, absorb, or
scatter light, but does exert gravity. We have measured its gravitational effects
with extraordinary precision — galaxy rotation curves, gravitational lensing,
the CMB acoustic peaks, large-scale structure formation. We have not detected
a dark matter particle in any laboratory experiment.

The Unitary Manifold makes a specific, falsifiable geometric claim:

> Dark matter is not an invisible particle. It is the geometric pressure of the
> Irreversibility Field B_μ.

In the 5D geometry, B_μ is the off-diagonal component of the metric that encodes
irreversibility — the "arrow of time" field. It is not a particle; it is a
geometric field that permeates the manifold. In four dimensions, after integrating
out the fifth, B_μ appears as an effective energy-momentum source that behaves
like dark matter from a gravitational standpoint.

The key calculation: for a galactic-scale profile where B_μ falls as 1/r (the
behaviour expected from the geometry), the effective dark matter density is:

    ρ_dark(r) = ρ₀ r_s² / r²

This is the isothermal sphere profile — the same profile that was historically
fitted to galaxy rotation curves as a phenomenological model. In the framework,
it is not fitted; it is derived from the B_μ ∝ 1/r falloff.

The dark mass enclosed within radius r:

    M_dark(<r) = 4π ρ₀ r_s² × r

This scales linearly with r, which gives:

    v_flat² = G M_dark(<r) / r = 4π G ρ₀ r_s² = constant

A constant asymptotic rotation velocity — which is exactly what is observed.
Galaxy rotation curves go flat at large radii, and they have been flat since
Vera Rubin's measurements in the 1970s. The framework derives the flat curve
from first principles: it is the gravitational signature of a B_μ ∝ 1/r field.

The falsification conditions are specific:

1. If a direct detection experiment (XENONnT, LUX-ZEPLIN, PandaX) detects a
   dark matter particle with the properties expected from WIMPs or axions, the
   geometric dark matter claim is challenged. The existence of a dark matter
   particle is not excluded by the framework — but it would require explaining
   why the B_μ field *and* a new particle are both contributing to galaxy dynamics.

2. If galaxy rotation curves deviate systematically from the isothermal B_μ
   profile in ways inconsistent with the geometric prediction, the claim is
   weakened. The NFW profile (which peaks more steeply at the centre) is
   actually preferred in some N-body simulations; whether the core-cusp problem
   (tension between simulated and observed dark matter core densities) is
   consistent with the B_μ model is an open question.

## Geometric dark matter vs. MOND

Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND) is the most prominent alternative to the
particle dark matter hypothesis. It modifies gravity at low accelerations rather
than postulating new matter.

The framework's approach is different from MOND but related in spirit: both reject
the dark matter particle and attribute the rotation curve anomaly to a geometric
or modified-gravity effect. The difference: MOND modifies the 4D gravitational
law phenomenologically; the framework derives an extra gravitational contribution
from a 5D geometric field.

MOND has two well-known successes: it predicts the Tully-Fisher relation
(luminosity ∝ v_flat⁴) and the rotation curves of individual galaxies from their
baryonic mass distribution alone, without free parameters per galaxy. The B_μ
model generates the same isothermal profile but requires setting ρ₀ and r_s
for each galaxy — two parameters that play the role of MOND's single acceleration
constant a₀.

This is an honest asymmetry. The geometric dark matter model has more freedom
than MOND per galaxy. Whether this extra freedom is justified by the deeper
geometric derivation is an open question that requires a systematic comparison
against rotation curve data.

## Stars as FTUM fixed points

The stellar module's central claim is compact:

> A star is a FTUM fixed point. Hydrostatic equilibrium is the condition
> U Ψ* = Ψ* applied to the coupled gravity-pressure system.

Hydrostatic equilibrium requires:

    dP/dr = −ρ(r) g(r)

The defect |dP/dr + ρg| measures how far the star is from its FTUM fixed point.
A main-sequence star on the hydrogen-burning sequence has defect ≈ 0; a star
in the later stages of evolution — as it burns helium or undergoes shell flashes —
has an elevated defect and is approaching a different fixed point (red giant,
white dwarf, neutron star, black hole, depending on the initial mass).

The framework uses this language to unify stellar evolution: the main sequence is
a line of FTUM fixed points parameterised by mass. Stars move off this line when
the nuclear fuel changes (from hydrogen to helium to carbon) and find new fixed
points. The final fixed point is determined by whether the remnant mass is
below the Chandrasekhar limit (white dwarf), the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff
limit (neutron star), or above (black hole).

**Star formation** in the framework is B_μ-driven Jeans instability. The Jeans
mass — the minimum mass for gravitational collapse:

    M_J = (5 k_B T / G μ m_H)^(3/2) × (3 / 4π ρ)^(1/2)

— is the threshold below which thermal φ-capacity pressure exceeds B_μ
gravitational compression. Below M_J, the cloud disperses; above M_J, the FTUM
operator drives it toward the stellar fixed point. The sun's mass of ~10³⁰ kg
is comfortably above the Jeans mass for molecular cloud conditions (~1–10 M_☉).

## Earth science: geology and the planetary fixed point

The Earth module applies the same fixed-point language to the planet. A tectonically
active planet is not in equilibrium — it is cycling continuously between states
as the mantle convects and the crust recycles. But it has a long-term attractor:
the mean surface temperature, sea level, and atmospheric composition all exhibit
stability on geological timescales, punctuated by excursions (ice ages, hothouse
periods) followed by return to the reference attractor.

The geological carbon cycle — the exchange of CO₂ among rocks, the ocean,
and the atmosphere over million-year timescales — is the primary restoring force:

    dCO₂/dt = F_volcanic − F_weathering(CO₂, T)

Volcanic outgassing adds CO₂; chemical weathering of silicate rocks (which
accelerates with temperature and CO₂) removes it. The system is a negative
feedback loop — a stabilising attractor. This is the Urey reaction, which has
maintained Earth's climate in the habitable zone for 4 billion years despite the
sun brightening by ~30%.

In the framework's language, the geological carbon cycle is a φ-attractor with
a very deep basin (the weathering feedback is strong), so large B_μ perturbations
(large igneous provinces, bolide impacts) shift the system far from the attractor
but the restoring force eventually returns it. The current anthropogenic CO₂
forcing is — as Post 21 discussed — introducing a B_μ perturbation that is
operating on a timescale orders of magnitude faster than the weathering feedback
can respond.

## The origin of biological complexity

The biology module (`src/biology/`) addresses one of the deepest questions in
all of science: why did life arise, and why did it become complex?

The framework's answer is structural, not detailed:

**Life arose** because chemistry at the warm-dilute-solution fixed point of
early Earth — the conditions after the ocean formed and before the Late Heavy
Bombardment ended — crossed the threshold where autocatalytic φ-networks could
self-sustain. The first replicating molecules were not an improbable accident;
they were the inevitable fixed-point attractor of a sufficiently complex organic
chemistry system driven far from equilibrium by solar energy.

**Life became complex** because complexity increases φ-capacity: a more complex
organism can occupy a larger number of distinct stable configurations (more
cell types, more behaviours, more responses to environmental variation). The
FTUM operator applied to a population of organisms under natural selection
drives toward higher-φ configurations — which are, in general, more complex
organisms, because complexity provides more degrees of freedom for adaptation.

This is the connection between evolution and the physics core: natural selection
is the operation of the FTUM irreversibility operator on the genotype-phenotype
landscape. The arrow of biological complexity points in the same direction as the
FTUM operator — toward higher φ, higher information capacity, more stable attractors.

The critical caveat: this is a qualitative argument, not a derivation. The framework
does not compute the probability of abiogenesis or the rate of complexity increase
from first principles. It provides the formal language in which these processes
are consistent with the physics of irreversibility.

## The morphogenesis module: Turing in five dimensions

The biology module includes a morphogenesis sub-module: the process by which a
single fertilised egg produces the specific geometry of an organism — fingers,
eyes, the heart on the left, the spine down the middle.

Alan Turing showed in 1952 that reaction-diffusion equations — two chemical species,
one activating and one inhibiting, diffusing at different rates — can spontaneously
produce spatial patterns: stripes, spots, spirals. These are the φ-field patterns
of the developing embryo.

In the framework's language, morphogenesis is pattern selection in the φ-field.
The specific patterns selected — which stripes form where, which cells become
which tissue type — are determined by the φ-landscape defined by the maternal
gradient (the initial φ-field configuration set by the egg). The developing embryo
is a sequence of bifurcations, each splitting a uniform φ-attractor into two
regions that differentiate further.

The framework's specific prediction at this level: topological constraints limit
which morphogenetic patterns are stable. Patterns with incompatible topological
winding numbers cannot coexist. This is consistent with the observation that
body plans are highly constrained — the 35 or so animal phyla that exist represent
a tiny fraction of the geometrically possible body plans.

Whether this topological constraint is derived from the framework's 5D geometry
or is simply a mathematical property of reaction-diffusion equations is an open
question. The formal structure is the same; the causal direction has not been
established.

## What the test suites confirm — and do not

The astronomy, earth, and biology modules cover:

- Stellar structure (Jeans mass, hydrostatic equilibrium defect, main sequence
  mass-luminosity relation)
- Dark matter rotation curves (isothermal profile, flat curve velocity, B_μ
  field dark mass enclosed)
- Geological carbon cycle (CO₂ equilibrium, weathering feedback, timescale)
- Biological information content (genome φ-capacity, morphogenetic pattern stability)

What the tests do not confirm:

- That the B_μ isothermal dark matter profile is the correct model for all galaxy
  types (the core-cusp problem and the diversity of dwarf galaxy rotation curves
  remain unresolved)
- That star formation follows the Jeans criterion in the specific form implemented
  (real molecular cloud collapse involves magnetic fields, radiation pressure, and
  turbulence not captured in the module)
- That the origin of life followed the FTUM autocatalytic fixed-point mechanism
  (this is a qualitative hypothesis, not a quantitative derivation)
- That animal body plan diversity is determined by the winding number topology
  of the framework rather than by developmental constraints with a different origin
