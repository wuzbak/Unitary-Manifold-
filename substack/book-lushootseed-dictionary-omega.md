## A Note on This Dictionary

This dictionary is dedicated to the ongoing survival and revitalization of **dxʷləšucid** — Lushootseed — the language of the sdukʷalbixʷ (Snoqualmie people) and their neighbors throughout the Puget Sound lowlands. It is offered as a companion to *People of the Moon: The sdukʷalbixʷ and the World of the Snoqualmie* and as a resource for learners, researchers, and all who believe that the survival of a language is the survival of a way of seeing the world.

Lushootseed is a **living language**. Although the number of fully fluent speakers has been drastically reduced by a century of boarding-school suppression, forced assimilation, and administrative neglect, the language has never died. Elders have kept it alive in ceremony, in song, in prayer, in family memory. Language programs at the Snoqualmie Indian Tribe, the Tulalip Tribes, the Puyallup Nation, the Muckleshoot Indian Tribe, and the Suquamish Tribe are actively restoring it to daily life. This dictionary is offered in support of that work.

**Important caveats:**

1. **This dictionary is not a replacement for living teachers.** Lushootseed must be learned from speakers, from audio recordings, and from the immersion programs run by the tribal nations themselves. Words on a page cannot carry the music of the language.

2. **Spellings vary.** Lushootseed was primarily an oral language. Multiple orthographic systems have been used over the decades. This dictionary generally follows the standard Americanist phonetic notation codified by Vi Hilbert, Thom Hess, and their colleagues at the University of Washington — the same system used in the *Lushootseed Dictionary* (Bates, Hess, and Hilbert, University of Washington Press, 1994), which is the authoritative scholarly reference.

3. **Dialect matters.** Lushootseed has two main dialects: **Northern** (spoken by peoples around the northern Puget Sound — Snohomish, Stillaguamish, Sauk-Suiattle, Skagit) and **Southern** (spoken by peoples around the southern Puget Sound and the Cascade foothills — Snoqualmie, Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Puyallup, Nisqually, Suquamish). The Snoqualmie are **Southern Lushootseed** speakers. Where dialect differences are known, they are noted.

4. **Sacred knowledge is not here.** This dictionary does not attempt to reproduce ceremonial songs, restricted ritual language, or knowledge held privately by Snoqualmie families and ceremonial practitioners. That knowledge belongs to those communities and is transmitted through living relationship, not written texts.

5. **Some words are reconstructed or approximate.** Where a word is known from published scholarly sources, it is given with confidence. Where an entry is approximated from related Lushootseed dialects or from broader Salishan cognates, that is noted.

**Primary source:** *Lushootseed Dictionary*, Dawn Bates, Thom Hess, and Vi Hilbert (University of Washington Press, 1994). Supplemented by *Lushootseed Reader* (Thom Hess), *Haboo: Native American Stories from Puget Sound* (Vi Hilbert), and language materials from tribal language programs.

**For learners:** The Snoqualmie Indian Tribe's Cultural Department, the Tulalip Lushootseed Language Program (tulaliplushootseed.com), and the University of Washington's Lushootseed archives are the best starting points for active language study.

- Phonemes that have no English equivalent are described in the Pronunciation Guide below

## Part I — Pronunciation Guide: The Sounds of dxʷləšucid

Lushootseed is not phonologically simple for English speakers. It uses sounds that do not exist in English and requires careful attention to distinctions (such as glottal stops) that English treats as meaningless. This guide covers every major phoneme of the language.

### Consonants: The Unfamiliar

| Symbol | Description | Closest English Approximation |
|--------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| **xʷ** | Voiceless labiovelar fricative | Like a raspy "wh" pushed far back in the throat, with rounded lips. Imagine fogging glasses but through rounded lips. |
| **x̌** | Voiceless uvular fricative | Like the "ch" in Scottish *loch* or German *Bach*, but even further back — produced at the uvula. No English equivalent. |
| **x̌ʷ** | Voiceless labialized uvular fricative | Like **x̌** but with simultaneous lip rounding |
| **q** | Voiceless uvular stop | Like "k" but produced at the uvula (the dangling bit at the back of your throat), not at the velum. Sounds deeper and further back. |
| **qʷ** | Labialized uvular stop | Like **q** with lip rounding |
| **č** | Voiceless palato-alveolar affricate | "ch" in *church* |
| **š** | Voiceless palato-alveolar fricative | "sh" in *shore* |
| **ƛ̕** | Lateral ejective affricate | No English equivalent. The tongue touches the alveolar ridge (where you make "l") and the air is released with a glottal pop sideways. Roughly "tl!" said sharply. |
| **ɬ** | Voiceless lateral fricative | Like Welsh "ll" in *Llangollen*. Air passes over the sides of the tongue without voicing. A "breathed l." |
| **ʔ** | Glottal stop | The catch in the throat between syllables of "uh-oh." Appears at word beginnings, middles, and ends. Changes meaning. |
| **c** | Voiceless alveolar affricate | "ts" in *cats* |
| **dz** | Voiced alveolar affricate | "ds" in *lads* (or "z" in some contexts) |
| **dʒ** | Voiced palato-alveolar affricate | "j" in *judge* |
| **ł** | Voiceless lateral fricative | Like **ɬ** — air passes along sides of tongue; like Welsh "ll" |

### Ejective Consonants

Ejective consonants are produced with a simultaneous glottal closure and a burst of laryngeal air. They sound slightly more forceful or "popped" than plain consonants. In Lushootseed orthography, ejectives are marked with an apostrophe after the consonant symbol.

| Symbol | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **p̓** | Ejective "p" — like "p!" said with a click in the throat |
| **t̓** | Ejective "t" |
| **k̓** | Ejective "k" |
| **q̓** | Ejective uvular stop — deeply explosive |
| **č̓** | Ejective "ch" |
| **c̓** | Ejective "ts" |
| **ƛ̕** | Ejective lateral affricate (always ejective) |
| **s̓** | Ejective "s" (rare) |

### Note on the Name *dxʷləšucid*

The word *dxʷləšucid* — the language's own name for itself — is often translated as "the mouth of the dxʷləšú people" or "the language of the people of the Inside [Puget Sound]." Breaking it down:

- **dxʷ-** — a directional prefix meaning "toward the inside" or associated with the inside waters (Puget Sound)
- **-lə-** — connective
- **šucid** — "mouth" or "language, speech"

So the language calls itself, roughly, *the mouth-speech of the People of the Inside Waters.* It is a name that locates language in the body, in geography, and in identity simultaneously.

### Section 28: Grammar Notes — How Lushootseed Works

Lushootseed grammar differs fundamentally from English. Understanding the basic patterns helps learners make sense of the vocabulary above.

#### Word Order
Lushootseed uses **Verb-Subject-Object (VSO)** word order as its default, unlike English (Subject-Verb-Object). The verb comes first.

> **ɬuʔ čəd** = "I eat" (lit. "eat I")
> **ɬubəč čəd tə sxʷiləq** = "I see Mount Si" (lit. "see I the mountain-being")

#### Pronouns
Pronouns are often expressed as enclitics (attached to the verb or particle) rather than free-standing words.

| Person | Pronoun | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| I (1st singular) | čəd | čəd = I |
| you (2nd singular) | čəxʷ | čəxʷ = you |
| he/she/it (3rd) | (marked on verb with ʔu-) | ʔuʔəł = he/she comes |
| we (1st plural) | čəl | čəl = we |
| you all (2nd plural) | čəlap | čəlap = you all |
| they (3rd plural) | tiʔəɬ | tiʔəɬ = they |

#### The Nominalizer **s-**
Adding **s-** to a verb root turns it into a noun. This is one of the most productive processes in Lushootseed:

> **ɬuʔ** (to eat) → **sɬuʔ** (food; the act of eating)
> **č̓əłub** (to sing) → **sqʷəy** (song; power-song)
> **ʔuʔəł** (to come) → **sʔuʔəł** (the coming; arrival)

#### Articles
Lushootseed uses articles (like English "the" and "a") but they function differently:

| Article | Usage |
|---------|-------|
| **tə** | Singular definite article ("the") |
| **tiʔiɬ** | Those (distal plural demonstrative) |
| **ʔal** | At; in; to (locative) |

#### Aspect (Not Tense)
Lushootseed primarily marks **aspect** (whether an action is ongoing, completed, or projected) rather than **tense** (when it happened). Context determines time reference.

| Suffix | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **-təb** | Progressive: is/are doing |
| **-ib** | Future: will do |
| **-ad** | Object marker (us/our) |

## Part V — A Note on Language Revitalization

Lushootseed — dxʷləšucid — is classified by linguists as a **seriously endangered language**. At the time of this writing, the number of fully fluent first-language speakers is small. The threat to the language is real.

But the language is not dead. And the efforts to restore it to daily life are extensive, sustained, and growing:

**The Tulalip Tribes** operate one of the most active Lushootseed revitalization programs, including immersion classes, youth programs, online resources (tulaliplushootseed.com), and a comprehensive online dictionary.

**The Snoqualmie Indian Tribe** runs language classes and cultural programs through its Cultural Department, with specific attention to the Southern Lushootseed dialect of the sdukʷalbixʷ.

**The Puyallup Nation** and **Muckleshoot Indian Tribe** maintain Southern Lushootseed programs serving the related dialects of the southern Sound.

**The University of Washington** holds archival audio recordings of Lushootseed elders — including Vi Hilbert and other early scholars — in its Special Collections, providing irreplaceable documentation of pronunciation, intonation, and oral style.

**Endangered Language Fund and First Voices** platforms support digital preservation and online access to language materials.

Every word spoken in Lushootseed is an act of survival. Every learner is a keeper of a ten-thousand-year-old way of being in the world.
